2. Methodology
Stability scores are derived from a composite index constructed at the locality level. Indicators are grouped into thematic dimensions, typically covering security, 
services and livelihoods, social cohesion, and, in some contexts, resilience or disaster adaptation. Three anchor perception questions underpin the analysis: (i) 
perceptions of stability, (ii) future intentions (whether people feel they need to leave), and (iii) the third anchor question captures key informants' perceptions of 
how the situation in their locality has evolved over the preceding six months.
For each locality, three separate logistic regression models are fitted, one per anchor question. Each model produces a predicted probability (0 to 1) that the 
locality is perceived positively on that anchor. The final stability score is the unweighted average of these three probabilities, rescaled to a 0-100 scale. The logistic 
regression coefficients are then used to identify which indicators are most strongly associated with perceived stability, with higher-magnitude coefficients indicating 
stronger association. Thematic sub-scores are calculated separately as the mean of the indicator values within each dimension, rescaled to a comparable range.
Cluster analysis is used to group localities with similar stability profiles. The clusters are defined using the stability score and thematic dimensions, then described by 
their average scores across security, services, social cohesion and resilience where available. This helps identify whether different groups of localities are mainly 
affected by security, service, livelihood or cohesion constraints.
Data were collected across multiple rounds between 2022 and 2024, with the specific rounds varying by country as detailed in each crisis chapter. Multiple key  
informants were interviewed for each locality to support cross-checking of reported information. Results represent conditions at the time of data collection and 
may change between rounds.
4. Limitations
Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the results presented in this report. Year-to-year crisis comparisons are affected by differences in locality 
coverage, with some localities assessed in one year but not the other. Not all indicators were used in every context due to country-specific exclusions and missing 
data. With logistic regression, localities with missing indicator responses may be dropped, which can shift crisis-level averages. Coverage and access constraints mean 
that some localities, particularly in the most insecure areas, could not be assessed, which may reduce representativeness and introduce coverage bias. Finally, the 
score is perception-linked because it is anchored to sentiment questions and should be read as indicative rather than purely objective. Key informants are not 
randomly selected, and perceptions may vary by respondent profile, recent events, and local context.
Coverage scope may vary within a crisis context across years. In some cases, additional subnational areas were assessed in one year but not the other (for example, 
additional Nigerian states in the Lake Chad Basin, or Ménaka and Dosso in the Liptako-Gourma context). Where these areas appear in tables or figures, they 
should be interpreted with caution, as year-to-year comparisons are most robust for areas assessed in both rounds. Readers should refer to the data coverage 
sections of each crisis chapter for details on which areas were included in each year.
The West and Central Africa (WCA) region faces some of the world's most complex displacement crises. Across the Lake 
Chad Basin, the Liptako Gourma tri-border area, and the coastal countries of the Gulf of Guinea, millions of people are affected 
by armed conflict, intercommunal violence, climate shocks, and governance challenges, driving large-scale population movements, 
straining services, and undermining community resilience.
In response, IOM's Displacement Tracking Matrix (DTM) has deployed the Solutions and Mobility Index (SMI) to measure 
perceived stability at the locality level and provide actionable evidence for humanitarian, development, and peacebuilding actors.
This report presents a comparative analysis of SMI results for 2023-2024 across three crisis contexts:
Lake Chad Basin: Nigeria (BAY States), Cameroon (Far North), Niger (Diffa), and Chad (Lac Province); Liptako Gourma: 
Burkina Faso (Est, Sahel), Mali (Gao, Kidal, Mopti, Ségou, Timbuktu), and Niger (Dosso, Tahoua, Tillabéri); Coastal Countries: 
Benin (Alibori, Atakora), Côte d'Ivoire (Bounkani), and Ghana (North East, Upper East, Upper West).
Drawing on over 5,000 locality-level assessments, the report enables cross-crisis comparison and highlights differentiated 
priorities for each setting. While crisis-level averages provide a useful summary, they are aggregations of conditions that vary 
significantly across localities. Different areas face distinct challenges and require differentiated, context-specific support. A stable 
average can therefore coexist with significant subnational variation.

3. Partnerships
Data collection for the SMI relied on close collaboration between IOM, government counterparts, and humanitarian partners across all three crisis contexts. In 
Nigeria, the tool was implemented by IOM to support authorities and partners in understanding relative stability in locations hosting displaced people and 
returnees. In Cameroon’s Far North, implementation depended on coordination with local authorities and community-level stakeholders. In Mali, the National 
Directorate of Social Development collaborated with IOM to implement the Stability Index in 2023 and the Solutions and Mobility Index in 2024. In Niger, IOM has 
used the SMI since 2019 to support strategy design, resource prioritization, and planning in fragile areas across both the Lake Chad Basin and Liptako Gourma 
contexts. In Burkina Faso, the SMI was also used in Est and Sahel regions, while in the coastal countries, partnerships with national disaster management agencies 
and local authorities were central to implementation.

5.1 Lake Chad Basin
Crisis trend: The overall stability score increases slightly from 80 in 2023 to 81 in 2024, remaining strong overall.
Underlying stress: The security sub-score drops from 74 to 66 and social cohesion from 74 to 69, while services 
improve slightly from 58 to 60.
Main drivers: Perceptions remain shaped by security conditions and daily functioning, including the security situation, 
incident trends, daily public life, and freedom of movement.
5.2 Liptako Gourma
Crisis trend: Overall stability declines from 66 in 2023 to 64 in 2024.
What drives the decline: Security remains broadly stable at around 70–71, while the decline is driven by a drop in 
social cohesion from 71 to 64 and a sharp fall in services from 65 to 55.
Most fragile cluster identified in the 2024 cluster analysis: Very weak averages across dimensions, with stability at 34, 
security at 42, social cohesion at 44, and services at 43, reflecting compound fragility.
Main drivers: The 2024 profile emphasises insecurity exposure, including incident trends and worry about security, 
alongside basic conditions such as housing quality and primary school access.
5.3 Coastal Countries
Crisis trend: Stability decreases slightly from 78 in 2023 to 76 in 2024 (-2). Services weaken from 58 to 56 (-2), 
while resilience to natural hazards improves from 23 to 30 (+7), with security (72 to 71) and social cohesion (69 to 
70) broadly stable.
Uneven geography: Stability remains high in parts of Ghana, such as North East, while severe fragility is concentrated 
in parts of Benin.
Key hotspot: Only 9 per cent of assessed localities in Alibori were perceived as safe and 91 per cent unsafe, 
indicating acute stress.
Main drivers: The 2024 profile shifts towards insecurity and mobility constraints, with worry about security and 
freedom of movement rising in prominence, alongside access and resilience perceptions.
Key Findings Across Crisis Contexts (2023-2024)
Regional observation: When results are summarised as crisis-level average scores (the mean 
score across all assessed localities within a crisis area)*, the overall average shifts only slightly 
between 2023 and 2024. However, changes are uneven across countries and locality clusters, 
meaning a “stable” crisis-level average can still mask pockets of vulnerability.
*Note: Mean scores are calculated on a 0-100 scale across assessed localities. These crisis-level averages summarise the overall trend but do not capture all 
variation between countries, regions and localities.
What these changes mean: Small shifts in the overall stability score should not be interpreted in 
isolation. In several crisis contexts, the overall score changes only slightly, but thematic sub-scores 
show more important deterioration in security, social cohesion or services. This means 
programming should look beyond the overall average and identify which dimensions are 
weakening in each country, region or locality.

Overall Direction of Change
Based on stability measurement results derived from key informant interviews, the comparison between 
2023 and 2024 shows a small average increase in the overall stability score across assessed areas, with an 
average change of plus 1 point. Over the same period, the security sub score decreased on average by 8 
points and the social cohesion sub score decreased on average by 5 points. Access to services increased by 2 
points, with increases recorded in almost all areas. These results indicate that changes were not uniform 
across dimensions, with more negative shifts recorded for security and social cohesion than for services, while 
the overall stability score shows a modest net increase.
Areas Showing the Largest Increases and Decreases
Across assessed areas, overall stability changed only slightly, with the largest increase in Diffa (+5 points) and a smaller 
increase in Extrême-Nord (+2 points). Borno recorded a slight decrease (-1 point), while Adamawa, Lac and Yobe 
showed no change. At crisis level, the Lake Chad Basin overall stability score increased by +2 points.
However, the sub-scores show more important shifts. Security declined by -8 points overall, with the largest decreases 
in Adamawa (-15), Yobe (-13), and Lac and Borno (-11 each). Social cohesion also declined by -5 points overall, with 
the largest decrease in Diffa (-11). Services improved slightly overall (+2 points), although Adamawa recorded a small 
decline (-1).
Programmatically, these fluctuations show where stabilisation support may need more focus. Even where the overall 
stability score remains high, declines in security and social cohesion suggest that some areas may require closer 
monitoring and targeted interventions. Areas with the strongest negative shifts, particularly Adamawa, Yobe, Borno, Lac 
and Diffa, should be considered priority areas for stabilisation programming, depending on the specific constraints 
identified in each locality.

6.5 Key Indicators Shaping Stability Profiles (2023 vs 2024)
Table LCB-3 summarises the highest-ranked indicators associated with perceived stability in 2023 and 2024. 
The results point to both continuity and change in the factors shaping local perceptions of stability. Some 
dimensions remain prominent across both years, particularly everyday security, freedom of movement, and 
daily public life, suggesting that routine social functioning and the ability to move safely remain central to how 
stability is experienced. At the same time, the ranking also indicates shifts in emphasis. In 2023, security-
related indicators were especially prominent, including recent security incidents, incidents involving non-state 
armed groups, and informal curfews. In 2024, concern about security remained important, but other 
dimensions such as farmland conditions and resilience to disruption became more visible among the top-
ranked indicators. Overall, the findings suggest that while security continues to underpin perceived stability, 
livelihoods and local adaptive capacity also play an important role in shaping how communities assess their 
situation.
This section examines the indicators most strongly associated with perceived stability in 2023 and 2024, 
based on the ranked results of the logistic regression analysis. The purpose is not to suggest that all ranked 
indicators are directly comparable across years, but rather to identify which dimensions were most influential 
in each year and where continuity or change can be observed.
To support interpretation, indicators are reviewed by thematic group, namely services, safety and security, 
social cohesion, and resilience. Where the same indicator, or a closely related indicator within the same 
thematic group, appears among the top-ranked results in both 2023 and 2024, its evolution is discussed 
comparatively. For indicators that appear only in one year’s ranking, the analysis focuses on their relevance 
within that year rather than implying a direct year-on-year comparison.
This approach makes it possible to distinguish between enduring drivers of perceived stability and shifts in the 
factors most closely associated with stability across the two years.

In the Lake Chad Basin crisis context, marked by persistent insecurity, recurrent population movements, and gaps in protection and access to essential services, 
cluster analysis was used to identify groups of localities with similar multidimensional stability profiles. Rather than interpreting each locality only through its overall 
stability score, this approach helps show how different thematic dimensions combine within each profile, particularly security, social cohesion, services, and 
resilience where available.The clusters were generated using a K-Means clustering algorithm applied to the standardized thematic sub-scores. Once the clusters 
were identified, the average overall stability score and the average thematic sub-scores were calculated for each group to support interpretation. The objective 
was therefore not to rank localities by the overall stability index, but to identify broad patterns among localities that share similar characteristics across the main 
dimensions of stability.This analysis helps distinguish localities that may have similar overall stability levels but different underlying constraints. For example, some 
profiles may be mainly affected by weak access to services, while others may be more constrained by lower security or weaker social cohesion. In this way, the 
cluster analysis supports more targeted prioritisation by identifying which dimensions require the most attention within each group of localities.

6.8 Cluster Analysis
Cluster profiles in 2023
In 2023, three cluster profiles were identified across the Lake Chad Basin crisis context.
Cluster 0 shows the strongest overall profile, with high average stability (87), high security (81), high social cohesion (84), and strong services (74). 
This pattern is consistent across countries, with the highest stability in Nigeria (89) and the lowest stability within this cluster in Niger (77).
Cluster 1 is an intermediate profile, with moderately high stability (77) and security (72), while social cohesion is lower (70) and services are 
substantially weaker (43).
Cluster 2 records the lowest profile, with lower average stability (70) and security (63), alongside lower social cohesion (63) and mid-range 
services (55).
At crisis level, the 2023 averages indicate relatively high stability (80), with similar security (74) and social cohesion (74), while services remain the 
lowest scoring dimension (58). The consolidated dataset includes 2,341 assessed localities.
Cluster profiles in 2024
In 2024, three cluster profiles were also identified, but their ordering differs from 2023.
Cluster 2 presents the most favourable profile, combining high stability (87) with strong services (75), high social cohesion (78), and moderate 
security (70). This pattern is observed across countries, with stability reaching 88 in Cameroon and Nigeria within this cluster.
Cluster 0 shows moderately high stability (81), but lower security (67), social cohesion (69), and especially services (50) compared with Cluster 2.
Cluster 1 is the most fragile profile in 2024, with lower stability (69), lower security (57), lower social cohesion (50), and weak services (47).
At crisis level, the 2024 averages show stability at 81, security at 66, social cohesion at 69, and services at 60. The consolidated dataset includes 
2,043 assessed localities.
Crisis-level comparison between 2023 and 2024
Across the crisis, average stability is broadly similar between 2023 (80) and 2024 (81), but the composition of the underlying dimensions changes 
notably. Security declines from 74 to 66 and social cohesion from 74 to 69, while services improve slightly from 58 to 60. This means the modest 
increase in overall stability masks significant deterioration in security and social cohesion, partially offset by a small gain in service access. The 
number of assessed localities also decreased from 2,341 to 2,043, reflecting changes in coverage between rounds.
In 2024, the strongest profile is characterised by high stability alongside strong services and social cohesion (Cluster 2), while another large profile 
combines moderately high stability with weaker services (Cluster 0). The most fragile profile (Cluster 1) concentrates lower security and social 
cohesion, with services also weak. Overall, the cluster results indicate that the main constraints vary by profile, with some localities primarily shaped 
by service limitations and others by lower security and social cohesion.

6.9 Conclusion
Crisis context and continued relevance of the tool
Across 2023 and 2024, the Lake Chad Basin crisis remained shaped by persistent insecurity, attacks by non-state armed groups, and repeated 
population movements, including new and protracted displacement. In this context, the Stability Index and the Solutions and Mobility Index 
continued to provide a structured way to measure perceived stability in displacement-affected hosting areas and to support prioritisation linking 
humanitarian response with recovery, stabilisation, and long-term solutions across Nigeria, Cameroon, Niger, and Chad. The SMI can support 
prioritisation across humanitarian response, recovery, stabilisation and durable solutions to internal displacement, particularly in localities hosting 
IDPs, returnees and affected host communities.
Stability perceptions and intention to stay remain strong
Perceived safety stayed high across assessed areas. At crisis level, 92 per cent reported areas are safe in 2023. In 2024, safety remained high 
across all areas, including Extrême-Nord (89%), Lac (94%), Diffa (98%), and Adamawa (99%). In Nigeria, Borno remained lowest at 85 per cent 
safe in both years, while Yobe decreased from 94 per cent to 90 per cent.
Key Informant perceptions suggest that residents in most assessed localities do not need to leave. At crisis level in 2023, 95 per cent reported 
communities do not need to leave. In 2024, Key Informants in most assessed localities reported that residents did not need to leave, with 
particularly high shares in Lac, Adamawa and Yobe. Reports that residents may need to leave remained limited overall, reaching 9 per cent in 
Diffa and Borno. Compared with 2023, “may need to leave” increased slightly in Diffa (5% to 9%) and Borno (4% to 9%), while Yobe improved 
from 10 per cent to 2 per cent.
Confidence about trends is the most uneven signal
Perceptions of how the situation is evolving varied the most across areas. At crisis level in 2023, 54 per cent were more hopeful. In 2024, 
optimism ranged from Adamawa (82 per cent more hopeful) and Borno and Yobe (79 per cent each) to Diffa (42 per cent), while Lac remained 
largely pessimistic (15 per cent more hopeful).
Index results stayed stable overall, but dimensions shifted
At crisis level, overall stability remained broadly similar (80 in 2023 to 81 in 2024), but underlying dimensions changed:
• Security: 74 to 66
• Social cohesion: 74 to 69
• Services: 58 to 60
• Resilience (new in 2024): 34 on average
Nigeria maintained the highest stability in both years (86 to 85), supported by strong services and social cohesion despite a marked security 
decline. Lac showed a sharp security drop but retained relatively strong cohesion, while Diffa recorded the largest decline in social cohesion.
What drives stability shifted between 2023 and 2024
In 2023, leading drivers were mainly incident-based security indicators (recent incidents, incidents linked to non-state armed groups, informal 
curfews). In 2024, security remained prominent but shifted toward perceived concerns about security, alongside stronger contributions from 
livelihood and resilience factors (including farmland use and village resilience). Across both years, daily public life and freedom of movement 
remained consistent drivers.
Implications for prioritisation
Overall, perceived stability and intentions to stay remain high, but confidence about trends varies sharply by area, and security and social cohesion 
weakened on average despite slight improvement in services. This supports differentiated prioritisation by context, with constraints more linked 
to services and livelihoods in some areas and to security and social cohesion in others. In areas where services are the main constraint, 
prioritisation could focus on access to basic services and livelihoods. In areas where security and social cohesion are weakening, programming 
could focus on community safety, social cohesion initiatives and support to local governance. Links to country-level SMI reports should be

7.5 Key Indicators Shaping Stability Profiles (2023 vs 2024)
Table LG-3 summarises the highest-ranked indicators associated with perceived stability in 2023 and 2024. 
The results suggest both continuity and change in the dimensions shaping perceptions of stability across the 
Liptako-Gourma region. In 2023, the most influential indicators combined security and living conditions, with 
residents worried about security, connectivity situation, market situation, daily public life, and the presence of 
police or gendarmerie all appearing among the top-ranked factors. In 2024, trend in incident related activities 
becomes the highest-ranked indicator, while residents worried about security remains prominent. Other 
leading indicators point more strongly to the functioning of everyday community life and access to basic 
services, including daily public life, housing quality, and primary school situation. Only one indicator, daily public 
life, appears in both years, suggesting that the continuity of routine social functioning remains closely linked to 
perceived stability, even as the broader profile of key influences shifts over time.
This section examines the indicators most strongly associated with perceived stability in 2023 and 2024, based on the 
ranked results of the logistic regression analysis. The indicators are ranked according to their average logistic regression 
coefficients across the three anchor questions: perceived stability, intention to stay or leave, and perceived evolution of 
the situation. Higher-ranked indicators therefore represent those most strongly associated with modelled stability in 
each year. The purpose is not to suggest that all ranked indicators are directly comparable across years, but rather to 
identify which dimensions were most influential in each year and where continuity or change can be observed. To 
support interpretation, indicators are reviewed by thematic group: services, safety and security, social cohesion, and 
resilience. Where the same indicator, or a closely related indicator within the same thematic group, appears among the 
top-ranked results in both 2023 and 2024, its evolution is discussed comparatively. For indicators that appear only in 
one year’s ranking, the analysis focuses on their relevance within that year rather than implying a direct year-on-year 
comparison.
This approach helps distinguish between persistent drivers of perceived stability and shifts in the factors most closely 
associated with stability across the two years.

Security remains strained and shifts slightly toward higher concern in 2024. In 2023, 29 per cent of localities were not worried 
about security, 54 per cent somewhat worried, and 16 per cent very worried. In 2024, not worried rises to 34 per cent, but 
very worried also increases to 18 per cent. Burkina Faso shows the strongest concern in 2024, with only 17 per cent not 
worried and 26 per cent very worried, and Est stands out at 39 per cent very worried.
Incident trends affecting activities improved overall in 2024. At crisis level, 64 per cent of assessed localities reported a decrease 
in incidents affecting activities over the previous three months, compared with 12 per cent reporting an increase. Niger shows 
the strongest improvement signal, while Ségou shows a deterioration. However, the Ségou result should be interpreted with 
caution due to the very small number of assessed localities in 2024.

63 per cent in 2023 to 56 per cent in 2024, while essential-needs-only movements rise from 14 per cent to 20 per cent. This 
shift is driven mainly by Burkina Faso.

than half of shelters in good condition. School access is mixed, with 57 per cent reporting primary schools are open, but serious 
gaps remain in Burkina Faso.

In the Liptako-Gourma crisis context, marked by persistent insecurity, armed violence, ongoing population movements, and sustained constraints 
in access to essential services, cluster analysis was used to identify groups of localities with similar multidimensional stability profiles. Rather than 
interpreting localities only through their overall stability score, this approach helps show how thematic dimensions combine within each profile, 
particularly security, social cohesion, services, and resilience where available.
The clusters were generated using a K-Means clustering algorithm applied to the standardized thematic sub-scores. Once the clusters were 
identified, the average overall stability score and the average thematic sub-scores were calculated for each group to support interpretation. The 
objective was therefore not to rank localities by the overall stability index, but to identify broad patterns among localities that share similar 
characteristics across the main dimensions of stability.
7.8 Cluster Analysis
Cluster profiles in 2023
In 2023, three cluster profiles were identified across the Liptako-Gourma assessed localities.
Cluster 2 shows the strongest overall profile, combining high average stability (74), high security (80), and the highest social cohesion (82), 
alongside strong services (81). This profile is consistently strong in both Mali (73 stability, 80 security, 83 social, 81 services) and Niger (80 stability, 
82 security, 77 social, 77 services).
Cluster 0 also records relatively high stability (75) with strong security (77) and social cohesion (71), but services are notably weaker (50). This 
pattern is driven by lower services in Mali (43) and mid-range services in Niger (52).
Cluster 1 is the most fragile profile, with low stability (47) and low security (51), while social cohesion (57) and services (59) remain higher than 
stability and security.
At crisis level, the 2023 averages indicate moderate overall performance, with stability at 66, security at 70, and social cohesion at 71, while 
services average 65.
Cluster profiles in 2024
In 2024, three cluster profiles were also identified across the assessed localities, now including Burkina Faso alongside Mali and Niger.
Cluster 0 presents the most favourable profile, with high stability (78) and the highest security (82), alongside solid social cohesion (75) and 
services (69). This cluster includes localities across all three countries, with services ranging from 64 to 77.
Cluster 1 is an intermediate profile, with moderate stability (57) and relatively high security (74), but weaker social cohesion (57) and very low 
services (34). This service constraint is consistent across Burkina Faso (41), Mali (34), and Niger (28).
Cluster 2 is the most fragile profile in 2024, with very low stability (35), low security (42), low social cohesion (45), and limited services (43). This 
profile reflects compound fragility where all dimensions score poorly.
At crisis level, the 2024 averages show stability at 64, security at 71, social cohesion at 64, and services at 56.
Crisis-level comparison between 2023 and 2024
Across the crisis, average stability is broadly similar between 2023 (66) and 2024 (64), and average security is stable (70 to 71). The main changes 
are in social cohesion, which drops from 71 to 64, and services, which decline from 65 to 56. These two dimensions drive the slight overall 
stability decline. In 2024, the strongest profile (Cluster 0) combines high stability with stronger services than the crisis average, while a distinct 
intermediate profile (Cluster 1) shows that services can fall very low even when security remains relatively high. The most fragile profile in 2024 
(Cluster 2) concentrates low scores across all dimensions, particularly stability and security, and represents the localities facing the most severe 
compound constraints.

7.9 Conclusion
Crisis context and continued relevance of the tool
Across 2023 and 2024, the Liptako Gourma crisis remained driven by persistent insecurity and armed violence, continued population 
movements, and acute humanitarian needs among displaced populations and host communities. Displacement was both new and protracted, 
with many households living in precarious conditions in urban areas, with host families, or on self-settled sites. In this context, the Stability 
Index and the Solutions and Mobility Index continued to provide a structured way to measure perceived stability in displacement-affected 
areas and to support prioritisation linking humanitarian response with recovery, stabilisation, and longer-term solutions across Mali, Niger, and 
Burkina Faso.
Perceptions of security and day-to-day functioning remain uneven
Perceptions of security remained strained and slightly more polarised in 2024. At crisis level, the share not worried increased from 29 per 
cent (2023) to 34 per cent (2024), while the share very worried also increased from 16 per cent to 18 per cent. In 2024, concern was most 
concentrated in Burkina Faso, where only 17 per cent of localities were not worried and 26 per cent were very worried. Est stood out with 
39 per cent very worried and only 4 per cent not worried. Mali remained highly uneven, with Kidal at 0 per cent very worried while Ségou 
reached 75 per cent very worried, confirming that stress is concentrated in specific settings rather than evenly distributed across the crisis.
Key message
• Security perceptions are not uniform and show strong subnational contrasts, especially within Mali and Burkina Faso.
Incident trends improve in 2024, but local hotspots persist
In 2024, incidents affecting activities were more often reported as decreasing. At crisis level, 64 per cent reported decreases in the past three 
months, compared with 12 per cent reporting increases. Niger showed the strongest improvement signal. However, Mali (Ségou) remained a 
clear outlier, with 100 per cent reporting increases, highlighting the need for targeted attention to hotspots even when overall trends 
improve.
Index results shifted slightly downward, driven by cohesion and services
At crisis level, overall stability decreased from 66 (2023) to 64 (2024). Security remained broadly stable (70 to 71). The main declines were:
• Social cohesion: 71 to 64
• Services: 65 to 56
This indicates a deterioration in perceived social conditions and service access that drove the slight decline in overall stability despite stable 
security.
Service and living condition constraints are prominent in 2024
Living conditions emerged as a key pressure point in 2024:
• Housing quality: 65 per cent of localities reported fewer than 50 per cent of shelters in good condition.
• Education access: At crisis level, 57 per cent reported primary schools are open, but major gaps persisted, especially in Burkina Faso:
• Burkina Faso: 14 per cent reported schools open; 34 per cent reported closed with no accessible nearby option (rising to 57 
per cent in Est).
• Mali: extremes remained pronounced, with Ségou at 100 per cent of localities with the school closed with no accessible 
nearby school, while Kidal reported 100 per cent of localities with an open school.
Implications for prioritization
Across 2023 and 2024, average stability declined slightly, driven mainly by weaker social cohesion and services, while security remained stable 
at crisis level. This supports differentiated prioritisation:
• In some settings, constraints are most linked to service access and living conditions (shelter quality, schooling).
• In others, security concern and day-to-day functioning remain the primary stressors.
Overall, the results point to the need for integrated interventions that reduce protection risks, restore essential services, and strengthen social 
cohesion and resilience in displacement-affected communities.

8.4 Geographic Distribution of Stability Scores
Based on stability measurement results derived from key informant interviews, the comparison between 
2023 and 2024 across the coastal countries assessed areas suggests a broadly stable overall picture. On 
average, the stability score shows no change (0) across assessed areas. Dimension-level changes are also 
limited: security (0) and social cohesion (0) remain unchanged on average, while services show a small decline 
(-1). By contrast, resilience increases (+5) on average, indicating that the main positive movement between 
cycles is concentrated in the resilience component rather than in perceived stability, security, or social 
cohesion.
Across areas, changes are uneven and concentrated in a small number of locations. The largest increase in 
overall stability is recorded in Benin (Alibori) at +11, while the largest decreases are observed in Ghana

8.5 Key Indicators Shaping Stability Profiles (2023 vs 2024)
Table CC-3 summarises the top indicators for 2023 and 2024, highlighting both continuity and change. In 2023, the 
leading signals were anchored in community resilience and security perceptions, with proximity of community disaster

toward security perceptions and mobility, with residents worried about security ranked first and residents’ freedom of 
movement ranked second, while resilience remains prominent. A security actor presence indicator— NSAG presence—
emerges among the top five in 2024, suggesting a stronger role of armed-group dynamics. Four indicators— access to 
population, residents’ freedom of movement, evolution of village resilience and residents worried about security—
remain consistent across both years, indicating that access constraints and the ability to move safely continue to be 
closely linked to perceived stability.
This section examines the indicators most strongly associated with perceived stability in 2023 and 2024, based on the 
ranked results of the logistic regression analysis. The indicators are ranked according to their average logistic regression 
coefficients across the three anchor questions: perceived stability, intention to stay or leave, and perceived evolution of 
the situation. Higher-ranked indicators therefore represent those most strongly associated with modelled stability in 
each year. The purpose is not to suggest that all ranked indicators are directly comparable across years, but rather to 
identify which dimensions were most influential in each year and where continuity or change can be observed. To 
support interpretation, indicators are reviewed by thematic group: services, safety and security, social cohesion, and 
resilience. Where the same indicator, or a closely related indicator within the same thematic group, appears among the 
top-ranked results in both 2023 and 2024, its evolution is discussed comparatively. For indicators that appear only in 
one year’s ranking, the analysis focuses on their relevance within that year rather than implying a direct year-on-year 
comparison. This approach helps distinguish between persistent drivers of perceived stability and shifts in the factors 
most closely associated with stability across the two years.

clusters were identified, the average overall stability score and the average thematic sub-scores were calculated for each group to

among localities that share similar characteristics across the main dimensions of stability.

8.9 Conclusion
Crisis context and continued relevance of the tool
Across 2023 and 2024, the Coastal countries context in West Africa was shaped by two reinforcing pressures: climate and environmental 
shocks affecting livelihoods and straining natural resources, and the spillover from the Central Sahel crisis increasing insecurity and population 
movements in northern border areas. In this setting, the Solutions and Mobility Index supported measurement of perceived stability, 
identification of fragility pockets, and prioritisation across assessed areas of Benin, Côte d’Ivoire, and Ghana.
• Main pressures: climate shocks and Sahel spillover
• Use of the tool: measure stability and target prioritization
Perceptions of stability and future intentions vary sharply by country
In 2024, perceived stability was high in Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire, but lower in Benin. Ghana recorded very high “safe” shares, while Côte 
d’Ivoire (Bounkani) remained predominantly positive. Benin is the main pressure point, especially Alibori (very low “safe”, high “unsafe”), with 
Atakora mixed but slightly negative. Intentions to remain were very strong in Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire, whereas Benin was more divided, 
notably in Alibori where “may need to leave” is substantial.
• Benin: most negative signals, especially Alibori
• Ghana and Côte d’Ivoire: strong perceived stability and intentions to stay
Crisis level changes are limited, but resilience improves
Between 2023 and 2024, crisis-level averages were broadly stable: stability unchanged, security and social cohesion unchanged, and services 
slightly lower, while resilience increased. Changes were uneven, including a strong increase in Benin (Alibori) and declines in Ghana (North East) 
and Côte d’Ivoire (Bounkani). Côte d’Ivoire showed the most notable deterioration at country level due to weaker services, despite improved 
resilience.
• Main positive change: resilience up
• Main negative change: services down, notably in Côte d’Ivoire
Priority indicators indicate a shift toward security and mobility in 2024
In 2024, priority indicators placed more weight on security perceptions and freedom of movement, while resilience remained prominent and 
NSAG presence appeared among top indicators. Access and mobility remained key in both years.
• 2024 signal: stronger emphasis on security and movement
Key perception indicators show stable aggregates but concentrated stress
Crisis-level aggregates are broadly stable, but stress remains concentrated. Benin consistently shows higher security concern, more movement 
restrictions, higher resilience degradation, and sharper access inequality, particularly in Alibori, while Ghana reports lower concern and near 
universal absence of movement restrictions.
• Stress concentration: Benin, especially Alibori
Cluster patterns confirm differentiated profiles and rising resilience in 2024
Cluster analysis confirms differing locality profiles. The strongest profile (stability, 88) combines high social cohesion, services, and resilience, with 
Côte d'Ivoire standing out. The most fragile profile (stability, 44) remains largely unchanged across years. Resilience is the dimension showing 
the most consistent improvement, rising from 23 to 30 at crisis level, even as services decline slightly from 58 to 56.
Implications for prioritization
Crisis-level averages remain broadly stable, but stress is concentrated in Benin, particularly Alibori, where security concern, mobility restrictions, 
and access inequality coincide. Côte d'Ivoire shows deterioration driven by declining services despite strong resilience gains. Ghana remains 
comparatively stable. The results support differentiated prioritisation: services and livelihoods where service decline drives fragility, protection 
and mobility where insecurity is most salient, and resilience interventions addressing climate pressures and resource-related tensions.

Conclusion across all crises
Across 2023 and 2024, the three crisis contexts show that perceived stability can remain broadly steady at crisis level while stress 
concentrates in specific countries and subnational areas. The Stability Index and the Solutions and Mobility Index remain useful 
because they capture these differences and help translate them into prioritisation across security, services, social cohesion, and 
resilience.
Main regional pattern
• Lake Chad Basin and Liptako Gourma are primarily shaped by insecurity and armed violence, with repeated displacement and 
persistent service gaps. This produces uneven stability profiles within the same crisis area.
• Coastal countries are shaped by climate and environmental pressures combined with spillover from the Central Sahel crisis, 
which increases pressure on livelihoods, natural resources, and basic services in northern hosting areas. Outcomes are highly 
uneven across countries.
Where the problems are most concentrated
• Benin is the clearest pressure point in the Coastal countries context.
• In Alibori, perceived stability is very low in 2024 at 9 per cent safe and 91 per cent unsafe, and intentions are more 
divided, with 45 per cent may need to leave. This aligns with high stress across multiple dimensions, including mobility 
constraints and access inequality.
• Côte d’Ivoire shows deterioration driven by services, despite improved resilience.
• In Bounkani, the year to year change indicates weakening services, even while resilience indicators improve, suggesting 
that coping capacity can increase without immediate improvement in service delivery.
• Nigeria remains the main security stress area in the Lake Chad Basin narrative.
• The country level pattern continues to be driven by the north east, with Borno repeatedly standing out as the key 
insecurity hotspot relative to other assessed areas.
• Liptako Gourma continues to show concentrated fragility.
• The 2024 cluster structure includes a profile where security remains relatively high but services fall very low, 
confirming that service access is a defining constraint in parts of the crisis.
What the numbers say across crises
• Lake Chad Basin: stability stays high and changes little at crisis level, from 80 in 2023 to 81 in 2024, but the balance shifts 
underneath it, with security and social cohesion weakening and services improving slightly.
• Liptako Gourma: stability declines slightly, from 66 to 64, driven mainly by drops in social cohesion and services, not by security.
• Coastal countries: overall change is limited, with stability moving from 78 to 76, services declining slightly, and resilience 
increasing from 23 to 30, indicating improved perceived resilience alongside marginally weaker services.
How to use this for prioritisation
• Target services and livelihoods where service decline is driving fragility.
• This is most relevant where scores show deterioration in services and where cluster profiles indicate service 
constrained localities.
• Target protection and mobility where insecurity and movement restrictions coincide.
• This is most relevant where security concern is high and freedom of movement is restricted, as seen in specific settings 
rather than uniformly across crises.
• Use resilience programming to address climate pressures and resource related tensions.
• This is most relevant in the Coastal countries context, where resilience improves at crisis level but access and service 
pressures remain, and where tensions around natural resources can affect social cohesion.
Overall, the three crises point to a common operational message: crisis level averages can appear stable, but the drivers of fragility 
differ by crisis and concentrate in specific locations, requiring differentiated, locality specific prioritisation.

ANNEXES 
1.  Locality selection
The index is built as a composite measure intended to link 
stability analysis with programming priorities, including in a 
Humanitarian Development Peace Nexus logic. The data 
collection tool captures a set of indicators describing conditions 
within each locality and their potential contribution to stability. 
Indicators are organised into themes and thematic sub-scores 
to support operational interpretation by dimension. Depending 
on the report and country, these themes include safety and 
security, access to basic services and or livelihoods, social 
cohesion, and in some contexts resilience or adaptation 
capacity related to shocks and disasters. The approach also 
relies on anchor perception questions, commonly three, 
covering sense of stability, community future intentions, and 
perceived trends in the situation. In some contexts, an 
additional anchor question related to adaptation capacity may 
be included. Before estimation, response categories for 
indicators are ordered from more favourable to less favourable 
conditions and then normalised to ensure comparability and 
model readiness.
3.  Logistic regression and score production
4.  Limitations and interpretation notes
Locality selection aimed to be as comprehensive as possible 
within displacement and or return affected areas, to ensure a 
sufficiently strong evidence base for crisis level analysis. An initial 
list of localities was compiled using available displacement and 
return information, complemented by other existing reference 
sources where relevant (for example administrative lists, census 
references, or other established registries).The objective was to 
cover a large enough number of localities to support meaningful 
statistical analysis and allow internal comparisons across areas. A 
“locality” corresponds to the lowest administrative unit available 
in each country context, and is typically represented through a 
formal or informal local authority structure. For the aggregated 
analysis used in this work, datasets were consolidated at the 
crisis level and analysed separately by year, meaning that 2023 
and 2024 were treated as distinct analytical snapshots. As a 
result, some localities present in one year may not be present in 
the other, reflecting differences in coverage, accessibility, or 
operational prioritisation rather than only changes in conditions.
2.   Index design and calculation
Logistic regression is used to examine the relationship between 
the stability indicators (independent variables) and the anchor 
perception responses (dependent variable, defined as binary 
according to the operational specification used in each report). 
This method estimates, for each locality, a probability associated 
with stability as captured by the anchor framework. Model 
output is produced on a 0 to 1 probability scale and then 
rescaled to a 0 to 100 score to facilitate interpretation and 
comparison across localities. Where multiple anchor questions 
are used, separate models are run for each anchor question, 
and the resulting locality scores are then combined, for example 
by averaging, to produce a final stability score for each locality. 
In addition to the overall stability score, thematic sub-scores are 
generated using the indicators belonging to each theme. These 
sub-scores help identify which dimensions are most closely 
associated with higher or lower stability and can support more 
targeted prioritisation.
Several limitations should be considered when interpreting 
results.
- Coverage and access constraints: Some localities may not be 
assessed due to insecurity, access restrictions, or logistical 
constraints. This can reduce representativeness in the most 
difficult areas and may introduce coverage bias.
- Year to year comparability: Because the crisis level datasets 
were aggregated and analysed separately for 2023 and 2024, 
the set of localities included may differ across years. Some 
observed changes can therefore reflect shifts in coverage as well 
as genuine changes in conditions.
- Indicators not used in scoring: Not all collected indicators are 
necessarily included in the final score. Exclusions may occur to 
maintain consistency across rounds, because an indicator is not 
comparable across contexts, or because data availability is 
insufficient.
- Missing data and dropped observations: In logistic regression, 
localities with missing values on required indicators may be 
excluded from model estimation. This can affect aggregated 
means and reduce comparability across areas.
- Perception based measurement: The index is anchored in key 
informant perceptions. It provides an indicative, operationally 
useful measure of perceived stability, but it is not a fully 
objective measurement of a complex concept. Key informants 
are not randomly selected, and perceptions may vary by 
respondent profile, recent events, and local positioning.
