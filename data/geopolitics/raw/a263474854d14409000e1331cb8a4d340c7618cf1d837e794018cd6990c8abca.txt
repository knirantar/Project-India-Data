Context 
South Sudan is facing one of the most severe food insecurity crises 
globally. According to the latest Integrated Food Security Phase 
Classification (IPC) analysis, 55 percent of the population is experiencing 
high levels of acute food insecurity (IPC Phase 3 or above), including 
73 000 people in Catastrophe (IPC Phase 5).1 Additionally, four counties – 
Akobo and Nyirol in Jonglei State and Luakpiny/Nasir and Ulang in 
Upper Nile State – face a risk of Famine under a probable worst-case 
scenario, which has a realistic likelihood of occurring.
The crisis is driven by a combination of recurrent shocks and structural 
vulnerabilities. Four consecutive years of severe flooding have destroyed 
crops, livestock and critical infrastructure, significantly undermining 
agricultural production and livelihoods. Concurrently, protracted 
conflict and insecurity continue to restrict humanitarian access, 
displace communities and disrupt local economies.
Since the start of the conflict in the Sudan in April 2023, South Sudan 
has experienced a massive influx of over 700 000 returnees and 
refugees.2 More than 70 percent have settled in already vulnerable 
areas where FAO is providing support, placing additional strain on 
limited resources and services. This is compounded by a deteriorating 
economic situation characterized by currency depreciation, rising 
food prices and disrupted markets, further eroding households’ 
purchasing power.
These shocks are occurring in a context of widespread structural 
poverty and limited livelihood diversification, leaving households with 
minimal coping capacity and highly vulnerable to further deterioration. 
The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) is 
scaling up its response to enable vulnerable families to produce and 
access nutritious food, protect their livelihoods and prevent further 
hunger and malnutrition. Under the  South Sudan: Emergency and 
Resilience Plan, 2026–2028, FAO requires USD 255 million to support 
5.25 million people. Of this amount, an initial USD 10 million is urgently 
needed to deliver time-critical, life-saving agricultural interventions to 
1.8 million people.

Activities implemented and funding received so far
FAO, in collaboration with its local partners, is delivering emergency 
livelihood support to the most vulnerable households across the 
country. Under the current funding envelope of approximately 
USD 3.5 million – comprising USD 1.62 million from the South Sudan 
Humanitarian Fund, USD 140 000 from the Government of Japan, 
and approximately USD 1.7 million from savings and carry-over from 
previous projects – FAO is supporting an estimated 103 000 households 
(approximately 564 000 people) countrywide. A further 9 420 households 
in Unity State are being reached thanks to funding from the Governments 
of Norway and the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland.
The Organization’s current response spans 25 counties across eight states 
(Central Equatoria, Eastern Equatoria, Jonglei, Northern Bahr el Ghazal, 
Upper Nile, Unity, Warrap and Western Bahr el Ghazal), with a 
strategic focus on the locations with the highest levels of vulnerability. 
FAO is operational in two of the six counties where communities are 
experiencing Catastrophe (IPC Phase 5) (Luakpiny/Nasir and Ulang), 
supporting 13 333 households in the most critical areas. 
Assistance is currently being delivered to the 103 000 households through 
the following interventions:
• Seed fairs (cash/voucher modality): Approximately 
8 000 households are receiving support through seed fairs, enabling 
them to select their preferred seeds from local markets while 
strengthening local food systems.
• Direct input packages: The remaining 95 000 households are 
receiving livelihood support packages comprising crop seeds 
(groundnut, maize and sorghum), vegetable seeds (amaranth, collard, 
okra, onion and tomato), one hand tool per household, and fishing 
kits to diversify their food sources.
• Livestock support: Current interventions also include the vaccination 
and treatment of approximately 200 000 animals, safeguarding the 
livestock assets of agro/pastoral households.
Scaling up to reach 1.8 million people in need
FAO aims to scale up its emergency livelihood response to reach 
vulnerable people in the most food-insecure areas during the critical 
2026 agricultural season. Additional resources would enable FAO to more 
than triple its current reach, providing a comprehensive, multi-sector 
package of crop, fisheries and livestock support to communities at the 
highest risk of Famine.

The cost of inaction 
Without immediate and scaled-up support, the humanitarian 
consequences will be severe, irreversible within the season, and likely to 
drive a further deterioration in IPC classification across multiple counties. 
FAO warns of the following cascading impacts if funding is not mobilized 
before the June 2026 planting deadline: 
1. Expansion of areas facing Catastrophe (IPC Phase 5) and risk of Famine 
Catastrophe (IPC Phase 5) is currently documented in six counties across 
Upper Nile and Jonglei states, affecting over 73 000 people. However, the 
structural drivers – persistent flooding, conflict, displacement and market 
failures – remain unaddressed across a much wider geographic area. 
Without emergency livelihood interventions during the current planting 
season, the following projections apply:
• Counties currently in Emergency (IPC Phase 4), including Rubkona in 
Unity, Malakal in Upper Nile, and Canal/Pigi, Duk, Ayod and Pibor in 
Jonglei, face a high risk of deteriorating into Catastrophe (IPC Phase 5) 
conditions during the June–September 2026 lean season.
• The 2.49 million people currently in Emergency (IPC Phase 4) are 
one missed harvest away from catastrophe. A failed main season will 
eliminate their primary domestic food source and erode any residual 
coping capacity.
• An estimated additional 500 000–700 000 people could slide into 
Catastrophe (IPC Phase 5) by the second half of 2026 if no emergency 
agricultural support is provided.

3. Multiplier effects on agrifood systems and markets 
• Failure to support the current planting season will drive increased 
dependence on food assistance programmes, placing additional 
pressure on already-strained World Food Programme and 
non-governmental organization pipelines.
• Livestock losses from preventable disease outbreaks are irreversible 
within a season and take years to rebuild, permanently reducing 
community resilience and productive capacity.
• Market disruption resulting from livelihood collapse will further inflate 
local food prices, disproportionately affecting the urban poor and 
displaced populations in Juba, Malakal and Renk – cities already 
hosting large IPC 3+ populations.
4. Returnee and refugee pressure
Since April 2023, large numbers of returnees and refugees have entered 
South Sudan from the Sudan, with most settling in counties covered by 
current and proposed FAO interventions, particularly in Renk (Upper Nile 
State), where nearly 377 000 people are already in IPC Phase 3+. This 
sustained influx places extraordinary pressure on host community 
agrifood systems. Without scaled-up support, host communities will 
exhaust their remaining coping mechanisms, triggering inter-community 
tensions and leading to further displacement.
