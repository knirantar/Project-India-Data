ESCALATION IN THE MIDDLE EAST AND BEYOND — MOBILITY REPORT
Data collection period: 12 - 18 May 2026 | Publication date: 21 May 2026
OVERVIEW
The escalation of hostilities across the Middle East and beyond has triggered a humanitarian crisis spanning 
multiple regions in an already fragile context, with significant impact to population mobility. National social 
systems and host community safety networks are under considerable strain as protection needs continue to rise. 
The temporary cessation of hostilities remains fragile and subject to change, requiring continued monitoring and 
operational readiness while the humanitarian consequences of earlier hostilities continue to affect populations 
across multiple regions.
In the Islamic Republic of Iran, widespread destruction of more than 149,000 civilian infrastructures and 
essential services,1 including hospitals, schools and emergency facilities, continues to reduce basic living conditions. 
As of 29 April 2026, an estimated 400,000 people have seen their homes directly affected by housing 
damages. Mobility patterns are being shaped by housing damage, livelihood loss, and service disruptions, leading 
to temporary internal relocation, reliance on family networks, and increased labour related movement. While 
no large scale cross border displacement has been observed, the cumulative pressures are contributing to 
heightened interest in outward migration among vulnerable groups, including youth, migrants, and low income 
urban households. Overall, population movements remain localised, temporary, and economically driven, 
with no signs of mass displacement; however, ongoing strain on housing, livelihoods, and basic services is 
likely to sustain elevated mobility levels in the near- and medium-term. 
In Lebanon, despite the announcement of a new 45-day ceasefire on 15 May, hostilities persist. As security 
conditions worsens, displacement remains severe, with over one million internally displaced persons (IDPs) 
reported,2 including 129,721 IDPs accommodated across 632 collective sites3 though a majority remain 
outside formal sites. Geographic distribution of displacement is uneven – areas such as Beirut, Mount Lebanon 
and North Lebanon are experiencing mounting pressure from successive waves of displacement, while returns 
remain limited due to insecurity, damaged housing, lack of services, and risks associated with unexploded ordnance. 
Collective sites and host communities face sustained pressure as some previously displaced families return to 
shelters after unsuccessful attempts to return home. Cross-border flows into the Syrian Arab Republic are 
continuing, with a total of 333,856 movements recorded as of 18 May 2026,4 placing additional pressure on 
an already overstretched humanitarian system.
IOM’s Displacement Tracking Matrix (DTM) is on the ground, actively tracking displacement and cross-border 
mobility while scaling operations in coordination with local authorities and partners to better understand how 
the crisis is impacting human mobility in the region.

IMPACT ON NEIGHBOURING COUNTRIES  
OF THE ISLAMIC REPUBLIC OF IRAN
The conflict is affecting mobility along shared borders between the Islamic Republic of Iran and its neighbouring 
countries, driving reactive movements and precautionary travel within and across borders. 
Since the start of the crisis, flows from the Islamic Republic of Iran to neighbouring countries have 
fluctuated over time, varying significantly between countries. Afghanistan receives the highest number of 
movements with substantial dips and spikes while Pakistan and Iraq see much smaller and more occasional 
movements. Overall, cross-border flows remain moderate and variable rather than steadily increasing.
AFGHANISTAN 
Between 01 March and 18 May 2026, a total of 358,496 individuals entered Afghanistan from the Islamic 
Republic of Iran, corresponding to a daily average of 4,538 movements, while 141,023 individuals departed 
Afghanistan to the Islamic Republic of Iran over the same reporting period (1,785 daily average).
During the reporting period of 12-18 May 2026, cross-border movements along the Afghanistan–Islamic 
Republic of Iran border at Milak, Islam Qala, and Abu Nasr Farahi border crossing points (BCPs) remained 
stable, with all three BCPs fully operational and no disruptions reported. 
Both inflows from the Islamic Republic of Iran to Afghanistan and outflows from Afghanistan to the Islamic 
Republic of Iran continued at a consistent level, in line with the trend observed during the previous week.
Inflows continued to be shaped by a mix of forced and voluntary returns with forced returns outweighing 
spontaneous returns. Reported drivers included police harassment, limited livelihood opportunities, and 
rising living costs in the Islamic Republic of Iran. This is particularly notable among inflows recorded at Malik 
BCP, with a higher volume of voluntary returns by Afghan passport holders with Iranian visas returning. This 
is reportedly due to unpaid wages, limited employment opportunities, approaching Eid celebration in the 
Islamic Republic of Iran.
Analysis of Flow Monitoring Survey (FMS) found that from 12 to 18 May 2026, outflows of Iranian nationals 
were predominantly recorded via Islam Qala–Taybad, with intended destinations concentrated in Sistan and 
Baluchestan (approximately 35%), Razavi Khorasan (23%), and T ehran (16%). The main drivers reported for 
leaving the previous country prior to transiting Afghanistan included family reunification in the Islamic Republic 
of Iran (around 40%), security concerns (34%), and visa expiry or non-renewal (28%). Nearly all respondents 
(approximately 99%) cited family, relatives, or social networks in the Islamic Republic of Iran as the primary 
reason for selecting their destination. 
In contrast, inflows of Iranian nationals from the Islamic Republic of Iran into Afghanistan were overwhelmingly 
recorded via Islam Qala–Taybad (approximately 96%), with final destinations mainly in Herat (48%), followed 
by Kabul (13%) and Balkh (9%). These movements were predominantly individual-based (approximately 89% 
traveling alone) and short-term, with 83% intending to stay less than one month. Reported motivations were 
largely economic or temporary in nature. Meanwhile, return movements of Afghan nationals continue to 
reflect ongoing economic hardship and protection pressures within the Islamic Republic of Iran, reinforcing 
sustained mixed and circular mobility dynamics across the border.

PAKISTAN
Between 01 March and 18 May 2026, a total of 22,673 
individuals crossed from the Islamic Republic of Iran 
into Pakistan, representing a daily average of 287 flows. 
Movements occurred primarily through the Taftan–Mirjaveh 
crossing (19,222 individuals) and the Gabd–Kumb–Rimdan 
crossing 3,451 individuals).8
Cross-border movement from the Islamic Republic of Iran to 
Pakistan through the Gabd and Taftan BCPs remains restricted. 
Currently, only students, drivers, pilgrims, and tourists with 
valid passports and visas are permitted entry. 
Many travellers are using Pakistan solely as a transit route 
due to the closure of Iranian airports amid the conflict, 
before continuing to onward destinations.  Those traveling 
irregularly to the Islamic Republic of Iran are detained by Iranian 
authorities and handed over to Pakistan’s Federal Investigation 
Agency (FIA) for processing.
Border authorities reported no influx of Iranian nationals 
other than those with valid passports and visas in Pakistan. 
The majority of Iranian arrivals subsequently returned to 
the Islamic Republic of Iran, primarily as their visits were 
trade-related. 
Outbound movement from Pakistan to the Islamic Republic 
of Iran continues on a limited basis, restricted to Pakistani 
students and drivers. Although many students initially returned 
to Pakistan at the onset of the conflict, some are now traveling 
back to the Islamic Republic of Iran to resume studies. 
As of 18 May 2026, a total of 68 Third Country Nationals 
(TCNs) were recorded in Pakistan. According to authorities, 
TCNs entering through the Taftan and Gabd–Rimdan borders 
were mostly tourists or businessmen. Many had been stranded 
in the Islamic Republic of Iran and, due to the unavailability of 
flights, transited through Pakistan before returning to their 
countries of origin.

IRAQ
CROSS-BORDER MOVEMENT
While no new movement data was received during the current reporting period (12 -18 May 2026), 
the most recent official figures from the Iraqi Border Point Commission, covering the period between 
01 and 30 April 2026, recorded a total of 271,751 individuals entering Iraq from the Islamic Republic 
of Iran, while 270,142 departed, resulting in a net incoming movements of 1,609 individuals.
As of 18 May 2026, border conditions remained broadly stable compared with the previous reporting 
period (week ending 11 May 2026), with all crossing points open and no significant restrictions observed. 
Despite the resumption of regular operations, Key Informants (KIs) reported that travel volumes have 
not yet returned to pre-escalation levels. Flows during this reporting week largely mirrored those of 
the previous week, with notable upticks recorded at Hajj Omran and Zurbatiyah. Hajj Omran BCP has 
reportedly nearly approached near-full operating conditions: Students are returning to universities in 
the Islamic Republic of Iran, medical travel is picking up, and commercial activity is regaining momentum.
No BCPs reported emergency response or security incidents during the reporting period. 
DTM FLOW MONITORING ACTIVITIES AT KEY BORDER CROSSING POINTS (BCPs) 
Between 12 and 18 May 2026, Flow Monitoring access remained restricted to Bashmagh, where 
DTM teams recorded 2,072 incoming and 2,084 outgoing movements, a 47 per cent rise in incoming 
movements and a 12 per cent drop in outgoing movements compared with the previous reporting week.
Iranian nationals dominated both directions, accounting for 74 per cent of incoming movements 
and 73 per cent of outgoing movements, with no movements of other Third Country Nationals 
(TCNs) recorded through the crossing.
Incoming movements of Iranian nationals were driven primarily by family visits (43%), employment (33%), 
and tourism (12%), with no movements reported as conflict or insecurity-related during the period. 
Outgoing movements largely reflected returns to the Islamic Republic of Iran following long-term stays 
of three months or more (66%) or short-term stays of less than three months (30%).

ARMENIA
No new data on movements was received during this reporting period (12-18 May 2026). 
Between 28 February and 10 March 2026, a total of 4,930 individuals entered Armenia through the 
Agarak BCP, including 4,381 Iranian nationals. The main nationalities of TCNs included the Russian 
Federation (72), the People’s Republic of China (61), Kazakhstan (43), and Germany (38). As of 
01 April, 1,262 Iranian nationals were reported to have returned to the Islamic Republic of Iran via 
Armenia from the United Arab Emirates. 
Overall, flows of TCNs follows regular mobility patterns, with no sustained abnormal increases 
observed.
AZERBAIJAN
No new data on movements was received during this reporting period (05 -11 May 2026). 
Between 28 February and 29 April 2026, a total of 3,564 individuals crossed from the Islamic Republic 
of Iran into Azerbaijan via the Astara border crossing, including 622 Azerbaijani nationals. Other top 
nationalities included People’s Republic of China (742), the Russian Federation (397), India (294) and 
Bangladesh (198). 
Overall, flows at the border between Azerbaijan and the Islamic Republic of Iran remain stable, 
with no indications of large-scale or mass crossings.

LEBANON

No new data on cross-border movements was received during this reporting period (12 - 18

Between 02 March and 20 April 2026, there were 326,992 individuals crossing from the Syrian

INTERNAL DISPLACEMENT
Since 02 March 2026, hostilities across Lebanon have continued to intensify, prompting population 
movements in response to growing insecurity, widespread damage to infrastructure, and overstretched 
welfare services. 
The humanitarian situation is deteriorating rapidly, triggering large scale and repeated displacement, 
as well as onward movements with little notice.
On 15 May, a new 45-day ceasefire extension was announced; however, hostilities persisted, 
with continued strikes reported across southern Lebanon governorates. Following the 
deterioration in security conditions, displacement remain high, with over one million 
internally displaced persons (IDPs) reported, 13 including 129,721 IDPs accommodated 
across 632 collective sites though a majority remain outside formal sites. 12  
This represents a 7 per cent increase compared to the previous week, when 121,701 IDPs 
were recorded in collective sites. Geographic distribution of displacement is uneven with areas 
such as Beirut, Mount Lebanon and North Lebanon are experiencing mounting pressure from 
successive waves of displacement,, while returns remain limited due to insecurity, damaged housing, 
lack of services, and risks associated with unexploded ordnance.14

SYRIAN ARAB REPUBLIC
As of 18 May, a total of 333,856 movements had been 
recorded along the four points of entry (PoEs) at the 
Syrian- Lebanese border where DTM Syria tracks 
population movements into the Syrian Arab Republic. 
Most movements recorded involved Syrian nationals 
(96%), with Lebanese nationals accounting for the 
remaining 4 per cent.
In addition to cross-border movements into the Syrian 
Arab Republic, DTM Syria implemented Emergency 
Mobility Tracking (EMT) to capture arrivals into the 
country. Between 02 March and 18 May 2026, DTM 
Syria recorded 122,083 individuals arriving across 
1,230 locations in all 14 governorates in the Syria Arab 
Republic. Those recorded at arrival locations were 
predominantly Syrian nationals (99%), with Lebanese 
nationals accounting for less than 1 per cent.
Between the 12 – 18 May 2026, Key informants (KIs) 
reported that arriving returnees continue to face multiple 
reintegration challenges. Access to safe housing remains 
a primary concern, with many residing in damaged or 
partially destroyed homes, unfinished buildings, temporary 
shelters, or low-quality rented accommodation. 
Children’s educational integration continues to pose 
challenges, especially for those who have already been 
enrolled in Lebanese curriculum, resulting in difficulty 
adapting to the Syrian education system. 
Additionally, livelihoods remain precarious and 
overwhelmingly informal, leaving returnees largely 
dependent on seasonal agricultural work and low-paid 
daily labour, and few find employment commensurate 
with their prior experiences and skills. A saturated labour 
market, compounded by weak demands for workers 
has driven wages down and heightened exposure to 
exploitative employment practices. 
Most arrivals were adult men (31%), followed by adult 
women (25%), girls (22%), and boys (21%).15 A distribution 
that likely reflects employment-related drivers, including 
both access to livelihoods and job losses. Among the 
arrivals currently employed (44% of locations), the majority 
relied on daily wage labour (79%), followed by salaried 
work (39%) and agriculture or livestock activities (39%). 
Notably, most KIs considered available income to be 
inadequate, with 52 per cent reporting it was not sufficient 
at all to meet basic needs and 46 per cent indicating it 
was only partially sufficient. 
Such insufficient, unstable, and short-term income sources 
may push individuals to adopt negative coping strategies 
to meet basic needs, while also contributing to risks of 
secondary displacement. The continued reliance of some 
employed arrivals on remittances (18%) and humanitarian 
assistance (4%) further underscores that income alone is 
not sufficient to achieve financial stability.

METHODOLOGY
This analysis is based on information compiled weekly from IOM’s 
country offices. DTM monitors population mobility and displacement 
dynamics across countries in Afghanistan, Armenia, Azerbaijan, Iraq, 
Lebanon, Pakistan, the Republic of Türkiye, Turkmenistan, and the 
Syrian Arab Republic.
Data is drawn from primary DTM flow monitoring and mobility tracking 
activities, alongside secondary sources, such as government data, with all 
secondary information validated by our field teams. This includes data on 
cross-border movements, internal displacement, and qualitative insights 
on emerging mobility trends and humanitarian needs. While efforts 
are made to align reporting to the same reference week, collection 
periods may vary across countries.
