Since 28 February, Insecurity Insight has monitored how the conflict between Iran, 
Israel, Lebanon and the United States has affected the delivery of aid, education, 
food and water, and health care across Iran, Israel, Lebanon, and the Greater Middle 
East region. Insecurity Insight’s open source monitoring recorded a total of 302 incidents 
where conflict-related violence impacted aid, education, health, food and water systems 
in ten countries: Azerbaijan, Bahrain, Iran, Iraq, Israel, Kuwait, Lebanon, the occupied 
Palestinian territory (oPt), Syria and the United Arab Emirates (UAE). Of these incidents, 
97% (293) involved explosive weapons and occurred in all listed countries except the oPt. 
Explosive weapons included a range of weapons such as artillery, aerial and drone strikes, 
improvised explosive devices (IEDs) and missiles. The highest number of incidents with 
impact on essential civilian services were attributed to the Israeli Defence Forces (162) and 
joint Israel-U.S. operations (92), and the U.S military (15). Other conflict parties included the 
Islamic Republic of Iran Armed Forces, Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps and Hezbollah. 
Five incidents were attributed to an unidentified conflict party.
The highest number of impacts were recorded in Lebanon (150) followed by Iran (117) with 
the health system being particularly affected in both countries.

The publicly available incidents are not a full account of all incidents. Reporting restrictions 
are placing limitations on reporting and verification. The identified effects are not necessarily 
representative of all such reported cases where the use of explosive weapons caused 
civilian harm and damage to critical infrastructure. Instead, they are a sample of the types

Focus: The impact of hostilities on emergency services
This brief examines how explosive weapons have impacted emergency services 
across the Greater Middle East region. These attacks not only disrupt immediate life-
saving care but systematically weaken the emergency response networks that communities 
depend on. The analysis focuses on: 
1. Emergency medical facilities: Attacks on, or damage to, the physical infrastructure 
needed to provide urgent health care and emergency response. This includes emergency 
departments of hospitals, ambulance centres and headquarters, rescue stations, 
emergency medical centres and warehouses containing emergency medical equipment 
and supplies. These incidents involve destruction of buildings, damage to emergency 
departments and equipment, collapse of structures, fires, and facilities being put out of 
service. 
2. Emergency medical personnel: Health care and rescue workers harmed while carrying 
out emergency duties, including paramedics and ambulance crews, rescue workers and 
emergency response teams. The incidents describe personnel being killed, injured, or 
targeted while rescuing civilians, transporting patients, or responding to earlier strikes, 
as well as those not yet dispatched. 
3. Health providers: The organisations and institutions responsible for delivering health 
care and emergency services included state services, Red Cross (including local services, 
IFRC and ICRC), humanitarian organisation and private providers. In Lebanon, the main 
non-state providers included the Islamic Health Authority (IHA) and Islamic Risala Scout 
Association, as well as the Civil Defence, which is run by IHA.
4. Emergency medical transport: Vehicles and transport systems used to move injured 
people and emergency responders included ambulances, rescue vehicles and other 
emergency health vehicles. Vehicles included cars, motorcycles, air-ambulances and 
maritime ambulances and maybe transporting casualties, travelling to or from rescue 
sites or stationary within facilities such as ambulance centres. 
5. Sequential strikes: An attack on first responders who arrive at the scene of an initial 
strike, to treat or evacuate wounded, or remove bodies. It can also be carried out on 
emergency medical facilities such as emergency departments at the moment injured are 
brought in. 
*In the case of sequential strikes, details of what/who was impacted by the initial strike, to 
which medical teams are responding to, is often not reported. 
The categories above involve functional medical services which retain protection 
under International Humanitarian Law (IHL) and whose targeting raises serious IHL 
concerns.

Strikes on emergency medical services in the Greater Middle East region
Insecurity Insight documented 83 incidents where explosive weapons impacted 
emergency medical services in the Greater Middle East region. Reported incidents 
indicate a consistent pattern of damage to ambulances, emergency medical facilities, and 
aid infrastructure. They include strikes at the time of emergency response where emergency 
medical personnel are killed and injured, as well as on the emergency infrastructure - such 
as an Iranian Red Crescents Society (IRCS) warehouse that was struck by a Israel-U.S. 
drone strike, destroying two aid containers and an ambulance in Bushehr province (Iran). 
These incidents affect the ability to provide care to people injured by the conflict.
Nearly three quarters of incidents were attributed to Israeli forces (73%), and a 
quarter to joint Israel-U.S. strikes (25%). One incident was attributed to Iranian 
forces using drones in Kuwait. Of the 83 incidents, 83% involved airstrikes and 14% 
involved drone strikes.

Double tap or sequential strikes in conflict
“Double tap” originally comes from shooting sports or marksmanship training, where it simply means firing two quick 
shots at the same target to ensure accuracy. 
The term has been used to describe a second attack on first responders who arrive at the scene of an initial strike 
usually to treat or evacuate the wounded. It can also be carried out on emergency departments at the moment injured 
are brought into the emergency care. 
Transferral of a sports term into real-world conflict disguises a strike on medical care in conflict with the use of a 
technical term that not only softens a harsh reality but fails to clearly articulate that such strikes are contrary to the rules 
of war. While the initial target may have been a legitimate target in war, targeted strikes on medical personnel in the 
process of providing medical aid is prohibited under IHL.
The phrase “double tap” flattens a sequence of decisions and at times locations into something that sounds like a 
repeat action or technique. But under IHL, the legal classification depends on who is being targeted, what precautions 
were taken, and what the foreseeable effects are - not on the fact that there were “two strikes.” 
The term ‘double tap’ is misleading because it obscures both the humanitarian protections at stake and the legal 
scrutiny that should apply.
The impact of attacks on emergency medical services
Attacks on emergency services have immediate and long-term consequences for 
civilians and health system resilience. Strikes on ambulances, rescue teams, and first 
responders disrupt search-and-rescue operations, delay casualty evacuation, and obstruct 
access to urgent medical treatment at the moment it is most critical. The risk of preventable 
deaths and avoidable complications rises, while communities face reduced access to 
lifesaving care during periods of heightened vulnerability. A recent study in Gaza showed 
that those waiting over 30 minutes for ambulances faced longer durations in emergency 
departments, and in many cases, ambulances either did not arrive or could not reach the 
hospital. The killing and injury of emergency medical personnel further weakens already 
strained health systems by depleting critical skills, experience, and specialised emergency 
care capacity. Remaining staff face heavier workloads under dangerous and traumatic 
conditions, undermining both the continuity and quality of care. 
In southern Lebanon, strikes have increased caution among medics responding to incidents 
due to fears of follow-up attacks. A Red Cross paramedic stated that teams now operate with 
a deliberate 15-minute delay, describing it as “a gamble between life and death.” Despite 
these risks, one paramedic stated “We pledged to be present here in Nabatieh, and we 
will continue as long as the war lasts, even if there are more martyrs ,” following the 
killing of their two colleagues, one of whom was a minor, on 24 March.
Lebanon 
Emergency medical services in Lebanon were impacted by Israeli air and drone 
strikes on 59 occasions, making up 51% of the 115 documented incidents affecting health 
care between 2 March and 15 April 2026. All but one took place in Nabatieh and South 
governorates of Lebanon where first responders provided medical care in areas with a 
high concentration of Hezbollah control, since they formed to ‘fight Israel’s invasion and 
occupation of southern Lebanon’. One incident was recorded in the northeastern Baalbek-
Hermel governorate on 8 April during Israel’s largest strike across Lebanon since its military 
operation began on 2 March, damaging a Civil Defence centre and killing three people.
A total of 13 emergency medical facilities, 132 emergency medical personnel and 25 
emergency medical transports were impacted.
Sequential strikes on emergency responders
On at least 15 occasions, emergency health services in Lebanon were impacted by 
sequential strikes, killing at least 18 first responders and injuring 26 from the Civil Defence, 
Islamic Risala Scout Association, IHA and Red Cross. In one incident in Siddiqin town, 
South governorate on 3 March, three paramedics were killed and another six injured while 
recovering people injured by Israeli strikes. Rescuers were also fired at by a drone on 9 
April in Toul town, Nabatieh governorate, while responding to casualties of five Israeli drone 
strikes near Sheikh Ragheb Harb Hospital; an ambulance belonging to the Islamic Risala 
Scout Association was also damaged. 
A total of 13 emergency vehicles were damaged by sequential strikes. A recent case of 
sequential strikes occurred on 15 April in Mayfadoun, Nabatieh governorate.

Emergency medical facilities
At least 13 emergency medical facilities in Lebanon were reported damaged or 
destroyed by Israeli forces, of which nine were affiliated with the IHA. Complete destruction 
occurred in three incidents in Nabatieh: the headquarters of the Al-Risala Ambulance 
Association on 31 March which killed a paramedic; a Civil Defence (IHA-affiliated) centre on 
17 March which injured nine health workers; and Beit al-Talaba Emergency Medical Centre 
on 05 March which rendered it out of service, killing and injuring an unspecified number of 
people. In one attack on 13 April, Israeli forces reported that they had targeted and killed a 
Hezbollah member when a Lebanese Red Cross centre was hit by a drone strike. 
Emergency medical personnel
Emergency medical personnel were reported harmed in 81% of the 59 incidents 
affecting emergency responses in Lebanon. In total, 55 were killed and 77 injured. 
Emergency medical personnel are particularly vulnerable in conflict: Approximately 78% 
of the health workers killed in Lebanon between 02 March and 15 April were emergency 
responders. Among them a 16-year-old volunteer paramedic with Nabatieh Ambulance 
Services, who was killed alongside another young medic when an Israeli drone strike fired 
at their motorcycle whilst on a rescue operation; both were wearing uniforms which clearly 
marked them as medical personnel.

Focus: Sequential strikes on emergency medical services in Mayfadoun, Nabatieh 
governorate on 15 April 2026
On 15 April, an IHA ambulance and its team of responders was hit by an Israeli airstrike in the late morning while 
attempting to rescue casualties from an earlier Israeli strike in Mayfadoun town, Nabatieh district and governorate. One 
paramedic was killed and a second was reported missing. 
Minutes later, a second team from the IHA arrived at the scene, where two earlier strikes had already occurred. This 
second rescue team was also hit by an Israeli airstrike, injuring three paramedics.
Two ambulances - one from the Risala Scout Association and one from Nabatieh Ambulance Service - were deployed 
following the three previous strikes and were subsequently struck by an additional Israeli airstrike at the same scene. 
Two paramedics were killed and three others wounded. 
The four strikes (initial strike and three sequential strikes on responders) happened in rapid succession. In total, three 
responders were killed, six injured and one reported missing.  
Under IHL medical personnel, units and transport are afforded special protections, irrespective of their political 
affiliation. Ambulances and medics remain protected unless they are being used outside their medical role for acts 
harmful to the enemy.
At present, open-source reporting does not clearly establish the nature of the initial strike to which the first ambulance 
team was responding. Though the Israeli military made generalisations and justifications stating that Hezbollah 
uses ambulances and medical facilities to transport fighters and weapons, footage released by Nabatieh emergency 
services shows that the ambulances struck in Mayfadoun were visibly empty of weapons.
Information on the weapon system used in the sequential strikes remains limited, though a surviving medic from 
the strike stated that a missile fired from a drone was “directly targeting paramedics”. The strikes occurred in rapid 
succession, with each rescue team responding while casualties from the previous strike were still present, leaving 
responders exposed and clearly identifiable during active rescue efforts.
Emergency medical transport
At least 26 ambulances were reported damaged or destroyed in Lebanon, though 
this is likely underreporting. In one incident on 20 March, three Islamic Health Authority 
vehicles were damaged by an Israeli airstrike while on humanitarian duty in Deir al-Zahrani 
town, Nabatieh governorate, injuring nine paramedics. Emergency medical transport was 
also impacted while the vehicle was not actively responding, as seen when Beit al-Talaba 
Emergency Medical Centre in Nabatieh was destroyed by an Israeli airstrike, putting it out 
of service, damaging ambulances and killing and injuring an unspecified number of people.
Health providers 
Over half of the documented incidents in Lebanon where explosive weapons impacted 
emergency medical services affected the IHA, likely due to its affiliation with Hezbollah 
and prior claims by Israel that the IHA is used as a “cover for terrorist activities” by Hezbollah 
in southern Lebanon. The Islamic Risala Scout Association (an emergency and welfare 
organisation affiliated with the Amal Movement, a political ally of Hezbollah), the state-run 
ambulance services and the Red Cross were also affected.
Red Cross personnel were also harmed: at least two medics were killed and 11 injured across 
five incidents; four where ambulance teams were impacted and one where a Red Cross 
centre was damaged. At least two ambulances clearly marked with Red Cross emblems 
visible from all sides were struck by Israeli forces - one with a drone and one by aircraft - 
despite prior coordination of movements through the usual channels, including UNIFIL, 
raising concerns regarding the protection of humanitarian actors. 
Iran
Emergency medical services in Iran were impacted by air and drone strikes on 19 
occasions, making up 29% of the 66 documented incidents affecting health care between 
28 February and 15 April 2026. Incidents were reported across eight of Iran’s 31 provinces, 
with nearly half reported in Tehran. Incidents were mainly geographically clustered in two 
areas: the south, near key oil-producing areas and the Strait of Hormuz, and the west and 
northwest, near locations identified as missile launch sites during the June 2025 Twelve-
Day War.
All but two incidents were attributed to joint Israel-U.S. forces. Two were linked to Israeli 
forces. Eight emergency medical facilities, 132 emergency medical personnel and 19 
emergency medical transports were impacted.
Sequential strikes on first responders
Two incidents of sequential strikes in Iran involved Iranian Red Crescent teams. The 
first occurred on 10 March when responders were conducting debris-removal operations at 
a prior attack site in the Resalat area of Tehran. The second struck an ambulance assisting 
the injured in Lar, Fars province, on 18 March. No personnel were reportedly killed or injured 
in either incident.

Emergency medical personnel
At least 14 emergency medical personnel were killed and eight injured by Israel-U.S. 
airstrikes in Iran. Almost all casualties were recorded in Tehran province, including one 
incident which killed 10 personnel when the Naziabad Emergency Base was hit by an Israel-
U.S. airstrike on 7 March. Three emergency medical personnel were killed in Fars and 
another three injured in Qom on 3 March. 
Emergency medical facilities
Eight emergency medical facilities in Iran were damaged or destroyed by joint 
Israel-U.S. strikes across six provinces, five by aircraft and one by drone. One facility, the 
Emergency Medical Services (EMS) 115 base in Shiraz city, Fars province, was completely 
destroyed on 5 March during Israel-U.S. airstrikes targeting military sites. Five emergency 
medical workers were injured in two facilities, and 13 were killed in another two facilities.
Emergency medical transport
Israel-U.S. forces damaged or destroyed 18 ambulances in Iran, including a maritime 
ambulance in Hormozgan province on 11 March and an air ambulance in Bushehr province 
on 9 March. Israeli forces damaged an ambulance in Tehran. At least 16 ambulances 
were damaged in airstrikes. Two ambulances were destroyed when an IRCS warehouse 
in Bushehr province was struck by an Israel-U.S. drone which also destroyed two aid 
containers. Damage to or destruction of ambulances was mostly reported in Tehran but also 
occurred in the south-western provinces.

Iraq
All four incidents in Iraq where explosive weapons impacted emergency medical 
services were sequential Israel-U.S. aircraft strikes  impacting ambulance teams in 
western Iraq between 12 and 24 March. Incidents were split evenly between Al Anbar 
governorate, where a Popular Mobilization Forces military headquarters was damaged on 
12 March, and neighbouring Salah al-Din governorate.
At least one emergency worker was injured when the ambulance they were travelling in was 
hit by an Israeli aircraft strike while attempting to evacuate casualties from an earlier strike 
on 16 March. These incidents suggest repeated risks to emergency responders from attacks 
during evacuation operations, similar to patterns observed in Mayfadoun in Lebanon.
Kuwait
One incident where explosive weapons impacted emergency medical services was 
recorded in Kuwait. Debris from an intercepted drone strike from suspected Iranian forces 
fell onto an ambulance centre and injured two emergency medical workers in Kuwait city on 
17 March

Other resources
Armed drones are becoming a defining feature of modern conflict, creating escalating risks for 
humanitarian operations and forcing aid agencies to adapt their security strategies to better 
protect staff in high-risk environments. Insecurity Insight’s Sudden Threats from the Sky: 
Guidance for the Protection of Aid is a practical resource designed to help humanitarian 
organisations strengthen preparedness and operational resilience. Created for local aid agencies 
and their international partners, the guidance provides actionable strategies for assessing and 
mitigating drone-related risks, identifying feasible protective measures, and developing effective 
emergency response plans. Available in Arabic, Burmese, English, French and Spanish, the 
guidance is designed for real-world application in staff briefings, training, and office awareness 
materials. 
Hovering Threats: The Challenges of Armed Drones in Humanitarian Contexts explores the 
growing impact of armed drones on humanitarian operations in conflict zones, shedding light on 
the evolving risks faced by aid workers and health care providers. By examining both the technical 
capabilities of armed drones and the practical realities of operating under drone threats, it offers 
critical insights into how humanitarian organisations can strengthen security risk management 
and safeguard lifesaving assistance. Grounded in real-world challenges and emerging best 
practices, the report delivers actionable recommendations for aid agency security managers and 
advocates working to protect humanitarian access in increasingly volatile environments.  
IHL and the protection of food systems, health care and aid operations in Sudan highlights 
concerns under international humanitarian law arising from attacks on health care, food systems, 
and humanitarian operations in Sudan. It examines how sequential strikes on protected civilian 
infrastructure -  including medical facilities, markets, agricultural areas, and aid operations - may 
violate the obligation to respect and protect objects indispensable to civilian survival and ensure 
the safe delivery of humanitarian assistance. In a context where millions depend on aid, the 
report underscores how disruptions to essential services and humanitarian access can have 
foreseeable and devastating consequences for civilian populations.
Using data by Insecurity Insight, the Safeguarding Health in Conflict Coalition (SHCC) identified 
over 480 attacks on health care in Lebanon in 2024, including over 350 paramedics killed 
alongside repeated strikes on emergency medical services and health facilities. The escalation 
of Israeli airstrikes strained an already fragile health system, triggering widespread closures, 
overwhelming facilities with mass casualties, and contributing to a deepening humanitarian and 
economic crisis. Available in Arabic and English.
