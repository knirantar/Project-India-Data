The Situation Hub's analytical process uses a structured Human-in-the-Loop workflow that combines automated analysis retrieval
through GANNET Virtual Assistant, DFS's AI-powered humanitarian assistant, with expert review by the DFS analysis team. GANNET
automates the extraction and generation of crisis-related insights based on structured prompts, which are aligned with a tailored
analytical framework. These outputs are then reviewed, validated, and refined by analysts to ensure accuracy, contextual relevance,
and operational utility. The workflow is organized into four key phases: planning and setup, ongoing operations, quality assurance, and
system evolution, ensuring a balance between speed and analytical rigor.
Planning, Setup & Quality Control
In the planning phase, analysts develop a contextualized analytical framework, largely based on the Joint Intersectoral Analysis
Framework (JIAF), and convert it into structured prompts for AI-assisted data extraction. These prompts are refined over time to
improve output quality. Data sources are vetted for reliability, and translations are reviewed for accuracy. Using a centralized Content
Management System (CMS), analysts review and curate AI-generated outputs, validate them against external sources, and make
refinements to enhance clarity and relevance. The CMS enables collaborative editing and version tracking, ensuring outputs align with
humanitarian needs.

Analysts are crucial in identifying and correcting inaccuracies, especially in automated categorization. Future improvements aim to
enhance source credibility checks and sentiment analysis, further strengthening the reliability of the Situation Hub's outputs.

Summary
Myanmar has been in crisis since 1 February 2021, when the military (Tatmadaw) overthrew the democratically elected government.
This coup sparked widespread protests and armed resistance, leading to a severe political, economic, and humanitarian crisis. The
military regime, known as the State Administration Council (SAC), has responded with brutal force, resulting in thousands of civilian
deaths and widespread human rights abuses. As of March 2026, approximately 3.7 million people are internally displaced across the
country—a dramatic increase from roughly 300,000 pre-2021—with cross-border displacement projected to rise toward 5.2 million in
2026.
In attempt to seek international legitimacy, the military held general elections in three phases over December 2025 and January 2026
for the first time since the coup. The overwhelming majority of seats were won by the pro-military Union Solidarity and Development
Party (USDP) and saw SAC chairman Min Aung Hlaing elected as president in April 2026. The elections have been widely denounced as
neither free nor fair. Despite the transition to a quasi-civilian presidency, armed clashes have continued in multiple parts of the
country, including Sagaing, Magway, northern Shan, Kayah, Kayin, Kachin, and Rakhine. The Arakan Army (AA) now controls all but
three of Rakhine State's 17 townships, while resistance forces including the Karen National Liberation Army (KNLA) have consolidated
influence across parts of central and southeastern Myanmar. The UN's 2026 Humanitarian Needs and Response Plan (HNRP), released
10 December 2025, projects over 16 million people will require humanitarian assistance this year.
Recovery from the March 2025 earthquake remains slow, with an estimated 3.5 million tonnes of debris and reconstruction hampered
by insecurity, shortages of heavy machinery (43%) and labour (40%), and limited access. Many affected communities, particularly in
Magway, Sagaing, and Mandalay, continue to live in temporary shelters, while essential services remain disrupted. Approximately
24,200 houses were damaged (24% completely destroyed), and 132 health facilities were damaged—half in Mandalay.
The Rohingya crisis continues to worsen, with the junta's blockade of Rakhine preventing international aid delivery since late 2023,
leaving hundreds of thousands of IDPs with severely limited assistance. On 10 December 2025, a military airstrike destroyed Mrauk-U
General Hospital, killing at least 33 people and injuring 76-80—one of the deadliest attacks on healthcare in the conflict. Chronic
poverty now affects over 80% of the population; the World Bank projects GDP will contract 2.0% in FY2025/26 with inflation remaining
above 20%, and market disruptions have deepened food insecurity nationwide. The 2026 HNRP appeals for approximately $890 million
but reflects severely constrained funding and narrowed operational space, forcing agencies to prioritize only 2.6 million people with
the most severe needs for life-saving support.
Key Insights
1. Myanmar's humanitarian crisis continues to deepen amid persistent conflict, economic collapse, and the lingering impacts of the
March 2025 earthquake. An estimated 16 million people—nearly one in three citizens—are projected to require humanitarian assistance
in 2026, as conflict-driven displacement, natural disasters, funding shortages, and humanitarian access constraints exacerbate
vulnerabilities nationwide.
The 28 March 2025 earthquake remains a defining event in the humanitarian landscape. Official figures from SAC confirm 3,770 deaths
and over 5,000 injuries, with the Early Recovery Cluster reporting over 24,200 houses damaged (24% completely destroyed), 4,857
apartments affected, and 132 health facilities damaged. An estimated 3.5 million tonnes of debris blocks roads and markets, with
clearance impeded by shortages of heavy machinery (43%) and labour (40%). Nearly three-quarters of the most affected townships
continue to face critical shelter and livelihood gaps—64% of communities reported job losses and 30-31% reported damage to crops
and workplaces—while disrupted water systems (44% of groundwater sources contaminated) have fueled outbreaks of cholera and
dengue.
2. Following the junta-run general elections held over December 2025 and January 2026, as well as ceasefires between the regime and
some ethnic armed organizations in northern Shan, the number of armed conflict incidents has gradually decreased. Nonetheless, air
and ground offensives continue in the Central Dry Zone, as well as Rakhine, Kayah, and Kayin States. The new government's 100-day
roadmap announced on 21 April 2026, which invites resistance groups to enter peace talks and to surrender, have not been widely
accepted. 
Between January and November 2025, the military conducted 2,165 airstrikes, up from 1,716 in all of 2024. On 10 December 2025, a
military airstrike destroyed Mrauk-U General Hospital in Rakhine State, killing at least 33 people and injuring 76-80—prompting UN,
WHO, and Amnesty International condemnation and calls for investigation as a possible war crime.
IDPs have reached approximately 3.7 million people as of March 2025, with projections indicating it could rise further through 2026. The
Arakan Army now controls all but three of Rakhine's 17 townships, while resistance forces have captured extensive border areas in the
southeast. Attacks on healthcare are widespread: Insecurity Insight recorded 1,815 attacks on health care between February 2021 and
November 2025, with WHO verifying 67 attacks in 2025 alone. Access restrictions, including the junta's blockade of Rakhine since late
2023, continue to endanger civilians—particularly Rohingya communities facing persistent protection risks, restricted mobility, and

Operational Environment
Economy
Current Economic Situation
Myanmar faced acute economic pressures in mid-2026, driven by both domestic conflict dynamics and global shocks. The Middle
East crisis and Strait of Hormuz disruptions pushed up fuel and energy prices, with local fuel prices spiking by 30–50% in some areas
and basic staple food prices (rice, salt, pulses) rising approximately 22% since the start of the Iran war [17][4][23]. Myanmar's structural
vulnerabilities amplified these shocks: the country relies on imports for roughly 90% of its fuel and nearly all fertilizer, increasing risks to
agricultural production [17][23]. Domestic trade was further disrupted when junta authorities began inspections and seizures at
checkpoints from 4 May 2026, effectively halting many freight operations into northern Shan State and restricting movement of
Chinese goods along key internal trade routes [13]. Humanitarian financing remained severely constrained, with UNHCR appealing for
USD 328.5 million for the Myanmar situation in 2026 but receiving only 26% of the requested funds, limiting response capacity amid
these compounding economic shocks [6].
Conflict Economic Landscape
As of end-March 2026, Myanmar's conflict-affected economic landscape was shaped by mass displacement and entrenched
structural disruptions. An estimated 5.3 million people were forcibly displaced, including 3.7 million internally displaced persons (IDPs),
1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers, and 1.85 million Rohingya stateless persons [6]. Conflict-related access constraints, airstrikes,
and road blockages have restricted humanitarian access and disrupted supply chains, while ongoing insecurity, underfunding, and
energy shocks continue to undermine recovery and prolong economic hardship [4][17].
Trade and infrastructure disruptions are severely constraining economic activity at key borders: armed groups, notably the MNDAA,
control major northern Shan State border trade gateways, and the junta has blocked crossings and kept the Hsipaw bridge closed
since November, limiting access to Chinese markets and commercial routes [13]. Shipping disruptions linked to the broader regional
crisis have further harmed small businesses and reduced input availability for traders and manufacturers [17].
The conflict environment has also incubated illicit economies that distort labour markets and revenue streams. Online scam factories
expanded during the civil war, prompting the junta to propose an Anti-Online Scam Bill, and the phenomenon is linked to
multibillion-dollar losses abroad, including over $20 billion in reported U.S. victim losses for 2025 [11][10]. Political developments and
tentative regional re-engagement — including ASEAN deliberations and China's stated interest in stability to enable infrastructure and
investment — are cited by analysts as factors that could affect the scale and timing of foreign investment, though normalization
remains contested and conditional [7][26]. A large 11,000-carat ruby discovered in mid-April near Mogok and publicly inspected by
President Min Aung Hlaing in mid-May 2026 highlighted the gem sector's continued economic significance amid these disruptions
[35].
Stability of Currency and Bank System
Myanmar's monetary and financial stability has been further eroded by intersecting pressures. Many households report that daily
costs have roughly doubled from a year earlier, reflecting the cumulative impact of fuel and food price inflation [17]. Rice exports, a key
foreign exchange earner, generated approximately US$861 million in the previous year, though ongoing trade and supply chain
disruptions pose risks to this revenue stream [17]. Junta checkpoint inspections beginning 4 May 2026 in northern Shan State nearly
stopped freight through MNDAA-controlled border routes, further disrupting trade flows and constraining foreign currency earnings
[13].
Employment
Displacement and conflict have severely undermined livelihoods and employment across Myanmar. With 5.3 million people forcibly
displaced as of end-March 2026, livelihood opportunities for displaced populations remain extremely limited, particularly in
Bangladesh camps where arrivals since 2024 have further stretched services and employment prospects [4][6]. Fuel and fertilizer
shortages have sharply increased input and transport costs, disrupting farming and small businesses; some farmers have abandoned
land and tailoring enterprises have reduced activity, undermining rural livelihoods and income generation in Rakhine and other
affected areas [17]. Approximately 12.5 million people are already food insecure, further pressuring household coping strategies and
livelihood sustainability [17].
Attacks on the health sector have eroded the health workforce and public-sector employment: Insecurity Insight reported 1,918 attacks
on healthcare in Myanmar since the 2021 coup, with 173 health workers killed and 909 arrested [40]. The illicit online "scam" economy
continues to generate informal employment linked to trafficking and forced labour, while the military-backed legislature is drafting an
Anti-Online Scam Bill proposing heavy penalties, including the death penalty for coercion into scam centres, which could affect
responses to forced labour in the sector [11]. Local coordination networks have urged donors to prioritise direct support to NGOs and
community organisations that can reach displaced communities and deliver livelihoods and cash assistance outside junta-controlled
channels [31].
Politics
Governance Structures and Rule of Law
Since the 2021 coup, the military has sought to rebrand its rule — including deeply flawed elections held in December 2025–January
2026 that were widely rejected internationally, and the presentation of Senior General Min Aung Hlaing as a civilian president — moves
critics say aim to launder junta legitimacy [77][81][84][78]. Martial law has been imposed in dozens of townships, giving military
tribunals sweeping powers over civilians and constraining political rights and peaceful dissent [77].

The regime has also advanced new legislation such as an AntiOnline Scam Bill proposing harsh penalties, including the death penalty
for certain cyberscam offences, viewed as part of a wider agenda to consolidate control [81][84]. The junta revoked the publishing
licences of at least three independent media outlets on 11 May 2026, intensifying a broader press crackdown that reduces public
access to impartial information [100].
The Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar (IIMM) is investigating multiple airstrikes that appear to target civilians —
including the 12 May 2025 school attack in Sagaing and a 1 May 2026 playground strike in Chin State — and is collecting
chain-of-command evidence to identify perpetrators for potential war crimes or crimes against humanity [108][110][115]. The junta has
invited some ethnic armed organisations to informal talks (invitations sent 24 April), but EAOs and observers note that ongoing
airstrikes, drone strikes and artillery shelling undermine trust in negotiations [112].
ASEAN continues to press for implementation of the FivePoint Consensus and has described progress as "minimal," while debates over
calibrated reengagement reflect regional concern about normalising the junta without measurable accountability and humanitarian
access guarantees [101][86]. The regime has made limited concessions such as the reported release of approximately 5,500 prisoners
— including former vicepresident U Win Myint and more than 4,000 in an initial release followed by over 1,500 subsequently — and
moving Daw Aung San Suu Kyi from prison to house arrest with a reduced prison term, but these steps have been characterised as
insufficient by many international and regional actors [88][89][107][91].
Political Freedoms and Recent Laws
At the 48th ASEAN Leaders' Meeting in Cebu (7–8 May 2026), leaders reaffirmed the FivePoint Consensus and expressed concern
about "minimal progress" on its implementation amid continuing violence and restrictions on civic space [89][101]. ASEAN member
states and regional commentators have advocated calibrated, conditional reengagement — insisting on measurable reductions in
violence, inclusive dialogue involving the National Unity Government and ethnic actors, broader humanitarian access, and the release
of political prisoners to avoid legitimising the junta without progress on these benchmarks [88][86][77]. The junta publicly denounced
ASEAN's exclusion as discriminatory and defended its elections as "free and fair" [101].
Arbitrary arrests, routine identity and guestlist checks, torture and extortion have been reported in locations such as Sittwe, with
operations targeting men and producing frequent arbitrary detentions [76]. The junta has conducted prosecutions and highprofile
trials of detained civilian leaders, actions criticised as lacking transparency and used as leverage in political negotiations [77][85].
Monitoring groups report large-scale political violence: ACLED recorded around 95,000 fatalities linked to conflict since the February
2021 coup, estimating an average of about 1,550 killings per month and more than 28.6 million people exposed to violence in the past
year [85].
Influence of Non-State Actors and Political Factions
Political authority in Myanmar is fragmented between the junta, a civilian National Unity Government (NUG) in opposition, and multiple
ethnic armed organisations — an environment in which ASEAN engagement has struggled to enforce its own benchmarks
[85][107][86][77]. Nonstate armed groups, most prominently the Arakan Army (AA), control large parts of Rakhine State, have
expanded operations into neighbouring regions, and have pledged to seize the entirety of Rakhine, driving intense frontline fighting
and localised blockades that have eroded state control outside juntaheld towns [82]. Both the AA and the Burmese military have
been documented committing serious abuses — including arbitrary detention, forced recruitment, sexual violence and attacks on
civilians — contributing to entrenched impunity and acute protection risks for Rohingya and other communities [68].
The junta has engaged in informal meetings with certain ethnic armed groups — planned in Thailand and in Mawlamyine, Kyaingtong
(Kengtung) and Mongla — but observers say these overtures are inadequate while civilian deaths from airstrikes continue to rise [112].
The combined effect of armed nonstate actors and informal military and political networks has constrained humanitarian access
and logistics: UNHCR reported fuel shortages, roadblocks and movement controls disrupting programme delivery, and attacks on
health facilities have been recorded across the country [72]. As of endMarch 2026, the Myanmar situation involved 5.3 million forcibly
displaced persons, including 3.7 million internally displaced persons, 1.6 million refugees and asylumseekers, and 1.85 million Rohingya
stateless persons [72][99].
Public Trust in State Institutions
Public trust in Myanmar's state institutions is severely eroded amid a nationwide legitimacy crisis, as the junta lacks legitimacy across
large parts of the country where authority is contested by ethnic armed organisations, local defence forces and civil society networks
[77]. Ongoing military airstrikes, arbitrary detention, torture, village burnings and forced displacement since the 2021 coup have
deepened public distrust and undermined confidence in the state's protection role [77]. Efforts at peace talks have been further
undermined by continued strikes and rising civilian casualties, which observers say erode trust that dialogue can deliver security or
accountability [82][112].
Large-scale displacement and humanitarian deterioration have weakened confidence in public services — tens of thousands were
displaced in Kyaukphyu Township since clashes began in November 2025, and aid groups warned of shortages of food, clean water
and clothing for displaced families sheltering across nearly 70 villages [82]. Junta-led gestures — including prisoner releases and
moving Daw Aung San Suu Kyi to house arrest — have been seen by analysts and ASEAN officials as insufficient to rebuild trust without
transparency, verified access and substantive reforms [89][107][91]. Regional leaders urged verifiable steps before restoring
Myanmar's regional standing, including granting ASEAN's special envoy access to detained leaders and guaranteeing unhindered
humanitarian access and inclusive dialogue [107][86][89].

Entrenched dehumanising language and antiRohingya narratives remain pervasive in Myanmar society, undermining social inclusion
and increasing risks of targeted violence against the Rohingya [104]. International legal processes are underway: judges at the

Across the region, approximately 1.6 million refugees and asylumseekers from Myanmar had been documented by UNHCR as of

Security And Stability
Security Overview
Airstrikes by the Myanmar military have continued to hit civilian locations — including schools, hospitals, places of worship and
displacement sites — and are under investigation by the Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar (IIMM) for possible war
crimes or crimes against humanity [149][150]. Based on available (likely underestimated) data, children were killed or injured in at
least 640 airstrikes between February 2021 and 2025 [149][150]. In early May 2026, an air attack on a village playground in Chin State
killed at least five children according to the IIMM, with a locally updated casualty list reporting six children and one adult killed in that
strike [149][150][154]. The deadly attack on Oe Htein Kwin village school in Sagaing Region on 12 May 2025, which killed more than 20
students, remains a focal case for ongoing IIMM investigations [149][150]. 
Ongoing air and ground hostilities, attacks on health infrastructure, restrictions in Arakan Army (AA)- and junta-held areas, and
cross-border criminality (drugs, smuggling) are the principal security threats to civilians, aid workers and humanitarian operations
across Myanmar, constraining safe access and delivery of assistance [125][153]. The junta has simultaneously sought informal
engagement with some ethnic armed organisations while continuing air, drone and artillery strikes, undermining trust in talks and
complicating protection and access [156]. The AA controls and administers large parts of northern Rakhine — including Maungdaw,
seized in December 2024 — and reports operations to tackle narcotics and cross-border threats, affecting border security and
humanitarian access along the Bangladesh frontier [158][125].

Security Forces and Law Enforcement
Myanmar's military has intensified large-scale offensives across central areas — including Ngazun, Myingyan and Taungtha townships
— using airstrikes, artillery, drones and suicide drones in early May 2026, prompting mass displacement and civilian casualties [133].
Local monitoring groups recorded 1,122 human rights violations across Yangon, Sagaing, Magway and Naypyidaw between March 2025
and February 2026, attributing 915 incidents to junta troops and allied Pyusawhti militias — including nighttime raids,
conscription-related abductions, airstrikes and destruction of civilian structures [132].
In parts of Rakhine, the AA has effectively assumed security and policing roles following its seizure of Maungdaw Township in
December 2024, expanding troop deployments, tightening movement controls, operating administrative checkpoints and increasing
maritime patrols to detain suspected intruders or fishermen, while stating efforts to curb narcotics trafficking along the
Maungdaw–Bangladesh frontier [158]. Independent documentation also reports coercive enforcement practices in AA-controlled
areas between May 2024 and April 2026, including arbitrary detention, forced recruitment tied to village quotas and escalating
incidents of sexual violence and exploitation under local enforcement systems [125].
In northern Shan State, the junta began intensified inspections and seizures of Chinese-origin goods at checkpoints from 4 May 2026,
disrupting freight on routes controlled by the MNDAA and tightening border controls [127]. Law-enforcement actions against telecom
scam operations have included arrests and raids across Shan State — including 90 Chinese suspects detained in Matman — and the
publication of an Anti-Online Scam Bill on 14 May 2026 proposing severe penalties, including capital punishment for forced detention
or violent coercion in scam centres [138][129]. The junta has also sought external security cooperation, including reported talks on
border security with India's navy, while neighbouring states and ASEAN actors pursue calibrated engagement to manage cross-border
insecurity and related transnational crime risks [160][123].
These combined security dynamics — airstrikes, shelling, forced recruitment, arbitrary detention and movement controls by both state
and non-state forces — have severely constrained humanitarian access and protection. UNHCR recorded 3.7 million IDPs in Myanmar
as of end-March 2026 and cited 1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers from Myanmar in the region as of the same date [153][126].

Demography
Demographic Overview
As of end-March 2026, UNHCR reported 5.3 million people forcibly displaced within the Myanmar situation, including 3.7 million
internally displaced persons (IDPs) and 1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers from Myanmar, with 1.85 million Rohingya who are
stateless within the broader situation [165][164]. Internal displacement within Myanmar has concentrated in hotspots including
Sagaing, Rakhine and Kachin states, with major conflict- and disaster-related displacements recorded through 2025–2026 in those
areas [167]. UNHCR also documented continued risky maritime movements and interceptions involving Rohingya in early 2026, with
multiple boats intercepted or returned and ongoing maritime arrivals and returns since January 2026 [165].
Cross-border and return movements also include non-Muslim ethnic communities such as Marma, Mro, and Chakma relocating
between Bangladesh and Myanmar, with estimates of more than 20,000 Marma settled in Myanmar reported in recent years [171].

Socio-Cultural Overview

- 3.7 million were internally displaced inside Myanmar [193][227].
- 1.6 million were refugees and asylumseekers from Myanmar regionally, and 1.85 million Rohingya were recorded as stateless

Rohingya communities face entrenched social exclusion and dehumanising public narratives rooted in longstanding ethnic and
religious prejudice, while younger, digitally connected generations influenced by the 2021 anticoup movement show some greater

into Rakhine and Chin states in Myanmar, with reports of over 20,000 Marma settled in Myanmar in recent years, reflecting complex
crossborder ethnic movements and localised protection needs [199]. Humanitarian access and social cohesion are undermined by
insecurity, movement controls and administrative diversion of aid; local networks and ethnic service providers are urging donors to
bypass junta channels and deliver assistance directly to borderbased CSOs and ethnic organisations to reach displaced

Gender Dynamics
BROUK's documentation from Rakhine (May 2024–April 2026) describes organised patterns of sexual violence, trafficking, survival sex
and other coercive sexual exploitation affecting Rohingya women and girls, with significant underreporting and indications that sexual
violence is being used as a tool of intimidation that may amount to atrocity crimes [221]. Movement restrictions, economic deprivation
and restricted humanitarian access are linked to increased risks for women and girls, including dangerous maritime journeys with
heightened exposure to trafficking and sexual exploitation [221]. BROUK also highlights a nearabsence of accountability — with no
public evidence of credible investigations by the Arakan Army — and persistent barriers to access to justice that enable ongoing
abuse [221]. The scale of displacement and statelessness further exacerbates these gendered vulnerabilities [227]. At the same time,
UNHCR programmes supporting women's livelihoods — such as UNHCRsupported sewing workshops — are being used to build skills,
income and dignity, reflecting women's role in response and selfreliance efforts [227]. A recent Global Health Cluster evaluation found
Myanmar had made relative progress in mainstreaming equity considerations, including gender, into strategies and data
disaggregation, but noted that broader gender mainstreaming in health responses remains limited and accountability to affected
populations is weak [201].
Impact of Socio-Cultural Drivers on Aid
Dehumanising public rhetoric and entrenched social bias against the Rohingya persist in Myanmar, normalising exclusionary attitudes
that undermine acceptance of impartial aid and protection for Rohingya communities [196][221]. Religious leaders and sermons have
at times contributed to rhetoric justifying violence and exclusion, further weakening local receptivity to neutral humanitarian
assistance for affected minorities [196]. Early signs of shifting social norms are visible among the younger, digital-era anti-coup
generation, who show greater openness to diversity, potentially creating space for more inclusive, locally led engagement over time
[196].
Since 2023, the Arakan Army (AA) has expanded territorial control across large parts of Rakhine State. AA authority is associated with
forced recruitment, arbitrary detention, movement restrictions and organised sexual violence that isolate communities and impede
humanitarian access [221]. Detention practices — including incommunicado transfers to AA camps and village-level arrest operations
— have removed community protection mechanisms, increased stigma, and limited survivors' ability to seek services or report abuses
[221]. Fear of retaliation, stigma and severe access constraints mean sexual violence and related abuses are significantly
underreported, constraining needs assessment and survivor-centred responses [221]. Military-imposed communications controls and
the junta's revocation of independent media licences on 11 May 2026 have further reduced independent information channels and
public trust, complicating outreach, risk-communication and community engagement efforts [221][240].
Severe deprivation and movement restrictions are driving survival coping — including survival sex and risky onward movement —
increasing protection needs and undermining social cohesion between Rohingya and host communities [221]. Large-scale
displacement is reshaping host–displaced relations and straining informal support networks, with 1,194,123 Rohingya refugees
registered in Bangladesh as of 30 April 2026 [227][245].
9
Funding shortfalls compound these challenges: UNHCR appealed for USD 328.5 million for the Myanmar response in 2026 but had
achieved only 26% of that appeal, limiting operational capacity and community confidence in the humanitarian response [227].
Environment
Environmental Conditions
For the week of 4–10 May 2026, the ASEAN Specialised Meteorological Centre reported medium to high 7-day average rainfall across
Myanmar [257]. Tropical Cyclone HAGUPIT was tracked moving northwest and was forecast to weaken to a remnant low by the
evening of 11 May or early 12 May 2026, potentially moderating but also redistributing rainfall in the region [257]. ASMC forecast wetter
conditions over most of the equatorial region and predicted a long lasting effect of El Niño during June–July 2026, with
warmer-than-usual temperatures expected across much of southeastern Mainland Southeast Asia and the northeastern Maritime
Continent [257].
Environmental Challenges
Asia experienced an unprecedented wildfire season in early 2026, with researchers reporting approximately 44 million hectares burned
between January and April and naming Myanmar among the most affected countries [263][264][267]. Wildfires and associated
burning have contributed to deforestation, land degradation, and hazardous air pollution, with documented health consequences
highlighted by climate and public-health experts [264][263]. Climate scientists warned that an emerging El Niño and unusually rapid
swings from wet to dry conditions could further amplify fires, drought, and heat extremes in the coming months [264][263].
Large-scale displacement and refugee arrivals — including Rohingya movements into Bangladesh through March 2026 — have
increased pressure on WASH and other services, worsening environmental conditions in crowded camps and host areas [276][286]. A
related geophysical concern is that the first live video of surface fault rupture was recorded in Myanmar in March 2025, and
subsequent research shows soil density strongly influences how surface rupture and ground deformation manifest, with implications
for variable land and infrastructure vulnerability in seismic events [279].
Environmental Risks and Mitigation
Myanmar remains exposed to significant geophysical hazards: a shallow 7.7-magnitude earthquake on 28 March triggered around
327,000 displacements, destroyed nearly 12,000 homes, and left roughly 280,000 people still displaced, demonstrating how disasters
compound conflict-driven vulnerability [250][248]. The AHA Centre also recorded multiple disaster events affecting the country during
the week of 4–10 May 2026, including floods, landslides, and storms [268][257]. Climate agencies warned in May 2026 of a likely
transition toward El Niño conditions later in the year, raising the risk of drought, extreme heat, and wildfires in parts of southern Asia
and underscoring the need for heightened seasonal preparedness in Myanmar [264][263]. Noted mitigation and preparedness
measures include the ASEAN Emergency Logistics Roadmap 2026–2030 to strengthen regional emergency logistics, partners using
earthquake-response information to adjust operations, and recommendations for coordinated monitoring, emergency planning, and
financing for reconstruction — including insurance mechanisms — to reduce displacement and speed recovery [261][250].
Conflict
Overview of the Ongoing Armed Conflict
Active conflict zones span multiple regions of Myanmar. In Mandalay Region—notably Ngazun, Myingyan and Taungtha
townships—large-scale junta offensives, airstrikes and drone attacks have displaced tens of thousands and forced resistance
withdrawals [337]. Sagaing Region (including Tigyaing, Katha, Shwebo, Monywa and Kale areas) has seen frequent ambushes and
convoy attacks involving the Arakan Army (AA), Bamar People's Liberation Army and PDF units, with strikes on convoys on 10–11 May
seizing weapons and inflicting junta casualties [347][353]. Rakhine State remains highly contested: the AA controls most townships
while the junta sustains intensified air and drone strikes around Kyaukphyu, Minbya, Kyauktaw and Sittwe, producing large
displacements and acute shortages among civilians sheltering across dozens of villages [328]. Chin State has suffered multiple
airstrikes in early May—including a 1 May attack on Khawpuichhip—causing child and civilian deaths and reflecting an intensified junta
air campaign in western states [360][340][349].
Conflict intensity is high and growing, with routine use of fighter jets, artillery, howitzers, drones and suicide-drones, accompanied by
amphibious/naval manoeuvres and heavy ground assaults across multiple fronts [337][353][328]. Air and drone strikes have
damaged schools, hospitals, places of worship and displacement shelters, with the Independent Investigative Mechanism for
Myanmar investigating strikes that killed children and other attacks disrupting health and education services [349][360][328].
Battlefields feature multiple armed actors and growing weapons proliferation: the AA has expanded operations beyond Rakhine into
Sagaing, coordinated actions by PDF, BPLA and LDF units are common, pro-junta Pyu Saw Htee militias operate alongside the military,
and ambushes have yielded captured arms and equipment [347][337][314].
Humanitarian access is severely constrained by active hostilities, movement restrictions and localized coercive controls, prompting
calls from local networks for direct aid delivery to displaced communities bypassing the junta [343][356][328]. BROUK documents
escalating sexual violence against Rohingya women and girls in AA-controlled northern Rakhine linked to detention, forced
recruitment and incommunicado confinement, based on reporting through April 2026 [326][356].
Conflict Stakeholders
The main state actor is the military junta (Tatmadaw), supported by pro-junta Pyu Saw Htee/Pyusawhti militias, conducting
large-scale offensives, airstrikes, drone and artillery operations to retake resistance-held territory and reopen logistics routes in central
and northern corridors [337]. Key anti-junta actors include the National Unity Government (NUG) and its affiliated People's Defence
Forces (PDF) and Local Defence Forces (LDF), alongside a range of ethnic armed organisations (EAOs) and local resistance formations
10
such as the Arakan Army (AA), People's Liberation Army (PLA), Bamar People's Liberation Army (BPLA), Chin Brotherhood
Alliance/Chinland Defence Force, Kachin-aligned forces and the Karenni National Progressive Party (KNPP) [314][347][331][340][311].
Civil society monitoring (PMCSG) recorded 1,122 human rights violations across Yangon, Sagaing, Magway and Naypyidaw over the
twelve-month period from early March 2025 to end-February 2026, with Sagaing accounting for 670 incidents, Yangon 236, Naypyidaw
121 and Magway 95 [314]. The perpetrator breakdown attributed 915 incidents to junta troops and allied Pyu Saw Htee militias, 105 to
PDF/LDF forces and 118 to unidentified armed groups, with reported impacts including 843 civilians killed and 3,528 structures
destroyed over that period [314].
Since early May 2026, the AA has expanded operations beyond Rakhine and joined coordinated ambushes against junta convoys,
including attacks on 10–11 May in the Katha/Tigyaing corridor that seized weapons and destroyed vehicles [347]. Junta offensives
southwest of Mandalay (Ngazun, Myingyan, Taungtha) in early May used intensified air and drone strikes, displacing tens of thousands
and causing reported civilian deaths and injuries in attacks dated 4–7 May [337]. Resistance coalitions in central Magway reported
coordinated ambushes against advancing junta columns in Pauk Township on 4–5 May, reflecting growing operational cooperation
among NUG-aligned units, the PLA and local groups [331].
The conflict shows evolving alliances: previously tense relationships between some EAOs and NUG-aligned groups have given way to
tactical cooperation against junta advances, while intra-resistance disputes persist [331]. The junta has also engaged in informal
outreach to certain EAOs, issuing peace-talk invitations on 24 April with meetings planned in Thailand and border areas, even as it
continues offensive operations on the ground [311]. Regionally, Myanmar's junta remains diplomatically isolated in ASEAN forums—the
bloc reiterated the Five-Point Consensus and did not recognise the junta's military-organised election—while the junta has pursued
bilateral security contacts, including discussions on border security with India's naval leadership in Naypyidaw [324][340].
Displacement figures underscore the scale of the crisis: UNHCR reports more than 3.7 million people displaced inside Myanmar since
the 2021 coup, with over 62,000 fleeing to neighboring countries, and 1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers from Myanmar recorded
as of end-March 2026, shaping humanitarian coordination in Bangladesh and neighbouring states [340][361].
Peace Negotiations
Informal talks are under way between the military junta and certain ethnic armed organisations (EAOs) after the junta issued
invitations on 24 April, with preliminary meetings reported and preparations ongoing [311]. The Peace Process Steering Team (PPST)
and its member groups have received invitations and are preparing for discussions; PPST officials Colonel Khun Okkar and
spokesperson Colonel Saw Kyaw Nyunt confirmed engagement, while KNPP general secretary U Aung San Myint criticised the initiative
given continuing airstrikes [311]. Meetings are reportedly planned in Thailand and in Myanmar's Mawlamyine, Kyaingtong and Mongla
areas before any larger gathering in Naypyidaw, though independent confirmation that non-signatory or currently fighting groups
have been invited is lacking [311].
There is no evidence of a formal nationwide ceasefire. The junta's earlier "100-day peace plan," which demanded resistance groups
surrender, was rejected by major opposition organisations, and observers note that ongoing airstrikes and civilian casualties have
significantly undermined trust in any negotiation process [316][311][328]. ASEAN leaders reiterated support for the Five-Point
Consensus, acknowledged "minimal progress" on its implementation and welcomed continued humanitarian assistance via the AHA
Centre, while noting recent prisoner releases—reported variously as more than 4,000, a subsequent release of over 1,500, and other
figures cited around 5,500—as positive steps toward inclusive dialogue [324][307][321]. Several ASEAN members urged caution
against premature normalisation, and the junta publicly denounced its continued exclusion from high-level meetings as
"discriminatory" [316][324].
Conflict Outlook
The near-term outlook points to continued and potentially escalating hostilities across multiple fronts. The AA has consolidated control
across much of Rakhine State outside Sittwe, Kyaukphyu and Manaung, while the junta has intensified airstrikes and amphibious
operations; a 7 May airstrike reportedly killed three civilians and injured about 10 in Minbya and Kyauktaw, and tens of thousands have
been displaced in Kyaukphyu Township since November 2025 [328]. The junta has similarly stepped up offensives along the
Chin–Magway axis, including successive airstrikes on Mindat and forcing resistance withdrawals from several towns, reflecting a
broader campaign to retake territory lost to resistance and ethnic forces [368]. Analysts warn these concurrent offensives raise the
likelihood of broader escalation in the coming months as front lines remain tense and both sides expand operations [328][368].
UNHCR figures as of end-March 2026 illustrate the humanitarian scale of the crisis:
- 5.3 million people forcibly displaced within the Myanmar situation [365][361].
- 3.7 million internally displaced persons (IDPs) within Myanmar [365][361].
- 1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers from Myanmar [365][361].
- 1.85 million Rohingya stateless persons within the Myanmar situation [365][361].
Humanitarian consequences are likely to worsen: existing large-scale displacement and restricted access—due to insecurity, road
blockages and fuel shortages—are already impeding assistance, while documentation shows escalating and increasingly organised
sexual violence against Rohingya women and girls since early 2026 and rising attacks on health services that undermine response
capacity [365][361][326][319]. The Middle East crisis has additionally diverted international attention and resources, further
constraining assistance to Myanmar and neighbouring host countries [365].
11
On the diplomatic front, ASEAN remains split between calls for calibrated engagement and concerns about premature normalisation;
with "minimal progress" on the Five-Point Consensus and internal consensus fraying, the bloc risks losing leverage on the junta absent
measurable benchmarks [307][330][316]. Near-term prospects for a negotiated settlement are weak unless talks become genuinely
inclusive and conditioned on verifiable de-escalation; civil society and commentators have urged that any political dialogue include
the National Unity Government, ethnic armed organisations and civil society rather than engage the junta alone [336][307]. The overall
outlook reflects a high probability of continued hostilities and further displacement, a low likelihood of political settlement without
enforced and inclusive de-escalation, and growing regional humanitarian and security spill-over risks unless external actors secure
unimpeded humanitarian access and concrete benchmarks for progress [307][336][330][365].
Displacement
Displacement Overview
Key figures as of end-March 2026:
- 5.3 million people forcibly displaced within the Myanmar situation (regional total) [381][383]
- 3,708,300 internally displaced persons (IDPs) in Myanmar [432]
- 1.6 million refugees and asylumseekers from Myanmar in the region [381][383]
-1.85 million Rohingya recorded as stateless within the Myanmar situation [381][383]
UNHCR reported that displacement increased across all regions and remained severe and operationally constrained at the end of
March 2026, with restricted humanitarian access, fuel shortages and disruptions to staff movement and supply chains affecting
response delivery [381][383]. Monitoring groups documented widespread violations and localized surges in displacement: the Pyisama
Mandai Civil Surveillance Group (PMCSG) recorded 1,122 human rights violations across Yangon, Sagaing, Magway and Naypyidaw for
the period early March 2025–end February 2026, with Sagaing the most affected region—violence in these areas corresponds with
continued displacement pressures [411].
Acute events in May 2026 included junta forces burning an IDP camp in Hway Du Lar, Hpruso Township (Karenni State) on 8 May,
forcing more than 1,000 people to flee into surrounding forests and constraining humanitarian access as of 11 May [401]. Crossborder
and maritime movements remain significant: UNHCR reported approximately 150,000 Rohingya arrivals in Bangladesh since December
2024, and since early January 2026 at least four boats carrying 111 Rohingya returned from Bangladesh to Sittwe; recent maritime
incidents include a capsized boat with about 120 passengers and another intercepted vessel with nearly 480 people detained in Sittwe
[383]. UNHCR confirmed on 17 April 2026 that nearly 900 Rohingya refugees were reported dead or missing at sea in 2025 — the
deadliest year on record for maritime movements in South and Southeast Asia, with one in seven of the more than 6,500 who
attempted crossings reported missing or dead; the trend continued into 2026 with more than 2,800 Rohingya undertaking dangerous
sea journeys between January and 13 April 2026, including a major Andaman Sea incident around 26 March in which an estimated 250
people went missing [433][392]. Internal displacement patterns include sheltering in forests and repeated secondary movements
following attacks on camps and restrictions on movement, impeding durable solutions and access to assistance [401].
Living Conditions of the Displaced Population
UNHCR and partners reported delivering core relief items to over 10,700 people, shelter assistance to nearly 280 people and
multipurpose assistance to around 120 people by endMarch 2026 [383]. On 8 May 2026, junta forces torched the Hway Du Lar IDP
camp in Hpruso Township (Karenni State), destroying over half of its 68 houses and forcing more than 1,000 residents to flee into
surrounding forests; food needs were reported as very high, with roads still blocked and access for aid remaining constrained as of 11
May [401]. Karenni civil society organisations estimated that nearly 300,000 of an estimated 400,000 people in Karenni State have
been displaced and urgently need food and medicine [401].
On 9 May, security forces arrested six displaced Rakhine civilians in Sittwe during raids on a monastery, with one detainee reported to
have died from severe torture in detention, illustrating acute protection risks for IDPs sheltering in religious sites [424]. Monitoring by
BROUK documents rising recruitment, detention and sexual violence against Rohingya women and girls in northern Rakhine between
May 2024 and April 2026, including recruitment raids, incommunicado detention and cases of sexual violence amid severe
humanitarian restrictions and widespread underreporting [392]. Documented protection risks in northern Rakhine State also include
survival sex in constrained camps driven by severe deprivation, further undermining access to justice and longterm well-being for
Rohingya communities [392][421].
The Karen Peace Support Network and local partners report that aid channeled through militaryaffiliated administrative bodies is
often diverted or not delivered to displaced communities, with estimates indicating only around 1–10 percent of assistance reaches
intended recipients; they urge donors to bypass junta channels and provide direct support to borderbased organisations [396].
Drivers of Displacement
Ongoing conflict since the 2021 coup — including military airstrikes, arbitrary detention, village burnings and the imposition of martial
law — has driven large-scale internal displacement and cross-border flows [415]. The Arakan Army's expanded territorial control in
Rakhine since 2023, and associated abuses including forced recruitment, incommunicado detention, threats and incidents of sexual
violence, have been linked to intensified displacement and protection violations affecting Rohingya and other civilians [392].
Documented abuses by the Arakan Army and other actors — including detention, forced recruitment, sexual violence and attacks
during displacement — have contributed to repeated and protracted movements, particularly in Rakhine State [392][421].
12
In Rakhine State, heavy fighting and junta airstrikes have displaced tens of thousands in Kyaukphyu Township since November 2025,
with aid groups warning of worsening conditions — including shortages of food, clean water and clothing across nearly 70 villages —
after an airstrike on 7 May reportedly killed three civilians [428].
Economic drivers — notably rising fuel and energy costs — have increased transport and input costs (fuel, fertilizer), undermining
livelihoods, agricultural production and food security and thereby contributing to displacement pressures [383][393][409].
Humanitarian access and delivery have been further constrained by fuel shortages, movement controls, road blockages and
heightened insecurity, disrupting staff movement, supply chains and programme delivery [383][381].
Severe movement restrictions, stigma and fear of retaliation mean sexual and other protection abuses are likely underreported,
complicating protection responses and impeding durable returns or local integration for many displaced people [392][383]. Local civil
society coordination networks and partners report rising displacement and access challenges in Mon, Karen and Tanintharyi regions
and are calling for direct aid delivery that bypasses junta-controlled channels to reach displaced communities [396].
Integration
Integration prospects for displaced populations remain severely constrained. UNHCR reported persistent underfunding and limited
livelihood opportunities in host settings, notably in Bangladesh camps, while conditions inside Myanmar continue to preclude safe and
voluntary return [381][383]. Local networks and civil society groups have urged donors to bypass juntacontrolled channels and fund
organizations that can reach displaced communities directly, warning that aid routed through regime structures reaches only a tiny
share of intended recipients — estimates cited in reports range from around 1% to 10% — and naming Mon, Karen, Tanintharyi and Bago
as priority areas for direct support [396].
Regional and diplomatic commentary has called on ASEAN and partners to insist on unhindered humanitarian access through
channels that do not place aid solely under junta control, and to engage local civil society and crossborder actors to improve reach
and protection outcomes [413]. Documented protection risks — including recruitment raids, detention and incidents of sexual violence
reported in January–February 2026 — have further undermined access to justice, social cohesion and longterm integration prospects
for Rohingya communities [392][421].
Humanitarian Access
Humanitarian Access Overview
Humanitarian access in Myanmar remains highly constrained by active conflict, movement controls, and fuel shortages, which disrupt
road and supply-chain operations, staff movement, and programme delivery, while limiting cross-border and maritime lifelines
[442][452]. UNHCR and partners maintain in-country engagement across Rakhine, Karenni/Hpruso, Mon, Karen, Tanintharyi, and Bago
areas, with regional hubs and reception/support activities operating in Bangladesh, Indonesia (Aceh, North Sumatra, Riau), Malaysia,
and Thailand [442][452][471][450]. Key delivery actors include UNHCR and other UN agencies, local civil society and ethnic service
providers such as the Karen Peace Support Network, local coordination and rescue teams such as CTER-Karenni, ASEAN/AHA Centre
mechanisms, and international donors [442][450][471][446][461]. Humanitarian assistance is delivered by road, cross-border
operations, and maritime disembarkations, but road blockages, insecurity, and denial of territory frequently prevent safe access to
affected communities [452][471][442]. UNHCR estimated 5.3 million people forcibly displaced within the Myanmar situation and 3.7
million IDPs as of end-March 2026, underscoring large unmet needs amid constrained access [442][452]. Donor funding is commonly
routed through embassies and junta-affiliated ministries — a practice local networks say diverts most assistance away from IDP
communities — prompting calls to channel funds directly to border-based NGOs and CSOs, with local networks reporting that only a
small fraction of internationally-channelled assistance reaches front-line IDP areas [450][442].
Barriers to Humanitarian Access
Active insecurity and repeated attacks on health services have severely constrained aid delivery across Myanmar; Insecurity Insight
recorded 1,918 attacks on health care, with 173 health workers killed, 909 arrested, and over 497 health facilities damaged through 14
April 2026 [455]. Armed operations have directly blocked relief: junta troops burned an IDP camp in Hpruso Township on 8 May 2026,
displacing over 1,000 people and leaving roads blocked so humanitarian deliveries could not reach those sheltering in forests as of 11
May 2026 [471]. Fuel shortages during January–March 2026 disrupted staff movement, supply chains, and programme delivery, further
limiting operational access [442]. Movement controls and tightened checkpoints by both state and non-state actors have curtailed
civilian mobility and cross-border assistance flows: the Arakan Army increased checkpoints and travel restrictions along the
Maungdaw–Bangladesh frontier, requiring passes through AA-run checkpoints for essential travel and emergency medical cases
[468], while inspections and seizures at MNDAA-controlled border checkpoints since 4 May 2026 have effectively halted freight on
major trade routes in northern Shan State [481]. Border and policy measures further constrain access: Bangladesh's border remained
officially closed in Q1 2026, with over 850 people reported denied entry while UNHCR conducted biometric registration of nearly 5,500
new arrivals — bringing approximately 147,000 people identified since 2025 — limiting avenues for safe refuge and formal assistance
[452]. Political and bureaucratic obstacles compound these constraints, with the Karen Peace Support Network calling on donors to
bypass junta-controlled channels and fund local and cross-border organizations directly [450]. Identity-based restrictions, limited
communication, and constrained access to justice — particularly affecting Rohingya and other marginalised groups under both
Arakan Army and military influence — further limit protection monitoring, reporting, and safe access to assistance [440][451].
Humanitarian Access Gaps
Fighting and airstrikes in Mon, Karen, Tanintharyi, and Bago regions have driven rising displacement and constrained aid delivery, with
the Karen Peace Support Network urging donors to fund only NGOs and CSOs that can directly reach displaced people in those areas
amid declining donor support and growing healthcare needs [450].

Rohingya communities in northern Rakhine State under Arakan Army control face severe movement restrictions, arbitrary detention,
forced displacement, sexual violence, and broad humanitarian restrictions with little accountability; an estimated approximately
600,000 Rohingya remain in Myanmar following the 2017 exodus [440]. Clashes in Kyaukphyu Township and surrounding areas have
displaced tens of thousands since November 2025, with aid groups warning of acute shortages of food, safe water, and clothing

Information, Communication and AAP
Information Landscape
The information environment in Myanmar is deteriorating, with the military junta revoking the publishing licenses of Myaelatt Athan,
Red News Agency, and Asia Citizens News Agency on 11 May 2026, narrowing independent media and reducing public access to
impartial reporting — a move criticised by the Independent Press Council of Myanmar [537]. Key operational figures for the response
include 1,194,123 Rohingya refugees (248,169 families) in Bangladesh as of April 2026 [523], and a total of 5.3 million forcibly displaced
within the Myanmar situation as of end-March 2026, comprising 3.7 million internally displaced persons and 1.6 million refugees and
asylum-seekers [496]. UNHCR and partners supported over 10,700 people inside Myanmar with core relief items, shelter, and
multipurpose assistance by end-March from January 2026 [519].
On the coordination side, UNHCR introduced a UN-led Refugee Coordination Platform in Bangladesh in January 2026 to replace the
ISCG, coordinating over 150 UN agencies and NGOs to improve leadership and data-sharing for the response [519]. However, cluster
monitoring and information-use weaknesses persist: a Global Health Cluster evaluation found challenges securing timely and
consistent partner data in Myanmar, uneven uptake of 3W/4W tools, and limited use of monitoring data and community feedback in
decision-making and planning [487]. Local civil-society coordination also reports severe breakdowns in formal aid channels, with the
Karen Peace Support Network urging donors to bypass the junta and fund 22 local organizations working in Mon, Karen, Tanintharyi,
and Bago regions, arguing that corruption has left only approximately 1–10% of aid reaching intended displaced communities [495].
International displacement analysis further stresses the need for resilient national and humanitarian data systems and for
triangulation and partner validation to maintain displacement information when operational conditions change [489].
Communication Channels
Humanitarian and affected populations exchange information via independent media, UN and cluster coordination platforms, local
NGOs and cross-border service networks, and secure digital channels used for evidence reporting and outreach
[537][519][495][528][514]. The Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar has been soliciting first-hand information through
encrypted channels — specifically Signal with disappearing messages and secure email via ProtonMail — illustrating active use of
secure digital reporting as of 12 May 2026 [514][513]. Regional and local responders, including ethnic service providers and
cross-border groups, are identified as essential channels to reach communities beyond junta-controlled areas [528][495].
ASEAN and the AHA Centre continue to facilitate humanitarian assistance under the Five-Point Consensus (Point 4), though regional
coordination remains constrained by security and political limitations on access [490][539]. Operational constraints — including fuel
shortages, road blockages, movement controls, and insecurity — have disrupted staff movement, supply chains, and programme
delivery, reducing the reliability and timeliness of needs assessments and increasing dependence on local NGOs for data
[496][519][487]. The military's revocation of publishing licences on 11 May 2026 has further constricted independent media outlets,
compounding information gaps and heightening risks of misinformation amid press restrictions and hostile online narratives
[537][521].
Barriers to Information
Humanitarian access and data collection in conflict-affected areas are severely constrained. Fuel shortages, road blockages,
movement controls, and insecurity have disrupted staff movement, supply chains, and programme delivery throughout the
January–March 2026 reporting period [496][519]. Obtaining reliable, timely information often depends on engagement with local
NGOs operating in inaccessible areas, undermining comprehensive national assessments and triangulation [487].
Aid delivery and information flows are also politicized and opaque. Local networks report that assistance routed through
junta-controlled ministries is heavily diverted or delayed, and the targeting of health care infrastructure — with 83 recorded incidents
through end-April 2026, many against facilities — has further disrupted access to services and information [495][534][507]. Detention
and prolonged confinement of Rohingya (approximately 500 held in Thai government facilities as of end-March 2026) and recurrent
risky maritime movements complicate communication and protection for affected populations [519]. Online platforms have amplified
hostile narratives and misinformation — Facebook was cited for amplifying anti-Rohingya hate speech that preceded violence — and
widespread dehumanising language increases risks for targeted communities [521]. Restrictions on movement, communication, and
access to justice under armed actors further impede reporting and accountability, including for sexual violence [507].
14
Misinformation and Rumors
Misinformation and hate speech remain significant drivers of communal division in Myanmar, with studies documenting social media
— particularly Facebook — amplifying dehumanising narratives that contributed to large-scale violence prior to 2017 [521]. Restricted
access and data gaps mean humanitarian information often depends on local NGOs and partners, increasing uneven reporting and
the risk of rumours where independent verification is limited, according to the Global Health Cluster evaluation [487]. UNHCR's regional
updates from May 2026 note severe access constraints and heavy reliance on partners to collect and verify information, underscoring
fragile information environments across the response [523].
Low trust in military-aligned official channels is widespread: local journalists and community-based organisations are often more
trusted sources on the ground, and civil society networks such as the Karen Peace Support Network have called on donors to bypass
junta channels and fund border-based NGOs directly [524][495]. Rumours about aid diversion have already influenced local
advocacy and donor engagement, reinforcing these trust deficits [495]. To counter misinformation and strengthen evidence, actors
such as the Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar promote secure and confidential reporting channels for eyewitness
information, while coordination bodies emphasise improved partner engagement and needs-assessment practices to raise data
quality [514][487].
Accountability Needs and Transparency
Significant accountability gaps persist across the Myanmar response. UNHCR and partners ran legal awareness sessions and
workshops that included complaints and feedback mechanisms by end-March 2026, but access and programme delivery remained
constrained by insecurity, road blockages, movement controls, and fuel shortages throughout the January–March 2026 period
[519][496]. The Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar announced ongoing investigations into attacks affecting children
and called for secure reporting of airstrike and related evidence via Signal and ProtonMail in a 12 May 2026 statement [513].
Civil society briefings have documented additional accountability gaps: BROUK reported escalating sexual violence against Rohingya
under Arakan Army control, stated there is no public evidence of credible Arakan Army investigations, and urged strengthened
monitoring, documentation, and greater support for Rohingya-led organisations through April 2026 [507][499]. Local networks such as
the Karen Peace Support Network urged donors to bypass military-controlled channels and deliver aid directly to border-based
organizations, estimating that only approximately 1–10% of assistance reaches displaced people in some regions due to corruption
and regime routing [495]. Commentators and analysts at the May 2026 ASEAN discussions warned that routing aid solely through
regime-approved structures risks strengthening those actors, recommending engagement with local civil society and cross-border
networks instead [528]. Key accountability gaps identified across recent reporting include opaque routing of donor funds through
junta structures, weak or fragmented complaints and feedback mechanisms, underrepresentation and under-resourcing of
Rohingya-led monitoring and protection actors, and limited credible investigative or justice processes for sexual and other serious
abuses [496][495][528][487][499][507].
Legal and Policy
Legal and Policy Framework
ASEAN continues to treat the Five-Point Consensus (5PC) as its primary framework for engaging Myanmar, noting "minimal progress"
at the 7–8 May 2026 summit and pursuing terms of reference for a long-term special envoy to advance implementation
[561][560][577]. The bloc is also working to secure verified access to Aung San Suu Kyi and has extended the ASEAN Observer Team's
mandate through end-July to monitor ceasefires and confidence-building measures [561][557][560]. Myanmar's foreign ministry has
publicly complained of "discriminatory measures" and exclusion from ASEAN forums in response to these restrictions [576][558]. ASEAN
bodies, including the AHA Centre, have delivered humanitarian assistance to Myanmar, and regional workplans include initiatives to
mainstream International Humanitarian Law (IHL) and strengthen emergency logistics and civil–military coordination [550][588][560].
The UN Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar (IIMM) reported on 12 May 2026 that junta airstrikes have killed and injured
children across multiple regions, that such strikes are being investigated as possible war crimes or crimes against humanity, and that
the Mechanism is collecting chain-of-command evidence to identify perpetrators [570][569]. Grassroots monitors recorded
widespread rights abuses: the Pyisama Mandai Civil Surveillance Group (PMCSG) reported 1,122 human rights violations between early
March 2025 and end-February 2026, with Sagaing region the most affected and the junta responsible for the majority of incidents
[573].
Humanitarian and rights actors have urged international accountability measures — including UN Security Council engagement,
enforcement of International Court of Justice provisional measures, targeted sanctions, and strengthened mechanisms to address
atrocity crimes such as conflict-related sexual violence in Rakhine and elsewhere [551][579]. Local civil-society networks and ethnic
service providers, including the Karen Peace Support Network, are calling for direct delivery of international assistance to border and
community-based actors to avoid aid being routed through junta-controlled administrative channels [575].
Humanitarian Impact of Legislation
Humanitarian access in Myanmar remained heavily constrained by insecurity, road blockages, movement controls, fuel shortages,
and communications restrictions that disrupted staff movement, supply chains, and programme delivery [553][552][551]. Severe
access restrictions and communications controls imposed by the military also limit independent monitoring and corroboration of
abuses, constraining accountability and protection responses for affected communities [551].
International assistance has routinely been routed through embassies and transferred to ministries under the military junta — a
process the Karen Peace Support Network (KPSN) says has led to severe diversion and corruption, with reported aid reach as low as
1–10 percent of displaced communities — prompting calls to channel aid directly to local organisations [575].

Analysts have warned that routing aid solely through regime-approved structures risks strengthening the actors responsible for
civilian suffering, and have urged ASEAN and donors to insist on unhindered access and alternative delivery channels, including

Movement and trade restrictions imposed at checkpoints — including inspections, seizures of Chinese-labelled goods, and ID checks —

Monitoring organisations report systematic attacks on healthcare since the 2021 coup — including 1,918 attacks on healthcare facilities,

Regionally, host-state policy developments affect refugee rights and documentation: Malaysia launched the Refugee Registration
Document (DPP) system on 1 January 2026 and is engaging with UNHCR on registration, documentation, and pathways to legal
employment and education for refugees from Myanmar [552].
International Agreements Impacting the Crisis
ASEAN reaffirmed the Five-Point Consensus (5PC) as its primary framework for addressing Myanmar following the 48th Leaders'
Meeting in Cebu in early May 2026 [561][560][577][566]. Member states have debated a policy of "calibrated engagement"
conditioned on measurable steps — reduction in violence, expanded humanitarian access, release of political prisoners, and inclusive
dialogue — while warning against political normalisation without accountability [566][554][561][577].
UNHCR reported the following displacement figures as of end-March 2026:
- 5.3 million people forcibly displaced within the Myanmar situation [553][552]
- 3.7 million internally displaced persons in Myanmar [553][552]
- 1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers from Myanmar regionally [552][553]
Human rights and accountability actors have urged activation of UN Security Council measures, support for International Court of
Justice proceedings, expansion of ICC and universal-jurisdiction avenues, and coordinated targeted sanctions — including on arms
transfers, aviation fuel, and revenue streams — against perpetrators from both the Burmese military and the Arakan Army [551][579].
Donors and states were specifically urged to restore and increase humanitarian funding for Rohingya-affected populations and to
support Rohingya-led monitoring, documentation, and survivor-centred responses [551][579].
Limitations of Current Policies
Humanitarian access across Myanmar remains severely restricted by insecurity, road blockages, movement controls, and fuel
shortages, stretching response capacity and protection systems for an estimated 5.3 million forcibly displaced people as of
end-March 2026 [552][553]. Donor diversion and funding shortfalls — including resource strains linked to the Middle East crisis and
abrupt cuts to US foreign aid in 2025 — have weakened protection services, legal support, and accountability mechanisms relied upon
to document abuses and assist affected populations [552][553][574][584].
Aid pipelines routed through regime-approved structures have been undermined by corruption and diversion, with only a small
proportion of aid reported to reach displaced communities. The Karen Peace Support Network and partners have urged donors to
bypass the junta and deliver assistance directly to border-based organisations [575]. Regional diplomatic moves toward "calibrated
engagement" with the junta risk contradicting international protection objectives by normalising the regime and entrenching channels
that place aid under junta control, unless access is explicitly secured through non-junta, civil-society, and cross-border channels
[554][563][566].
Accountability gaps persist for atrocity-level crimes against Rohingya and other communities, with UN mechanisms documenting
ongoing sexual and other serious violations and calling for urgent international measures including ICJ/ICC avenues and targeted
sanctions [551][579]. The UN Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar is investigating repeated airstrikes that have killed
and injured children, illustrating how current policy and enforcement gaps are failing to protect civilians and generate accountability
for potential war crimes [569][570].

Sectoral Analysis
Food Security and Livelihoods
Access to Food and Market Functionality
Supply vulnerabilities have intensified since mid-May 2026 as higher fuel and shipping costs linked to disruptions in the Strait of
Hormuz have driven up fertilizer prices and raised input and transport costs, threatening planting and rice yields and prompting some
farmers to reduce cultivated area; field reporting described ploughing costs rising from about $24 to $476 and households facing
growing difficulty producing or selling rice [610][617]. Myanmar depends on imports for roughly 90% of its fuel and almost all of its
fertilizer, and farmers in Rakhine report being unable to obtain fuel to plough or fertilizer ahead of the monsoon paddy season, with
some abandoning land because costs and supply blockages make cultivation unviable [610]. Staple food prices have risen sharply,
with WFP reporting broad price increases of around 22% for basic staples such as rice, salt and pulses in some areas and localized
spikes of 30–50% where fuel availability is very limited, eroding purchasing power and driving negative coping such as meal reduction
[610][617]. Control of border trade routes in northern Shan State and related trade disruptions have constrained cross-border imports
of food products, agricultural machinery and inputs, altering local market supplies in key trading hubs such as Muse and other
northern border gates [650]. Fuel shortages, conservation measures and security-related road blockages have disrupted staff
movement, supply chains and programme delivery, and humanitarian access remains restricted by insecurity and movement
controls [609]. As of end-March 2026, an estimated 3.7 million people were internally displaced and 5.3 million forcibly displaced within
the Myanmar situation, increasing demand for food assistance amid significant delivery constraints [614][609][608].
Coping Mechanisms
Households in Myanmar are cutting food consumption — many report eating half a meal or predominantly rice and vegetables — and
caregivers are unable to afford infant formula or diverse diets as staple food prices have jumped approximately 22% since the onset of
the Iran war, intensifying food insecurity [610]. WFP warned of "alarming levels of food insecurity" in Rakhine and cautioned that
continued disruptions to assistance could produce famine-like conditions [610]. Displaced people are resorting to negative survival
strategies, including begging and prioritizing limited cash for food over other needs, leaving households unable to cover hygiene or
education expenses [610]. Monitoring by BROUK documents that severe deprivation in northern Rakhine and displacement settings is
driving some Rohingya women into survival sex and increasing risks of trafficking and sexual exploitation along maritime routes [626].
Continued maritime movements of Rohingya and recent arrivals in regional camps — UNHCR notes approximately 147,000 arrivals in
Bangladesh since 2024 and ongoing boat arrivals in January–March 2026 — reflect migration as a coping or survival strategy for some
communities [614][622][658]. Local coordination networks (KPSN) report rising commodity prices and declining donor support, urging
donors to fund organizations that can deliver aid directly to displaced communities to avoid diversion via the junta [608].
Livelihood Disruption
Renewed offensives and air/drone strikes in Myanmar's central Dry Zone (Ngazun, Myingyan and Taungtha) have forced tens of
thousands to flee, with dozens of villages burned in Myingyan and Taung Thar in April, destroying homes and productive assets and
disrupting local markets and livelihoods [656][657]. The Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar highlighted continued air
campaigns that have killed children and civilians and documented hundreds of airstrikes since 2021, contributing to displacement and
reductions in local labour capacity [638][663]. UNHCR reports more than 3.5 million people displaced across Myanmar since the 2021
coup and 1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers regionally as of end-March 2026, with persistent underfunding and limited livelihood
opportunities in Bangladesh further constrained by approximately 147,000 Rohingya arrivals since 2024, undermining household
self-reliance [658][614][609]. Movement restrictions, blockades and constrained humanitarian access — particularly in Rakhine —
have driven economic deprivation, reducing regular income opportunities for vulnerable households [626]. Extractive livelihoods such
as gem mining remain an important income source but operate in contested, insecure areas where fighting and changing control
interrupt incomes and can channel revenues to armed actors, undermining stable employment in mining communities [659].
Impact on Agricultural Activities and Production
Following the Middle East conflict and Strait of Hormuz disruptions that began in late February 2026, international fuel and fertilizer
markets tightened and prices rose sharply, keeping urea, DAP and sulfur above pre-conflict levels and raising costs for agricultural
inputs worldwide [617]. Border and internal trade disruptions — including junta actions affecting China–northern Shan State routes —
have constrained imports of food products and agricultural machinery and disrupted exports of crops such as watermelon, sugarcane
and corn, reducing access to inputs and market outlets for producers [650]. Ongoing fighting, airstrikes and blockades in Rakhine,
Mon, Karen and Tanintharyi regions continue to drive rural displacement and limit humanitarian and commercial access, undermining
labour availability, input delivery and prospects for upcoming planting and harvest cycles [610][608][626]. These combined pressures
risk lower plantings and yields in the 2026 monsoon season and heightened losses of rural livelihoods and food production if supply,
fuel and access constraints persist [610][617].
Food and Livelihood Needs and Current Response
UNHCR's 2026 appeal for the Myanmar response was USD 328.5 million, with funding at only 26% by year-end, limiting scale-up of
life-saving assistance including food and livelihoods support [614]. The Joint Response Plan prioritised life-saving interventions — food,
health, WASH, education and protection — while partners continued joint assessments and response planning, including shelter and
WASH assessments in Kachin displacement sites to inform relocation planning [609]. By end-March 2026, UNHCR and partners had
reached approximately 10,700 people with core relief items, around 280 with shelter assistance and approximately 120 with
multi-purpose assistance, but underfunding and access limits leave needs far unmet [614][609].

UNHCR coordinates with 15 national and nine international partners, leads the Protection and Shelter/Land/Site clusters and engages

Education
Learning Environment and Facility Conditions
The UN Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar (IIMM) reported on 12 May 2026 that the military's air campaign continues
to strike civilian sites including schools, recalling the 12 May 2025 Oe Htein Kwin school attack that killed more than 20 students
[671][674]. An air attack on 1 May 2026 in Khawpuichhip village, Falam Township, Chin State, killed at least five children (later updates
cited six children and one adult), showing recent strikes on places where children gather [674][671]. Based on available data — likely
an underestimate — the IIMM states that children were killed or injured in at least 640 airstrikes between February 2021 and 2025 [671].
Repeated airstrikes and bombardments have affected schools and other education-related facilities across multiple regions,
including strikes in Mandalay, Bago and Rakhine in March–April 2026, two schools in Rakhine in September 2025, and a monastery
sheltering internally displaced people in Sagaing in July 2025 [671][674]. Local reporting also describes strikes that exploded between
schools and nearby religious sites in Me Taw village, Shwebo Township, causing civilian injuries and reinforcing that the proximity of
strikes prevents safe school attendance in affected areas [694]. Fighting and shifting control in northern Sagaing have left towns
heavily damaged and largely abandoned, and although regime employees and civil servants arrived to reopen government offices
and schools in recaptured towns, schooling remained constrained with plans to open only in select localities such as Na Bar and
Mawlu rather than in Indaw as of 10–11 May 2026 [675]. The IIMM is prioritizing investigations of airstrikes that appear to directly target
civilians in locations where children are present, noting such strikes may amount to war crimes or crimes against humanity, thereby
gravely undermining the safety and functionality of education infrastructure [671].
Access to Education and Barriers
As of end-March 2026, UNHCR estimated some 3.7 million people internally displaced in Myanmar, a scale of displacement that has
severely disrupted enrolment and attendance across large parts of the country [677]. Ongoing military airstrikes represent the most
direct barrier to school attendance: the IIMM's documentation of at least 640 child casualties across airstrikes since February 2021,
combined with continued strikes on schools and civilian gathering places, deters enrolment and contributes to dropouts in
conflict-affected areas [671][674]. Beyond physical attacks, UNHCR and partners have warned that insecurity, road blockages,
movement controls and fuel shortages have disrupted staff movement, supply chains and programme delivery, further limiting access
to education and related services for displaced and host communities [677][670]. The IIMM has prioritised investigation of airstrikes
affecting children and is collecting evidence to identify perpetrators, underscoring the persistent protection risks that continue to deter
safe school attendance [671].
Education Needs and Current Response
The scale of educational disruption in Myanmar demands a multifaceted response spanning safe learning spaces, trained teachers,
learning materials, psychosocial support, and continuity of education for millions of displaced children. Humanitarian access and
programme delivery face severe operational constraints — including insecurity, road blockages, movement controls and fuel
shortages — limiting the ability to restore safe, continuous schooling and education services inside the country [677]. The restarting of
schooling only in limited localities, as illustrated by indications that schools in the Indaw area would open only in Na Bar and Mawlu,
further underscores the need for targeted support to reopen schools safely and expand temporary learning spaces where needed
[675]. UNHCR reported that education is included among life-saving interventions in regional and refugee responses, and that UNHCR
in the Myanmar situation coordinates with 15 national and nine international partners while leading protection and shelter/site
clusters, indicating an active but access-constrained education and protection coordination role [670][677]. At the local level, the
Karen Peace Support Network (KPSN) coordinates 22 organisations providing assistance — including education services for displaced
communities in Mon, Karen, Tanintharyi and Bago regions — and is calling for direct donor support to organisations that can reach
displaced people without routing aid through junta channels due to diversion risks [681]. UNHCR and partners' regional Joint Response
planning has prioritised education access for displaced and refugee populations, but restrictions on access and funding shortfalls
remain key gaps to scaling safe, inclusive learning, psychosocial support and continuity of education for affected children [670][677].

programme delivery while humanitarian access remained restricted by insecurity [714][713]. Movement controls imposed by non-state

Disease Outbreaks
A formative evaluation of the Global Health Cluster found that country-level health information in Myanmar was strong on cyclone
response but perceived as narrowly focused on cholera and weak on immunization and broader disease surveillance, limiting
comprehensive outbreak preparedness and planning [712]. Vaccination initiatives have been subject to violent attacks, undermining
campaigns and outbreak control efforts [715]. UNHCR and partners prioritized life-saving health and WASH interventions, but persistent
underfunding and restricted humanitarian access continued to limit vaccination coverage and broader outbreak response capacity
[713][714]. The mass displacement of an estimated 3.7 million internally displaced people as of end-March 2026 has increased health
and WASH needs and further disrupted service delivery, compounding risks of disease transmission in affected communities [713][714].
Health System Challenges
Myanmar's health system faces compounding systemic challenges driven by conflict, displacement, and chronic underfunding.
Attacks on health care have severely undermined service delivery, with Insecurity Insight documenting 83 incidents affecting health
care up to end-April 2026, including approximately 70 directed against facilities [715][733]. Airstrikes and bombardments have hit
hospitals and other civilian sites, with the IIMM reporting multiple strikes and ongoing investigations into attacks on schools, hospitals,
and places of worship, indicating widespread infrastructure damage and threats to health services [729]. Fuel shortages and
conservation measures have disrupted staff movement, supply chains, and programme delivery, while road blockages and
movement controls have restricted humanitarian access to conflict-affected areas [714]. Market and supply disruptions linked to fuel
shortages have increased the cost and reduced availability of medicines and basic items, further constraining health service delivery
and patient access [747][714]. Local coordination networks warn that declining donor support, rising commodity prices, and corruption
in aid channels mean only a small fraction of assistance reaches displaced people, worsening healthcare shortages and access for
IDPs in Mon, Karen, Tanintharyi, and Bago regions [716]. Humanitarian coordination analysis notes that access to conflict-affected
areas is difficult and that data and needs assessments depend heavily on engagement with local NGOs operating in those areas,
limiting planning and response for health services [712]. Health cluster partners have prioritized services for internally displaced and
other vulnerable populations, but limited access, data gaps, and funding shortfalls continue to impede consistent delivery of essential
health services across conflict-affected areas [712][714].
Mental Health and Psychosocial Support (MHPSS)
UNHCR and Protection/Shelter cluster partners in Myanmar identified mental health and psychosocial support (MHPSS) as a priority
need and included it in protection planning during the January–March 2026 reporting period [713][714]. However, access constraints
and reliance on local NGOs limit needs assessments and service delivery in conflict-affected areas [712]. Children exposed to repeated
airstrikes are reported to suffer deep and lasting psychological harm, with the IIMM highlighting recent child fatalities and ongoing
investigations as of 12 May 2026 [730][724]. Documented sexual violence against Rohingya women and girls between May 2024 and
April 2026—with significant underreporting—has increased the need for specialized MHPSS and survivor-focused protection services
[720][731]. Local coordination networks and 22 partner organisations are delivering frontline assistance, including health and support
for women and children, and have urged donors to channel funding directly to actors that can reach displaced communities beyond
junta-controlled channels [716]. Cuts to funding and diverted global attention have led some organisations to suspend legal and
psychological support, further weakening MHPSS delivery [743]. Overall, while MHPSS is included in planning and prioritization, its
integration into the broader response is constrained by access limitations, security threats to health workers and facilities, and funding
shortfalls [713][712][715][743].
Health Needs and Current Response
Airstrikes and widespread violence are driving urgent needs for trauma and emergency care, with recent incidents including a May
2026 air attack that killed at least five children in Chin State and other strikes across Mandalay, Bago, and Rakhine in March–April 2026
under active investigation by the IIMM [730]. A junta attack burned an IDP camp in Hpruso (Karenni State) in early May 2026, leaving
more than 1,000 people sheltering in forests with high unmet food and health needs while humanitarian access remained limited [741].
Key figures summarizing the scale of need include:
- 3.7 million internally displaced people in Myanmar as of end-March 2026 [714].
- 1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers from Myanmar as of end-March 2026 [714].
- UNHCR's regional appeal for Myanmar in 2026 was only approximately 26% funded by end-March 2026, against a total appeal of
USD 328.5 million [713].
Humanitarian actors, including UN agencies, continue to prioritize life-saving health interventions alongside WASH and protection, with
health cluster partners reporting outreach to hard-to-reach areas through mobile clinics where access allows [713][712]. Major gaps
persist, including severe funding shortfalls limiting scale-up of health services and supply procurement [713], widespread destruction
and insecurity impeding facility-based care and staff safety [715], and operational constraints on aid delivery routes. Local networks
and ethnic service providers are critical for access, while groups such as KPSN have urged donors to channel assistance directly to
local NGOs and border-based actors rather than through junta-controlled systems due to diversion and corruption [716].

Nutrition
Availability and Access to Nutrition Services
Humanitarian access, supply chains, and programme delivery across Myanmar were disrupted by insecurity, road blockages,
movement controls, and fuel shortages during January–March 2026 [771]. Attacks on health care have heavily undermined service
capacity: Insecurity Insight recorded 1,918 attacks on health care in Myanmar between February 2021 and 14 April 2026, with 173 health

[791]. Airstrikes and bombardments through 2025 and into May 2026 — including intensive strikes documented by the Independent
Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar and a playground strike in Chin State on 1 May 2026 — have damaged hospitals and clinics and
killed or injured children, further reducing availability of maternal, child health, and nutrition services [781][779][784]. Collectively,
damaged facilities, loss and arrest of health staff, disrupted supply chains, and restricted access have diminished capacity for
malnutrition screening, supplementary feeding, and maternal/infant nutrition support across affected areas [791][771][769].
Renewed displacement — an estimated 3.7 million IDPs in Myanmar as of end-March 2026 — and large Rohingya movements into
camps (about 147,000 arrivals since 2024) have increased needs and stretched essential services in camps and host communities
[771][773]. The health cluster has prioritized vulnerable internally displaced populations and maternal/child services, but access

Nutrition Needs and Current Response
WFP warned of "alarming levels of food insecurity" in Rakhine and estimated some 12.5 million people facing hunger in Myanmar, with
staple prices (rice, salt, pulses) up about 22% and localized fuel-related price spikes of 30–50%, while reports describe mothers unable
to produce breastmilk and unable to afford formula in affected areas [769]. A briefing documenting abuses affecting Rohingya
communities (May 2024–April 2026) links severe food insecurity to exploitation and survival coping strategies, and calls for increased
humanitarian funding and cross-border access to reach Rohingya and other crisis-affected communities in Rakhine [775]. Border and
supply disruptions linked to MNDAA control of key China–northern Shan trade routes and changing checkpoints have further affected
food imports and local markets, aggravating supply-chain constraints for food and nutrition commodities [793].
Ongoing nutrition interventions face significant obstacles. Funding constraints, rising commodity prices, and corruption in channels
that route assistance through junta structures continue to undermine response capacity [776][771]. Limited healthcare staff trained in
nutrition response and restricted humanitarian access further hamper the delivery of therapeutic feeding programmes and
community-based nutrition initiatives across conflict-affected areas [771].

Shelter and Non-Food Items (NFI)
Damage to Housing and Shelter Needs
As of endMarch 2026, an estimated 3.7 million people were internally displaced in Myanmar, with the broader Myanmar situation
accounting for 5.3 million forcibly displaced [876]. A powerful shallow 7.7 magnitude earthquake on 28 March struck the
Sagaing–Mandalay border region, triggering around 327,000 displacements and destroying nearly 12,000 homes and buildings, with
about 280,000 people remaining displaced by yearend (approximately 264,000 living with relatives and 15,000 in temporary
evacuation centres) [874]. Military airstrikes and shelling have damaged and destroyed civilian housing and communal infrastructure,
with the Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar documenting attacks on schools, hospitals, places of worship and a
monastery sheltering IDPs, and reporting children killed or injured in strikes in March–May 2026 [883][879]. Grassroots monitoring
documented 3,528 structures—including homes and religious buildings—destroyed over one year, and reports describe forcible land
clearance for military projects that removed houses without compensation [891][900]. On 8 May 2026, junta forces torched an IDP
camp in Hpruso Township, Karenni State, burning more than half of the camp's 68 shelters and forcing over 1,000 people to flee into
surrounding forests, with roads remaining blocked and humanitarian access constrained as of 11 May 2026 [881]. These patterns have
increased reliance on host families, spontaneous temporary settlements and makeshift forest shelters, and have complicated
rebuilding due to tenure loss, insecurity and restricted access for materials and aid [874][876][900]. Humanitarian shelter response
reach has been severely limited: UNHCR reported providing shelter assistance to nearly 280 people (61 families) in Myanmar by
endMarch 2026, while access restrictions, fuel shortages and movement controls continue to hinder delivery and reconstruction
efforts [875][876].
Living Conditions in Camps or Settlements
UNHCR reported 1.6 million refugees/asylum-seekers and 3.7 million internally displaced within the Myanmar situation as of endMarch
2026, with rising displacement placing additional strain on camp services and host communities [876]. The Rohingya refugee
population in Bangladesh reached about 1,194,123 individuals (248,169 families) as of late April/early May 2026, and roughly 147,000
Rohingya arrivals to camps since 2024 have increased overcrowding and pressure on infrastructure and services [877][875]. Shelter
quality in many displacement sites is poor, with displaced families in Rakhine/Myebon living in makeshift sites using tarpaulins for
roofs and floors that become sodden in rains, while tens of thousands sheltering across nearly 70 villages in Kyaukphyu Township face
shortages of food, clean water and clothing [896][903]. Protection risks in camps and displacement settings are acute, including
documented patterns of sexual violence, coercion, forced recruitment, incommunicado detention and survival sex among Rohingya in
central Rakhine, with monitoring covering May 2024–April 2026 and specific incidents reported in early 2026 [884][885]. Airstrikes and
attacks on civilian shelters—including strikes that killed children and attacks on facilities housing displaced people in March–May
2026—have further undermined safety and the integrity of displacement sites, compounding risks for IDPs and refugees [883][903].
Camps and settlements face severe service gaps, with UNHCR and partners flagging persistent underfunding and limited livelihood
opportunities, leaving many needs unmet [875][876].
NFI and Shelter Needs and Current Response
By endMarch 2026, UNHCR reported providing core relief items to over 10,700 people (1,889 families), shelter assistance to nearly 280
people (61 families), and multipurpose cash assistance to around 120 people (31 families) in Myanmar [875]. UNHCR leads the
Protection and Shelter, Land and Site Coordination Cluster and conducted joint shelter and WASH assessments—including in
Kachin—to inform relocation planning, though access constraints limit coverage [875]. UNHCR's 2026 appeal for the Myanmar situation
was only 26% funded by yearend, severely constraining procurement and scaleup of NFIs and shelter support [875]. Displacement
site conditions indicate insufficient NFI quality and quantity: many households shelter under tarpaulins with minimal household items,
few cooking utensils and scarce menstrual products, and some IDPs have received only partial shelter assistance [896][881]. Tens of
thousands displaced in Kyaukphyu Township since November 2025 face shortages of food, clean water and clothing, with intensified
airstrikes in early May further restricting aid access, while fighting along the ChinMagway border has driven repeated evacuations
affecting approximately 8,000 people displaced southwest of DDI Factory 24, complicating NFI and shelter delivery [903][895]. Key
logistical barriers to scaling NFI and shelter distributions include fuel shortages, road blockages, insecurity and restricted humanitarian
access, resulting in limited coverage relative to needs [876][881][878]. Local coordination networks such as KPSN warn that aid routed
through juntaaffiliated administrative channels reaches only a small fraction of intended recipients and call on donors to fund NGOs
and CSOs that can directly reach displaced communities [878]. Where assistance has reached people, UNHCR reported that
interventions helped mitigate protection risks and stabilise living conditions for small beneficiary groups, but overall coverage remains
a major gap given the scale of displacement [875][876].
Protection
Impact on civilians
As of end-March 2026, UNHCR reported 5.3 million people forcibly displaced within the Myanmar situation, including 3.7 million
internally displaced persons (IDPs), 1.6 million refugees and asylum-seekers, and 1.85 million Rohingya stateless persons [926][922].
Intensified fighting involving airstrikes, shelling, and drone strikes continued to cause widespread civilian harm and large-scale
population movements into May 2026, including incidents in central Dry Zone townships and Rakhine State that killed and injured
civilians and displaced tens of thousands [933][955].
21
The Independent Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar (IIMM) documented an airstrike on 1 May 2026 in Khawpuichhip Village that
killed six children and one adult, and a separate attack earlier in May that killed at least five children at a village playground in Chin
State [919][923]. The IIMM is also investigating the Oe Htein Kwin school attack of 12 May 2025, which killed more than 20 students, and
based on available — likely underestimated — data, found that children were killed or injured in at least 640 airstrikes between
February 2021 and 2025 [919][920][923]. The Burmese Women's Union recorded 44 women, including eight girls, killed and 57 injured
across Myanmar in April 2026 alone, with most deaths attributed to junta airstrikes and related attacks [924].
Local documentation groups recorded extensive civilian harm: the Pyisama Mandai Civil Surveillance Group documented 1,122
human-rights violations across Yangon, Sagaing, Magway and Naypyidaw from early March 2025 to end-February 2026, attributing
843 civilian deaths and the destruction of 3,528 structures during that period [928]. Insecurity Insight recorded 1,918 attacks on health
care in Myanmar between the 2021 coup and 14 April 2026, with 173 health workers killed, 909 arrested, and health facilities damaged
over 497 times [935].
Dangerous maritime movements by Rohingya have also intensified, with more than 150,000 fleeing to Bangladesh between late 2023
and 2025, nearly 900 reported dead or missing at sea in 2025, and early-2026 incidents including capsized boats and intercepted
vessels with large numbers detained in Sittwe [917][926].
Factors Driving protection risks
Ongoing, intensified conflict — encompassing airstrikes, shelling, drones and large-scale ground offensives — is causing widespread
civilian harm, repeated displacement, and severe restrictions on humanitarian access [926][919][955][933]. Arbitrary arrests,
conscription-related abductions and incommunicado detention have been widely reported alongside extortion, forced labour and
economic coercion, undermining legal protection and isolating communities [928][917][926]. Sexual violence, including rape during
recruitment raids and in detention as well as survival sex driven by humanitarian collapse, is documented as a tool of intimidation and
exploitation, particularly affecting Rohingya women and girls [917][918][926].
Attacks on health care and health workers have been extensive, further degrading access to medical services and protection for
civilians [935]. Severe economic deprivation, fuel shortages and disrupted supply chains are constraining aid delivery and driving risky
coping strategies, including trafficking and dangerous maritime movements with high fatality rates [926][925][917][918].
Psychological and mental health impacts
Repeated airstrikes, widespread displacement and reported village burnings are driving severe stress and trauma among civilians.
Children exposed to frequent bombardment are described by the IIMM as experiencing deep and lasting psychological impacts [920].
Rohingya survivors of sexual violence face fear of retaliation, stigma and restricted access to justice — factors that make abuse
severely underreported and exacerbate long-term psychosocial harm [917]. Children, survivors of sexual and gender-based violence
(notably Rohingya women and girls), and repeatedly displaced populations are consistently identified as particularly vulnerable to
trauma and psychosocial distress [920][917][977].
Humanitarian partners and protection clusters have identified mental health and psychosocial support (MHPSS) as a priority need, but
service delivery is constrained by insecurity, restricted access, and operational disruptions including fuel shortages [926][922]. Attacks
on health care have further degraded service availability and undermined MHPSS delivery [953]. Evaluations and partner feedback
indicate that mental health is often deprioritized in health coordination, and funding cuts have forced some organisations to suspend
legal and psychological support, reducing options for survivors and other affected people [938][952].
Protection Needs and Current Response
Documented protection needs span multiple regions and population groups. Conflict-related risks include forced recruitment,
extortion, arbitrary detention, and damage from airstrikes and shelling, while partners report urgent needs for civil documentation and
support to obtain or renew identity documents in Shan and Kayah [926]. Escalating sexual violence against Rohingya women and girls
— increasingly linked to forced recruitment, detention and coercive control in areas under Arakan Army control — is compounded by
an absence of credible investigations or access to justice [917]. In Bangladesh, UNHCR reported 1,194,123 Rohingya refugees as of 12 May
2026, with 137,061 unique people identified as having specific protection needs, underscoring significant demand for specialised
protection services and case management in camps and host communities [946]. Approximately 500 Rohingya were detained in
Thailand as of end-March 2026, including over 70 children [926].
Humanitarian operations face severe constraints from insecurity, road blockages, movement controls and fuel shortages that disrupt
staff movement, supply chains and programme delivery. The regional response has been further strained by underfunding and
diversion of attention and resources to other crises [922][926]. Local protection networks — such as the Karen Peace Support Network
coordinating 22 organizations — are urging donors to bypass junta-controlled channels and fund organizations that can directly reach
displaced communities along the border, citing widespread corruption and diversion that limit aid reaching IDPs [931].
Despite these constraints, UNHCR and partners continue to prioritise life-saving assistance and protection interventions, including
protection and cash assistance in host countries, documentation and registration support, legal awareness sessions, and
capacity-building for magistrates and judges on refugee protection and status determination [926][922]. Monitoring and
accountability mechanisms such as the IIMM collect evidence and solicit confidential reporting on air attacks, but restrictions on
movement, communication and access continue to hinder protection monitoring, case management and referrals [920][917].

Child Protection concerns
The IIMM warned on 12 May 2026 that children continue to be killed and injured by the military's air campaign, highlighting deliberate or
indiscriminate strikes on locations where children gather, including schools, playgrounds and displacement sites [919][920]. The
deadly school attack in Oe Htein Kwin in May 2025, which killed more than 20 students, was cited as part of this pattern [919]. Based on
available — likely underestimated — data, children were affected in at least 640 airstrikes between February 2021 and 2025 [919][920].
Approximately 500 Rohingya were detained in Thailand as of end-March 2026, including over 70 children, increasing risks to children's
safety and access to services [926]. Local monitoring groups documented conscription-related abductions and widespread civilian
targeting — including nighttime raids and arrests — across multiple regions between March 2025 and February 2026, signalling
elevated risks of recruitment and family separation for children in affected areas [928]. Reports describe rising risks of trafficking,
sexual exploitation and survival sex among displaced Rohingya and other affected communities as humanitarian conditions worsen,
increasing children's exposure to negative coping mechanisms and exploitation [917][918].
Violence against women and girls
Burmese Rohingya Organisation UK (BROUK) documents escalating sexual violence against Rohingya women and girls in areas under
Arakan Army (AA) control, based on field reporting collected between May 2024 and April 2026. Since early 2026, AA
forced-recruitment systems — including village quotas, named lists and arrest operations — and incommunicado detention have
directly targeted women and girls, increasing their exposure to sexual violence and using it as a tool of intimidation and control, with
documentation concentrated in Maungdaw and Buthidaung [917][918]. BROUK also reports that severe deprivation and movement
restrictions in displacement camps in central Rakhine State are driving some Rohingya women — particularly female-headed
households and widows — into survival sex, while displacement and risky maritime movements increase trafficking and sexual
exploitation risks [917].
The Burmese Women's Union recorded 44 women, including eight girls, killed and 57 injured in junta attacks across Myanmar in April
2026, with deaths linked to conflict-related sexual violence and other forms of abuse [924]. Human Rights Watch documented that
cuts to US foreign aid in 2025 disrupted investigations and led several organizations to suspend legal and psychological support to
victims in Myanmar, weakening accountability and survivor support mechanisms [950]. Underreporting remains significant due to fear
of retaliation, stigma and access constraints, meaning documented cases represent only a partial picture of GBV prevalence and risks
in affected areas [917].
Vulnerable Groups / Special Needs Groups
Unaccompanied and Separated Children
According to UNHCR's Joint Government of Bangladesh registration data from April 2026, 2,484 unaccompanied and separated
children were recorded among the Rohingya refugee population [988]. Around 500 Rohingya — including over 70 children — were held
in Thai government facilities as of end-March 2026 [980]. Monitoring and briefings by BROUK and UN sources document escalating
risks to separated children and their caregivers from forced recruitment, detention, sexual violence, trafficking and hazardous
maritime movements in Rakhine and other areas, exacerbated by displacement and restricted humanitarian access (information
collected May 2024–April 2026) [983][993][980]. Between January–March 2026, UNHCR and partners in Myanmar delivered
community-based protection activities, legal-awareness and protection-from-sexual-exploitation workshops, and supported
civil-documentation efforts in Shan and Kayah, strengthening access to protection services and casework pathways [980].
Pregnant and Lactating Women
Humanitarian access to reproductive and maternal health services in Myanmar is severely constrained by insecurity, movement
controls, road blockages and fuel shortages, while repeated attacks on healthcare have damaged facilities and disrupted service
delivery [982][1017]. Since the February 2021 coup through 14 April 2026, 1,918 attacks on healthcare were recorded in Myanmar, during
which 173 health workers were killed and 909 arrested, and health facilities were damaged over 497 times [1017]. At least 482 maternal
health services have been damaged or disrupted globally in attacks on healthcare, indicating maternal services are a frequent target
in such incidents [1017]. Documentation collected between May 2024 and April 2026 by BROUK and other monitors records escalating
sexual violence and exploitation of Rohingya women and girls — including rape, gang rape, detention-linked abuse, survival sex and
trafficking risks — with significant underreporting and little accountability in affected areas [983][993]. The Burmese Women's Union
recorded 44 women killed across Myanmar in April 2026, including one pregnant woman, underscoring acute protection and maternal
risk [1010].
Adolescents and Youth
One year after the Oe Htein Kwin school airstrike on 12 May 2025 that killed more than 20 students, children continue to be killed or
injured in nationwide airstrikes, including an early-May 2026 attack in Chin State that killed at least five children. The Independent
Investigative Mechanism for Myanmar (IIMM) estimated that children were killed or injured in at least 640 airstrikes between February
2021 and 2025 [985][987]. According to UNHCR, escalating violence across Myanmar has driven protection risks for young people —
including forced recruitment, extortion and arbitrary detention — and partners identified mental health and psychosocial support
(MHPSS) as a priority need for displaced and vulnerable groups [980][982]. A junta attack that set fire to an IDP camp in Hpruso
Township, Karenni State on 8 May 2026 forced more than 1,000 people to flee and left urgent food needs amid restricted humanitarian
access [1005]. Local civil-society networks report rising displacement and gaps in aid delivery for displaced families in Mon, Karen,
Tanintharyi and other regions, and have urged donors to channel assistance directly to organizations that can reach displaced
communities rather than routing aid through junta structures [984].

People with Disabilities
An evaluation of the Global Health Cluster found limited evidence of disability-related activities across focus countries, noting that
disability needs are less consistently addressed than those of other vulnerable groups [1012]. UNHCR's Rohingya registration data from
April 2026 records persons with disabilities among persons with specific needs (PSN), comprising approximately 12% of the PSN
caseload in the refugee population [988]. Monitoring by women's groups recorded 44 women killed and 57 injured in April 2026, with a
disabled woman among the injured, illustrating that people with disabilities are directly affected by recent violence and protection
threats [1010]. UNHCR and Protection Cluster partners prioritized support for vulnerable groups — including civil documentation, MHPSS,
and immediate improvements to living conditions — via joint assessments and Shelter/WASH missions in Kachin during
January–March 2026 [982].
Elderly
The Rohingya refugee population in Bangladesh totaled 1,194,123 individuals as of April 2026, including 23,462 people aged 60 and
above (approximately 2%) [988]. Myanmar's health cluster explicitly disaggregates data on elderly people, a practice not consistently
observed across all evaluated focus countries [1012]. On 8–11 May 2026, junta troops torched the Hway Du Lar IDP camp in Hpruso
Township, Karenni State, forcing over 1,000 people to flee into forest shelters, injuring a 90-year-old man, and preventing humanitarian
assistance from reaching the displaced due to security concerns and blocked roads, with efforts to reach them reported ongoing on 11
May [1005]. Fuel shortages, rising costs and displacement are further straining older people's livelihoods and mobility — illustrated by
accounts of farmers unable to access fuel and fertiliser, with resulting risks to community food security [1011]. UNHCR and Protection
Cluster partners have identified priority needs in displacement-affected areas — including support for vulnerable groups, civil
documentation, MHPSS and immediate assistance to improve living conditions — which are directly relevant to older persons in
response planning [980].
Displaced
As of end-March 2026, there were an estimated 3.7 million internally displaced people (IDPs) in Myanmar and 1.6 million refugees and
asylum-seekers from Myanmar, with the broader situation accounting for 5.3 million forcibly displaced persons and 1.85 million
Rohingya stateless persons [982]. The refugee outflow continues to strain host services: as of 30 April 2026, Bangladesh hosted 1,194,123
Rohingya refugees, including roughly 149,769 arrivals since 2024, increasing pressure on livelihoods, WASH, health and education in
camps [988]. Escalating violence — including airstrikes and shelling — is driving new displacement, risky maritime movements of
Rohingya (boats capsizing, interceptions and detention in Sittwe), and instances of boats returning from Bangladesh to Sittwe since
early January 2026 [980]. Fighting and intensified airstrikes in Rakhine have displaced tens of thousands since November 2025, leaving
nearly 70 villages sheltering displaced families with shortages of food, clean water and clothing; an airstrike on 7 May 2026 reportedly
killed three civilians including a child in Minbya/Kyauktaw areas [1003]. Protection risks for displaced people include arbitrary arrest,
detention and torture, with reports from Sittwe on 9 May 2026 of raids on monasteries where IDPs were detained and at least one
detainee died from severe torture [996]. By end-March 2026, partners in Myanmar had provided core relief items to over 10,700 people,
shelter assistance to nearly 280 people and multi-purpose assistance to around 120 people, and supported legal-awareness sessions
and civil-documentation renewal efforts in Shan and Kayah states [980]. Local protection and aid networks warn that routing
assistance through junta-controlled channels has led to diversion, with only a small fraction of aid reaching conflict-affected IDPs; the
Karen Peace Support Network and others have urged donors to channel support directly to local NGOs, CSOs and cross-border actors
able to reach displaced communities in Mon, Karen, Tanintharyi and Bago regions [984]. Persistent underfunding, fuel shortages and
movement restrictions continue to disrupt humanitarian operations and limit the feasibility of return, resettlement or local integration
for many displaced people [982][980].
Minorities
Rohingya communities remain systematically denied citizenship and face identity-based restrictions and severe protection risks
imposed by both the Burmese military and the Arakan Army (AA), contributing to ongoing displacement and risky maritime
movement; more than 150,000 Rohingya fled to Bangladesh between late 2023 and 2025, and nearly 900 Rohingya were reported
dead or missing at sea in 2025 [983][993]. BROUK's briefing documents escalating patterns of sexual violence against Rohingya
women and girls in AA-controlled areas of northern Rakhine State — including rape, gang rape, threats of sexual violence linked to
forced recruitment, arbitrary detention and incommunicado confinement — warning these abuses are used as tools of intimidation
and control; the briefing is based on information collected between May 2024 and April 2026 and calls for urgent international action,
including UN Security Council engagement and strengthened accountability measures [983][993]. UNHCR reported targeted
protection and assistance efforts inside Myanmar through end-March 2026, including legal-awareness sessions, workshops on
protection from sexual exploitation and abuse, and mechanisms to strengthen community-based protection and accountability to
affected populations [980].
