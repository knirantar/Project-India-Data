Methodology Overview
The Situation Hub's analytical process uses a structured Human-in-the-Loop workflow that combines automated analysis retrieval
through GANNET Virtual Assistant, DFS's AI-powered humanitarian assistant, with expert review by the DFS analysis team. GANNET
automates the extraction and generation of crisis-related insights based on structured prompts, which are aligned with a tailored
analytical framework. These outputs are then reviewed, validated, and refined by analysts to ensure accuracy, contextual relevance,
and operational utility. The workflow is organized into four key phases: planning and setup, ongoing operations, quality assurance, and
system evolution, ensuring a balance between speed and analytical rigor.
Planning, Setup & Quality Control
In the planning phase, analysts develop a contextualized analytical framework, largely based on the Joint Intersectoral Analysis
Framework (JIAF), and convert it into structured prompts for AI-assisted data extraction. These prompts are refined over time to
improve output quality. Data sources are vetted for reliability, and translations are reviewed for accuracy. Using a centralized Content
Management System (CMS), analysts review and curate AI-generated outputs, validate them against external sources, and make
refinements to enhance clarity and relevance. The CMS enables collaborative editing and version tracking, ensuring outputs align with
humanitarian needs.
Bias Mitigation
To minimize bias, the team diversifies data sources, cross-verifies information, and continuously evaluates the AI's performance.
Analysts are crucial in identifying and correcting inaccuracies, especially in automated categorization. Future improvements aim to
enhance source credibility checks and sentiment analysis, further strengthening the reliability of the Situation Hub's outputs.

Summary
The conflict between Hezbollah and Israel, which began in October 2023 and escalated sharply in September 2024, entered a new
phase in early 2026. Although a ceasefire came into effect on 27 November 2024, violations continued almost daily, with Israeli
airstrikes and continued occupation of five positions in southern Lebanon preventing reconstruction and the return of displaced
communities. 
The ceasefire effectively collapsed on 2 March 2026 following U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran. Hezbollah responded with rockets and drones
targeting Israel, prompting heavy Israeli bombardment, expanded ground operations, and evacuation orders across southern
Lebanon and Beirut’s southern suburbs. 
UNIFIL has faced severe operational constraints during its final mandate year. Since March, peacekeepers have been killed, patrols
targeted, and monitoring infrastructure damaged, undermining the mission’s ability to document violations along the Blue Line. Israeli
military operations intensified throughout March and April 2026, evolving from “limited” operations into a broader ground campaign
extending deeper into southern Lebanon. 
Since the massive military escalation that spiked on April 8, 2026, Israel has targeted thousands of sites across Lebanon, including
hitting over 100 concentrated targets on April 8 alone. A breakthrough in mid-May resulted in a 45-day ceasefire extension and a
roadmap for further military and political negotiations, although Hezbollah continued to reject the process and sporadic violence
persisted.As a result, the diplomatic deal remains highly fragile, with continued localized airstrikes, rocket fire, and civilian casualties on
the ground
The humanitarian impact has been catastrophic. Since 2 March 2026, over 3,070 people have reportedly been killed and nearly 9,362
injured, with women, children, and the elderly accounting for an estimated 25% to 35% of the total civilian casualties. The heaviest
structural destruction is concentrated in Southern Lebanon, the Bekaa Valley, and the southern suburbs of Beirut, where residential
blocks, key roads, schools and vital healthcare infrastructure have been flattened, forcing over 1.2 million people to flee their homes.
Humanitarian funding in Lebanon remains critically inadequate. By late May 2026, the UN’s USD 308.3 million Flash Appeal for March -

funding shortfall threatens essential services, including food assistance, clean water, and healthcare support for the more than 1.2

Operational Environment
Economy
Current Economic Situation
Lebanon is experiencing a deepening macroeconomic shock: Economy Minister Amer Bisat estimated the war has caused an
economic loss of around 7% of GDP and described the shock as existential [4]. The economy contracted sharply by 7.1% in 2024 before
a partial recovery estimated at +3.5% in 2025, while cumulative financial-sector losses since 2019 have been put at about $70 billion
with additional war-related losses cited around $11 billion [29][4]. The Lebanese pound has lost over 90% of its value against the US
dollar since 2019, and the banking collapse evaporated depositors' funds; the sector remains unresolved, with depositors still waiting
for access to blocked funds and authorities yet to finalize sector restructuring [4][1]. Inflation accelerated to 17.26% year-on-year in
March 2026 (from 12.27% in February) with a month-on-month increase of 4.91%, and purchasing power remains weak as households
rely on credit and cut non-food spending [1][55].
Key industries are severely affected: tourism occupancy rates were reported at roughly 5–10% overall and about 7–8% in Beirut for the
Adha/summer period, many businesses report collapsed revenues (some restaurants cited ~90% drops), and widespread reliance on
private diesel generators increases vulnerability to fuel price spikes [50][26][4]. Direct and indirect war losses have been estimated at
over $25–26 billion, adding major reconstruction pressures on public finances and the private sector [22][28]. Food insecurity is
worsening, with an estimated 1.24 million people projected to face acute food insecurity between April–August 2026 and FEWS NET
warning that staple food and fuel prices will remain significantly above average through September 2026 [1][89][55]. Humanitarian
financing gaps are constraining the response: the 2026 Lebanon Flash Appeal was only around 38–42% funded in early–mid May
(roughly US$117–129 million of the US$308 million requested), limiting partners' capacity to sustain services [18][51].
Post-Conflict Economic Landscape
Lebanon faces large-scale reconstruction needs and acute economic disruption. The World Bank estimated $11 billion in recovery and
reconstruction needs (physical damage $6.8 billion; economic losses $7.2 billion) for October 2023–December 2024, while Lebanese
private-sector estimates place direct and indirect war losses at about $25–26 billion, with roughly $12 billion identified as immediate
reconstruction needs and indirect losses of about $30 million per day cited by business leaders [44][15][2][22]. The World Bank
forecasted up to 4% real growth for 2026 conditional on reforms and reconstruction financing [37][1].
Key sectoral impacts include:
- Housing and infrastructure: housing was the most affected sector with an estimated $4.6 billion in damage; more than 10,000
housing units have been reported as destroyed or affected [15].
- Agriculture: direct agricultural damage is estimated at ~$118 million, losses at ~$586 million (total >$700 million), and
reconstruction needs at ~$263 million (with $95 million priority for 2025–2026); about 54,000 hectares were directly affected
(≈22.5% of national agricultural area), with large unharvested or late-harvest areas and damaged irrigation and cooperatives
reported [44].
- Displacement and food security: over 1.2 million people were forced from their homes between March and April; acute food
insecurity is estimated at ~1.24 million people for April–August 2026 (up from ~874,000 in November 2025–March 2026), with
southern districts facing the highest severity [2][1].
- Humanitarian financing: the UN flash appeal (US$308.3 million) was only ~38% funded, leaving major gaps as needs deepen [11].
The shock is concentrated in southern governorates — particularly Nabatieh and South — where agriculture, housing and basic
services are most affected, posing medium- to long-term risks to livelihoods and return. Reconstruction faces four principal obstacles:
access and clearance, documentation of damage, financing capacity, and prioritization of repair versus rebuild [15][11][44]. Diaspora
remittances and IOM-supported diaspora engagement remain important sources of emergency support and technical assistance for
recovery [84].
Stability of Currency and Bank System
The Lebanese pound remained officially at LBP 89,500 per USD in Bank of Lebanon data available until February 2026 [1]. Annual
inflation rose to 17.26% in March 2026 (from 12.27% in February 2026) with a month-on-month increase of 4.91% [1]. Parallel market
pressures and large official-to-parallel exchange-rate divergences are creating economic uncertainty and reducing the
local-currency value of USD transfers for UN agencies, which are typically required to convert at the official rate; WFP's exchange-rate
monitoring reports parallel rates for Lebanon using Lira Rate [35]. Market reports noted shortages of both Lebanese pounds and US
dollars in domestic markets as of 11 May 2026 [47].
The banking sector remains unresolved since the 2019 collapse that evaporated depositors' savings, leaving many depositors waiting
for access to blocked funds while sector restructuring and saver protections remain pending [4][1]. The renewed conflict has
compounded fiscal stress, with private estimates placing cumulative war losses at roughly $25–26 billion [22][28].
Employment
The ongoing hostilities and economic shocks have caused widespread job losses across tourism, agriculture, retail and informal day
labour, with traders closing shops, farmers abandoning land and many daily workers losing income [4][1][23]. The hotel sector
reported about 40,000 employees with unemployment among them estimated at 30–40%, current occupancy at 5–10%, and around
70% of hotels from Beirut airport to the south closed [50].

Politics
Governance Structures and Rule of Law
Lebanon's governance landscape remains severely strained by the interplay of active hostilities, institutional fragmentation, and the
entrenched role of non-state armed actors. Prime Minister Nawaf Salam has publicly documented war crimes and stated that 68
villages remain under Israeli control, while Speaker Nabih Berri has set political conditions for any ceasefire — demanding Israeli
withdrawal, reconstruction funding, deployment of the Lebanese army, and the return of inhabitants as prerequisites for restoring state
authority [138][134][204]. Authorities acknowledge limited state control on the ground, noting that Hezbollah remains a major armed
actor that refuses direct negotiation with Israel and constrains the state's ability to implement security arrangements [138]. The United
States announced a 45-day extension of the Lebanon–Israel ceasefire and the launch of a U.S.-facilitated security track, which the
Lebanese negotiating delegation said provides a margin for stability provided commitments are implemented through a phased,
verifiable mechanism backed by American guarantees [190].
The Minister of Information, Paul Morcos, reported 1,744 ceasefire violations and presented a government survey covering 17 April–8
May to document incidents for international and legal purposes, linking this effort to diplomatic briefing needs [143]. The Ministry is
coordinating awareness and training materials with UNESCO and UNDP to address hate speech and media practices, and a modern
information bill that would regulate electronic sites and criminalize defined forms of hate speech is awaiting parliamentary adoption
[143]. OCHA reported significant displacement dynamics during the ceasefire period: an initial 21% drop in registered shelter population
to 117,421 across 631 sites was followed by rebounds, with around 90% of displaced people outside formal shelters; fresh displacement
orders were issued for 11 localities on 4 May and overnight orders affected Sohmor on 11 May, while Israel warned people not to return to
74 localities [184].
Lebanon's social protection and service-delivery landscape remains fragmented and clientelist. The National Poverty Targeting
Programme expanded after the 2019 economic collapse, but access continues to be shaped by political affiliation and local networks,
and cash assistance is described as central yet limited in producing sustained economic outcomes [140]. Programmatic reviews from
Tripoli underscore that weak labour markets, informality, and political-economic exclusion constrain the effectiveness of cash-plus
interventions, pointing to the need for policy engagement and structural advocacy to address governance-related barriers to service
and livelihood recovery [153].
Political Freedoms and Recent Laws
Lebanese authorities and statements from Beirut link the resolution of pending cases — including the release of detainees and the
return of internally displaced persons — to implementation of the ceasefire and related security arrangements [114]. The National
Council for Scientific Research in Lebanon (CNRS-L) reported that more than 10,000 homes have been destroyed or damaged since
the truce, a figure Lebanon says undermines civilian protection and complicates the return of displaced persons and reconstruction
planning [111][114]. Hezbollah's armed presence continues to divide the Lebanese political scene, challenge the state's monopoly on
force, and complicate U.S. and Lebanese efforts to secure disarmament or a unified security framework [187][174].
Humanitarian and programme analyses highlight that Lebanon's social protection and access to assistance remain fragmented and
are often mediated by political affiliation and clientelist networks, limiting equitable state-led provision and potentially affecting equal
access to reconstruction and electoral participation opportunities [140][153]. Domestically, the Information Ministry is pursuing a draft
law against hate speech alongside parallel documentation efforts to address internal polarisation — both measures aimed at
strengthening civic space and restoring confidence in state institutions [143].
Influence of Non-State Actors and Political Factions
Hezbollah functions as a dominant non-state armed and political actor that constrains state authority, shapes Lebanon's political
paralysis, and risks gaining strength domestically as Lebanese leaders both criticise and lack the capacity to disarm it amid ongoing
Israeli operations [174][122]. Iran exerts significant influence through its ties to Hezbollah and regional manoeuvres to preserve its
foothold in Beirut, while external actors — notably the United States and Gulf states — pressure Lebanon to curb Hezbollah,
complicating domestic decision-making and negotiations [194]. Lebanon's political landscape is highly fragmented, with sectarian
divisions and elite clientelism undermining unified state responses and increasing reliance on alternative power centres and local
networks [174][140]. The interaction of a powerful armed political actor, external patronage, and entrenched clientelist networks is a
core driver of governance dysfunction, constraining humanitarian access, service restoration, and coherent recovery planning
[174][194][140][115].

Role of Marginalized Groups
In Tripoli, cash-plus programmes combining temporary cash transfers with vocational training found that labour-market saturation,

Security And Stability
Security Overview
The security situation in Lebanon remains highly volatile, with ongoing Israeli strikes, armed activity by Hezbollah, evacuation alerts in
southern localities, and widespread presence of weapons outside state authority that undermine civilian safety and humanitarian
operations [263][237][271]. UN officials have warned the ceasefire is increasingly fragile, with UNIFIL reporting six peacekeepers killed
since 2 March, and Lebanese authorities and AFP tallies putting the country's overall death toll since strikes began at over 2,800,
including more than 400 fatalities during the ceasefire window [263][271]. Attacks on health services continued, with six new attacks
recorded in the latest reporting period resulting in five health-worker deaths and eight injuries, including reported strikes on 11 May that
targeted paramedics in Toul and hit a health facility in Srifa (one killed, four injured) [259]. Humanitarian operations continue to face
impediments from damaged infrastructure, disrupted supply pipelines, and unexploded ordnance contamination that restrict safe
returns and service delivery [259].
Displacement levels remain elevated with no large-scale or sustained returns observed in South Lebanon and Nabatieh; collective
shelter populations are approaching pre-ceasefire peak levels, with protection actors reaching 182,313 IDPs as of 11 May and 192,600
IDPs as of 14 May [259][272][256]. Children remain disproportionately affected, with an estimated 390,000 displaced children and
heightened risks of family separation, exploitation, and interrupted services [256]. Key operational response figures include:
- Protection services: 64,976 people received MHPSS, 1,259 individuals received case management, 1,796 protection cash grants
were provided, 24,841 women/girls received dignity kits, and ~21,000 people were supported through protection
feedback/complaints channels [272].
- Shelter/WASH response: repairs/upgrades underway across 360 collective shelters (282 completed to date), WASH assistance
delivered in 611 collective shelters since 2 March, 88,043 hygiene kits and 50,333 menstrual kits distributed, and 3,765,725 litres of
potable water delivered [272].

Since mid-May 2026, the Lebanese Armed Forces (LAF) and internal security forces have been deployed on roadblocks and

State control on the ground remains limited because Hezbollah continues to operate as a major armed actor and has not joined the

capability and humanitarian access are constrained by an Israeli-declared military zone in the south, damaged infrastructure,
IED/UXO contamination, and disrupted communications, which limit movement, increase risks to personnel, and complicate security
operations [272]. Civil-military coordination has allowed some humanitarian access, with 121 Humanitarian Notification
System-facilitated movements enabling delivery of life-saving assistance across southern Lebanon between 2 March and 11 May,
though access is increasingly contingent on route predictability and local security conditions [272]. Verified attacks on health facilities
and personnel have further degraded security-sector capacity to protect civilians and responders, with WHO-verified figures
recording 158 attacks on healthcare causing 108 deaths and 249 injuries as of 11 May 2026, and field reports confirming severely
constrained service delivery in affected areas [221][233].
Demography
Demographic Overview
Lebanon's population is roughly 5.5 million people [294]. Since the escalation on 2 March, over 1.04 million people have self-registered
as displaced on the Government of Lebanon relief platform (MoSA), with overall displaced estimates exceeding one million by May
2026 [297][295]. Most displaced households are hosted outside formal shelters—in host families, rented accommodation, or informal
arrangements—placing sustained pressure on host communities and complicating humanitarian planning [317][292]. The
collective-shelter population has trended upward, rising from approximately 125,621 people on 6 May to roughly 127,210 by 11 May, and
reaching approximately 129,721 people across 632 collective sites by 14 May 2026—representing a 13% increase in IDPs in shelters since
17 April 2026 [297][295][318][292]. Children represent a large share of the displaced, accounting for about 35% of IDPs, with an
estimated 390,000 displaced children nationally and roughly 44,800 children currently in collective shelters [290][320][292].
Cross-border movements remain substantial: IOM/DTM recorded some 312,996 movements at official points of entry as of 11 May 2026
(approximately 96% Syrian nationals), with UN/IOM sources reporting roughly 321,666 Syrians crossing back to Syria between 2 March
and 6 May, while around 38,000 people have returned to Lebanon in the same period [306][297][307].
Health sector reporting (MoPH/WHO) through 15 May indicates 11,939 total casualties since 2 March, including 2,951 deaths and 8,988
injuries. Casualties are disproportionately male (approximately 86%), with children accounting for about 9% of casualties (209 child
deaths and 823 injured). The vast majority of casualties are reported as Lebanese (approximately 94%), with burdens concentrated in
Nabatieh and South governorates, followed by Mount Lebanon [312][292].
Socio-Cultural
Socio-Cultural Overview
In many parts of Tripoli, host and refugee communities are socially, economically and geographically intertwined with shared norms
and religious affiliation, and while tensions surface under scarcity, long-standing ties complicate a binary host/refugee framing [338].
Access to social protection and assistance is fragmented and often mediated by political affiliation and local networks rather than
solely by need, despite the National Poverty Targeting Programme's expansion since 2019 to include cash support [332]. Displacement
is reshaping community composition: families from predominantly Shia southern areas have settled in localities with different religious
fabrics, increasing concerns about inter-community tensions and pressure on urban services and housing stock [340][339].
Community relations are described as fragile but include local examples of mutual assistance and host support for livelihoods, even
as competition over rents, water and generators fuels tensions in host cities [333][340]. Elevated social tensions are further driven by
host-community pressure, shelter capacity shortfalls, intra-Lebanese polarisation and misinformation, and return movements
following ceasefire announcements were often short-lived in May 2026 [347]. Local solidarity persists—municipalities, volunteers and
diaspora-linked initiatives support shelter, food and recovery—but municipal capacities are overstretched and lack fuel, equipment
and liquidity to manage diverse damage and shelter needs [336][371]. Local informal support networks, community committees and
volunteer initiatives remain central coping mechanisms and channels for outreach and information sharing, while spontaneous
community solidarity (e.g., food preparation, ad hoc clinics) has been reported in affected areas [347][382]. Education has been
severely disrupted—schools converted into shelters and large-scale displacement are contributing to learning loss and widening
inequalities among students [341][337]. Widespread anxiety, trauma and rising psychosocial needs have been reported among
displaced children and families, with UNHCR estimating that children constitute about 35% of IDPs in Lebanon [331][350][384].
Gender Dynamics
Protection actors report heightened protection risks for women and girls. As of 11 May, 77 Protection, Child Protection and GBV partners
reached 182,313 IDPs; GBV partners provided 24,841 women and girls with dignity kits, 1,259 people with dedicated case management,
1,796 protection cash grants, and 64,976 people with MHPSS interventions [347]. By 14 May, partners had reached 192,600 IDPs, and
GBVIMS analysis indicated that ongoing and repeated displacement is disrupting continuity of care and complicating case follow-up

[335]. Community-based protection reached 9,724 displaced people since 2 March, 63% of whom were women and girls [333].
Identified GBV risks include increased domestic violence linked to economic stress, harassment restricting women's mobility, and rising
early marriage as a coping mechanism [333]. A nationwide GBV assessment with data collection closed on 8 May was launched to
inform prioritization, with publication expected by end-May [333]. Women and girls — particularly those living in tents, public spaces or
outside collective sites — and persons with disabilities remain among the most at-risk due to unsafe accommodation, lack of WASH
and disrupted services [333][335]. Nutrition and health assessments (March–May 2026) identified approximately 12,200 pregnant
women displaced nationwide, including at least 675 in collective shelters, underscoring acute risks for pregnant and lactating women
[373]. Funding shortfalls, access constraints and fragmented referral pathways are limiting the scale-up and continuity of GBV and
protection services across affected areas [347][335][339].
Impact of Socio-Cultural Drivers on Aid
Cultural norms, social hierarchies and caregiving responsibilities shape both vulnerability and access to humanitarian assistance.
Oxfam's evaluation of cash-plus programming in Tripoli shows that gender norms, caregiving responsibilities and limited female
mobility shape participation in training and employment transitions, with women prioritising proximity and childcare over available job
offers [338]. IWMI's Al Murunah pilot highlights the influence of local opinion leaders and the need for conflict-sensitive, locally-led
engagement to secure inclusive participation and adapt implementation under security and mobility constraints in Lebanon [370].
Protection risks are noted as disproportionately acute for women and girls, separated children and persons with disabilities, which
constrains safe access to assistance and services across displacement sites [347]. Key nutrition and caregiving gaps identified by
Action Against Hunger (March–May 2026) include: 38% of collective shelters lack nutrition support coverage, with the largest gaps in
Beirut and Mount Lebanon [373]; infant and young child feeding is severely disrupted, with exclusive breastfeeding under 6 months at
only 7% in Saida, 16% in Baalbek-Hermel, and 24% in Beirut and Mount Lebanon, while reliance on breastmilk substitutes reaches 85% in
Saida, 59% in Beirut and Mount Lebanon, and 38% in Bekaa and Baalbek-Hermel [373]. Mass protection messages reached
approximately 90,000 people and 21,000 protection feedback and complaints were processed as of 11 May [347].
Environment
Environmental Conditions
Conflict-related environmental damage is concentrated in southern Lebanon, with substantial agricultural and forest loss
documented through 2024–2025 and renewed strikes causing further harm in 2026 [426][411]. Explosions, fires and large-scale
demolition have produced vast volumes of rubble and airborne dust and soot, dispersing hazardous materials including asbestos and
heavy metals, with glyphosate also detected after a 2026 episode near the border [426][434][411]. A major marine pollution event from
the Jieh power-plant bombing spilled approximately 15,000 tonnes of oil and contaminated roughly 150 km of coastline [426]. Damage
to irrigation networks, orchards and wells — together with watershed degradation, deforestation, erosion and increased flash-flood risk
— has intensified water scarcity and undermined agricultural livelihoods and prospects for safe return [411][428][431]. Access
restrictions, ongoing hostilities and unexploded ordnance are impeding comprehensive soil and water testing and rubble clearance,
delaying identification of land safe for cultivation, drilling or reconstruction [457][459][411][456]. Independent environmental
monitoring (soil and water analyses), restoration of irrigation systems, and clearance of unexploded ordnance are highlighted as
priority indicators to determine where it is safe to rebuild, farm or drill wells [411].
Environmental Challenges
Conflict-related attacks since 2023 have caused concentrated environmental damage in southern Lebanon, with ministerial and
scientific reporting estimating about 54,000 hectares of agricultural land directly affected [411][426]. Key damage figures include:
- 5,000 hectares of forest cover and roughly 2,154 hectares of orchards destroyed, with 54,000 hectares of agricultural land
affected overall [426][411].
- Hundreds of white-phosphorus fires (812 reported October 2023–June 2024) and measured soil contamination, with phosphorus
recorded at approximately 1,858 ppm in impact assessments [426].
- Debris volumes estimated in the tens of millions of tonnes (reported ranges of ~50–100 million tonnes), with widespread UXO and
cluster munition contamination noted in affected areas [426][434][425].
Rubble and war waste contain hazardous materials — asbestos, paints, solvents, heavy metals and fuel remnants — alongside
unexploded ordnance, posing long-term soil, water and air pollution risks and complicating safe clearance and disposal [411][434].
Debris-management and reconstruction choices carry further environmental risk: international assessments note Lebanon's
post-conflict experience using rubble for coastal reclamation but warn of long-term environmental and governance controversies,
and UNEP and UN actors stress strict sorting, hazardous-waste removal and environmental and social impact assessment (ESIA)
controls before any coastal or landfill use of rubble [434][424].
Deterioration of WASH and shelter environments — including water leakage, sewage contamination, damp, soot and inadequate
sanitation in collective sites — is driving increased exposure to waterborne and respiratory illnesses and raising malnutrition risks
among displaced populations, as documented in nutrition and protection assessments in March–May 2026, while WHO continues to
monitor environmental and CBRN risks from strikes on industrial and water infrastructure [447][444][442].
Environmental Risks and Mitigation
Since early May 2026, renewed strikes and displacement have continued to generate contaminated debris, unexploded ordnance
(UXO) and damage to water, sanitation and energy infrastructure, threatening WASH continuity, complicating rubble removal and
delaying safe returns and recovery operations [419][436].
10
Unexploded ordnance and contamination are repeatedly cited as primary barriers to cultivation and return, requiring prolonged
submunition and EOD clearance and specialized environmental remediation before safe reconstruction can proceed [411][426].
Humanitarian mitigation and technical measures underway or recommended include environmental and social management plans
(ESMPs) and systematic environmental risk screening in programmes — NRC reported ESMP and NEAT+ use in 2025 — targeted
asbestos removal where identified, explosive ordnance clearance priorities, and WHO-led CBRN monitoring and preparedness actions,
including a radiological hazards webinar for Lebanese emergency response physicians on 15 May 2026 to strengthen health-sector
readiness [423][411][420][448]. Guidance on reuse of conflict debris and coastal reclamation stresses mandatory ESIA, strict material
screening and quality control, worker health and safety, and ongoing environmental monitoring, drawing on lessons from Lebanon's
prior rubble reclamation experience and UNEP recommendations for robust safeguards [424][434]. National technical reports and
assessments estimate overall environmental damage in the multi-billion USD range [426][411][425].
Conflict
Overview of the Ongoing Armed Conflict
The Israel–Hezbollah escalation began on 2 March 2026, leading to a ceasefire announced on 17 April 2026, which was subsequently
extended for 45 days following U.S.-mediated talks on 15 May 2026 [539][499][588]. Violence remains concentrated in southern
Lebanon—particularly Nabatiyeh, Tyre, Bint Jbeil, Marjayoun and Jezzine—while hostilities have also affected parts of the Bekaa Valley
and Beirut's southern suburbs [539][526][567]. Hostilities have continued despite the truce: WHO reported 14,674 total incidents from 2
March through 14 May, including 5,503 incidents after 17 April, and MoPH cumulative figures reached 11,939 casualties including 2,951
deaths as of 15 May 2026 [539]. UNICEF and Lebanese health authorities report heavy child casualties, with roughly 200 children killed
and 806 injured since 2 March and at least 23 children killed and 93 injured since the 17 April ceasefire [576][586]. Attacks on health
care have persisted: WHO's SSA data record 259 health-care worker injuries and 110 deaths since 2 March, WHO verified 15 attacks on
health care resulting in 12 deaths and 21 injuries as of mid-May, and multiple hospitals have been damaged, with three remaining fully
non-operational and sixteen partially damaged [519][555]. Displacement and shelter needs remain acute, with more than one million
people displaced nationally, roughly 127,000–129,700 people sheltering in some 629–715 collective sites, and renewed displacement
orders for about 27 localities driving additional movements and rising pressure on shelters since mid-April [555][519][583].
Humanitarian access is increasingly constrained by ongoing insecurity, route unpredictability, UXO/IED contamination,
telecommunications disruptions and risks of pipeline breaks for health and WASH supplies [583]. UN officials have highlighted the
continued presence of weapons outside Lebanese state authority, ongoing Hezbollah strikes and retaliatory Israeli
operations—together with attacks that have killed UNIFIL peacekeepers—as factors sustaining instability and the risk of renewed
escalation [561].
Conflict Stakeholders
Key state and non-state parties are the Government of Lebanon and the Lebanese Armed Forces (LAF), Hezbollah, and Israel, with the
United States acting as lead mediator and the UN/UNIFIL and major EU actors engaged in security and reconstruction discussions
[597][522][601][561]. Hezbollah remains the decisive armed non-state actor: it is absent from Washington negotiations but continues
military operations on the ground, complicating disarmament ambitions and the implementation of any ceasefire or territorial
handover [522][512]. Israel's objectives focus on neutralizing Hezbollah and maintaining an extended buffer/operational zone north of
the border, while Lebanon's official negotiating priorities are cessation of strikes, Israeli withdrawal and the safe return of displaced
persons [522][528]. The United States is pushing a sequencing that reinforces the LAF—including training and equipping programmes
and handover of occupied areas—as outlined in draft negotiation texts circulated during mid-May talks [597][528]. Iran exerts regional
influence through support for Hezbollah and IRGC-linked operatives, prompting Lebanese authorities to pursue measures including
arrest warrants and public distancing from Iranian actors [594]. Six UNIFIL peacekeepers (four Indonesian, two French) were killed since
2 March, highlighting the direct risk to international forces on the ground [561]. Humanitarian and UN actors are operational on multiple
fronts: UNHCR maintains country and field offices, and humanitarian partners using the Humanitarian Notification System facilitated 131
movements and delivered large-scale WASH and relief items—including 95,954 hygiene kits, 52,803 menstrual kits, 3,765,725 litres of
potable water, 76,090 m³ of water trucking, and 803,000 litres of fuel sustaining public water for 656,973 people—between 2 March and
14 May, though access remains constrained by insecurity, UXO contamination, GPS/communications disruptions and damaged routes
[602][542]. The security dynamic remained fluid during the 14–15 May Washington talks, with ongoing strikes, reported Israeli claims of
substantial Hezbollah losses and expanded operational perimeters in southern Lebanon continuing to shape negotiating leverage and
local civilian protection risks [609][528].
Peace Negotiations
Lebanon and Israel agreed to extend their 17 April ceasefire by 45 days after two days of U.S.-mediated talks in Washington, with a
Pentagon security track set for 29 May and a State Department political track scheduled for 2–3 June [468][483][497]. The talks
involved Lebanese government delegations (including special envoy Simon Karam and ambassador Nada Hamadeh), Israeli officials
(including Ambassador Yechiel Leiter and deputy national security adviser Yossi Draznin), and U.S. mediators from the State
Department and the Pentagon [483][493]. Hezbollah refused to participate and publicly rejected the direct talks, complicating
prospects because Israel insists on Hezbollah's disarmament as a precondition for a broader settlement [468][494]. Despite the
extension, Israeli strikes and cross-border exchanges with Hezbollah continued during and after the talks, causing new displacement
and testing the truce [517][497]. Beirut's negotiating priorities included restoration of sovereignty and Lebanese state authority, Israeli
withdrawal, return of displaced persons, reconstruction, release of detainees and recovery of remains [466][469]. U.S. mediation is
central to the process, while regional dynamics—notably Iran linking a Lebanon settlement to wider Iran–U.S. negotiations—add
geopolitical constraints on progress [474][494]. Negotiators described the sessions as "productive," but analysts and officials warned
11
that ongoing violence, divergent core demands and Hezbollah's absence leave the process fragile and at risk of collapse [492][512].
Conflict Outlook
Military trends show continued low- to medium-intensity clashes along the Lebanon–Israel frontier, with Israeli incursions, airstrikes
and artillery fire and reciprocal Hezbollah drone and missile attacks, and Israel reportedly studying expansion of ground operations
beyond the 10-km anti-armour line [541][508][503]. Diplomatic efforts—including U.S.-hosted Lebanon–Israel talks in Washington and
planned Pentagon and State Department meetings at end-May/early-June—coexist with breaches of the fragile 45-day ceasefire
extension, creating a narrow window for de-escalation but a persistent risk of breakdown [533][503][517]. External dynamics, including
U.S.–Iran negotiations and mediation efforts by Pakistan, Turkey and Egypt, amplify the prospect that shifts in Tehran–Washington
calculations could either stabilise the Lebanese front if tied to a wider deal or trigger renewed escalation if diplomacy falters
[477][577]. A negotiated settlement is possible in the short term but likely limited to temporary extensions of the truce unless the core
issue of Hezbollah's weapons and Lebanese state authority is resolved—an outcome complicated by Hezbollah's rejection of direct
disarmament talks and its insistence on involvement in any security decisions [531][504][532]. Humanitarian consequences are
severe and likely to worsen, with displacement exceeding 1.6 million, constrained health services with repeated attacks on healthcare
and suspended maternal services, and significant food assistance needs remaining despite large-scale deliveries already made
[563][583][530][523]. The near-term outlook is one of continued fragility with elevated probability of localized intensification unless
U.S.-brokered security mechanisms are implemented and linked to broader regional diplomacy; even with repeated temporary truces,
humanitarian needs and displacement pressures will remain high [517][507][528].
Displacement
Displacement Overview
The scale of displacement in Lebanon remains very large and fluid. The Government's Ministry of Social Affairs (MoSA) registry
recorded approximately 1,049,000 people self-registered as displaced as of 1 April [645]. As of 11 May, IOM reported approximately
127,210 IDPs sheltered in 628 active collective sites, while OCHA reported roughly 129,721 people in 632 collective shelters as of 14 May
[645][635][651]. The vast majority of displaced households continue to live outside formal sites in host families, rented or informal
arrangements. An estimated 48,500+ migrants have also been displaced [633].
Cross-border movements have been substantial: IOM reported approximately 451,000 movements from Lebanon into Syria between 2
March and 20 April, and DTM recorded 312,996 movements at Points of Entry and 115,850 individuals at arrival locations in Syria between
2 March and 11 May [633][656].
Displacement has been predominantly cross-governorate, with about 78% of IDPs relocating outside their governorate of origin. Host
districts including Beirut, Aley, Chouf, Saida and Baabda together host roughly three-quarters of the displaced population [633]. The
ceasefire announced in mid-April contributed to a partial slowdown in large-scale movement, but mobility remains fluid and fragile.
Returns are limited or have reversed in South Lebanon, Nabatieh, the southern suburbs of Beirut and parts of the Bekaa due to ongoing
insecurity, infrastructure damage and UXO contamination, prompting secondary displacement back into shelters [635][631][651].
Renewed displacement orders and airstrikes in Nabatieh governorate on 4 May triggered a further wave of displacement that pushed
total internal displacement to over one million as of 11 May 2026 [665][635].
Humanitarian access remains constrained in southern governorates and Nabatieh due to damaged roads and bridges, UXO and
localized insecurity, increasing pressure on collective sites and host communities. Funding shortfalls in the Lebanon Flash
Appeal—approximately 42% funded as of mid-May—and the risk of pipeline breaks, particularly for health and WASH, are further
constraining the scale-up of assistance [669][651].
Living Conditions of the Displaced Population
Collective sites are overcrowded and often unsuitable for prolonged habitation, as many are schools or non-residential buildings.
Shelter partners have supported approximately 43,980 households with distributions totalling 125,724 mattresses, 165,598 blankets and
62,431 pillows, and are repairing or upgrading around 360 collective shelters (282 completed to date) to improve safety and privacy
[669]. MoSA is scaling up site management with IOM support; IOM has facilitated referrals for thousands of displaced people in
non-government-managed sites and distributed core relief items and emergency household supplies since early March
[647][633][632]. Shelter monitoring has identified thousands of IDPs with disabilities in collective shelters, including approximately 1,200
with physical disabilities, 689 with mental disabilities, 422 with hearing impairments and 388 with visual impairments, underscoring the
need for tailored and accessible services [667].
Attacks and damage to health infrastructure have sharply constrained services. WHO verified 158 attacks on health facilities, patients
and staff—resulting in 108 deaths and 249 injuries—as of 11 May, rising to 161 incidents (110 deaths, 252 injuries) with continued facility
closures by 14 May, including three hospitals and 41 primary healthcare centres operating at reduced capacity or closed [622][651].
Mobile medical teams are filling gaps: International Medical Corps reported 13,741 consultations and 35 mobile units reaching 125
shelters as of 11 May, though service continuity remains fragile and partners warn of pipeline breaks for essential medicines and
supplies [622][626].
WASH partners have provided assistance in approximately 611–615 collective shelters since 2 March, delivering 88,043 hygiene kits and
50,333 menstrual kits, supplying millions of litres of potable water and installing emergency sanitation facilities. Partners flag risks of
service interruptions and resource shortages if funding and access constraints persist [669][647][645].
Nutrition and maternal food security are deteriorating. Action Against Hunger and health partners report worsening infant and young
child feeding (IYCF) and maternal nutrition indicators. National estimates indicate approximately 12,200 pregnant women are
displaced, including 675 in collective shelters [640][667].

Protection needs remain high, with 77 protection, child protection and GBV partners reporting that they reached 192,600 IDPs by 14 May
(61% inside collective sites), while approximately 390,000 children remain displaced and GBV/child protection referral capacity outside

Drivers of Displacement
The primary drivers of displacement in Lebanon are ongoing conflict and insecurity, environmental damage, and economic hardship.
No large-scale or sustained returns have been observed in South Lebanon and Nabatieh, where Israeli-declared military zones,
unexploded ordnance (UXO), damaged roads, conflict debris, and continued insecurity prevent returns and complicate humanitarian
access [669][677]. Renewed airstrikes and evacuation orders in Nabatieh on 4 May exemplify how active hostilities continue to
generate precautionary and forced displacement [665][635][633][660][651].
Environmental damage from earlier phases of the conflict—including extensive debris, contamination of agricultural land and water
infrastructure, and tens of thousands of damaged or destroyed houses—constitutes a major structural obstacle to return and recovery
[677][671]. These conditions have also contributed to secondary displacement, with households initially returning later moving again
due to deteriorating conditions.
Economic pressures compound the situation: rising rental costs, increasing evictions and competition over limited services are driving
housing insecurity and straining host communities, limiting both local integration and return options [655][669]. Displaced households
face severe financial strain, growing reliance on borrowing and negative coping strategies, and urgent multipurpose cash needs
[655][651].
Children bear acute protection and mental-health impacts from prolonged displacement. UNICEF reported at least 59 children killed
or injured in a single week and estimated approximately 770,000 children experiencing heightened distress, with 72% of caregivers
reporting child anxiety in a 2025 rapid assessment [682]. Worsening food insecurity is projected, with 1.24 million people expected to
face Crisis (IPC Phase 3) or worse between April and August 2026 [665][638]. The Lebanon Flash Appeal was approximately 38–42%
funded (roughly US$117–129 million of US$308 million required), and the Ministry of Social Affairs requested a three-month extension
covering June–August 2026, reflecting the sustained and deepening humanitarian need [655][669][651].
Integration
The majority of displaced persons in Lebanon live outside collective shelters, in hosted, rented or informal arrangements, facing rising
rental costs and increased eviction risk, particularly in Beirut and Mount Lebanon [633][655]. Movement restrictions, lack of legal
residency and access constraints—including checkpoints, curfews and raids—in the North, Bekaa, Baalbek, South and Nabatieh limit
displaced refugees' mobility and access to services and livelihoods [655][669]. Constrained livelihood absorption into local labour
markets, combined with specific barriers for displaced households and women, reduces the effectiveness of cash-plus and training
interventions where market access and productive assets are lacking [650][655].
On education, OCHA reported partial restoration of services with over 800 public schools reopened for approximately 220,000 students.
As of 14 May, approximately 164,059 displaced children were accessing formal online education and 94,067 were attending in-person
classes [669][647]. The Ministry of Education and Higher Education (MEHE) has authorized non-national students to register for
national exams regardless of residency status, reflecting active institutional efforts to maintain access despite a strained environment
[647].
Cash support has been scaled up but remains insufficient. The Government-led Shock Responsive Social Safety Net disbursed
approximately USD 15.6 million to 138,000 households [669]. The 2026 Lebanon Flash Appeal had received approximately US$129 million
of US$308 million required (approximately 42% funded) as of mid-May, constraining longer-term integration and service expansion
[651]. Health and WASH services are being sustained via mobile medical teams, primary health centres and shelter-based WASH
interventions, but continuity is threatened by access constraints and funding shortfalls [647][651]. MoSA is leading site-management
scale-up with IOM support, while UNHCR co-leads the protection and shelter sectors with MoSA, reflecting active institutional
coordination in a challenging operating environment [647][655].
Humanitarian Access
Humanitarian Access Overview
Humanitarian partners, coordinated with national authorities and supported by the Humanitarian Notification System (HNS), maintain
operations and reach conflict-affected communities across southern Lebanon and other governorates including Beirut (Dahieh),
Mount Lebanon, Bekaa, the North, Baalbek, Nabatieh, Tyre and Marjayoun [700][713][698]. Key actors active in delivery include UN
agencies and coordination bodies (OCHA, WHO, IOM, UNHCR), international NGOs (e.g., International Medical Corps, ACF), local
partners and government ministries, with bilateral support via the EU Humanitarian Air Bridge and the Government of Luxembourg for
airlifts [701][708][704][703][698]. Modes of delivery include HNS-facilitated land movements, emergency airlifts (over 4,500 shelter
and household items airlifted on 11 May), mobile medical teams, water trucking and pre-positioning of supplies [713][698][704][700].
Despite these efforts, sustainability and service continuity remain at high risk without urgent funding and safer access conditions, as
partners continue to adapt response modalities to reach hard-to-reach and newly displaced populations [700][690][698].

Barriers to Humanitarian Access
Humanitarian access in Lebanon is increasingly constrained by active hostilities, an Israeli-declared military zone in the south,
movement restrictions (checkpoints, curfews, raids), and widespread infrastructure damage — including damaged roads and bridges,
conflict debris, and unexploded ordnance (UXO) contamination — with the South and Nabatieh governorates especially affected as of
mid-May 2026 [713][690][698]. These conditions have delayed and limited aid deliveries despite coordinated facilitation: the HNS
enabled 121–131 facilitated movements to communities across southern Lebanon between 2 March and mid-May [713][698]. Repeated
attacks on healthcare have directly impeded medical response; since 2 March, at least 161 incidents affecting healthcare have been
recorded, resulting in approximately 110 deaths and 252 injuries among health personnel, with multiple hospitals and primary
healthcare centres remaining closed [698][690]. Operational capacity is further strained by displacement of national staff,
fragmented referral pathways for secondary and specialized care, administrative and bureaucratic impediments, and critical funding
shortfalls — the 2026 Lebanon Flash Appeal was approximately 42% funded (around US$129 million of US$308 million required) as of
mid-May 2026 [713][700][704][690][698]. Service sustainability and supply-chain risks threaten continuity of health, WASH and food
assistance, with partners warning of potential WASH interruptions in collective shelters and pipeline breaks if conditions persist
[698][704].
Humanitarian Access Gaps
As of 11 May 2026, IOM's Displacement Tracking Matrix (DTM) reported over one million internally displaced persons in Lebanon,
including 121,701 people sheltering across 628 collective sites, while the majority remain outside formal sites in host communities,
rented accommodation or informal arrangements [722]. Access constraints are concentrated in southern Lebanon and Nabatieh
governorates, where UXO contamination, damaged roads and bridges, conflict debris and localized insecurity are delaying
humanitarian movements, hindering restoration of services and restricting access to return areas and other hard-to-reach locations
[713][722]. People living in tents, public spaces or other open areas — particularly women and girls, persons with disabilities and
approximately 390,000 displaced children — face the highest protection and basic-service deficits, including urgent needs for safe
accommodation, water and sanitation, and tailored support [700]. Key sectoral gaps identified between 5–14 May include
overstretched shelter capacity in collective sites and host communities, disrupted continuity of healthcare with limited access to
secondary and specialized services, and gaps in protection, GBV and child-protection referral pathways and services, all exacerbated
by fragmented systems and insufficient funding [713][700]. Regarding logistics, the Masnaa border crossing remained open and
operational and humanitarian cargo is exempted from transshipment requirements, but overall mobility and logistics remain fragile
[722].
Information, Communication and AAP
Information Landscape
Coordination is led by the Ministry of Social Affairs under the Lebanon Response Plan, with UNHCR co-leading the Inter-Sector
Coordination Group and co-leading the Protection, Shelter/Core Relief Items and Cash Working Group alongside government and NGO
partners, providing the primary structure for information collection and sharing [779]. Information systems are producing actionable
outputs — assessment reports, rapid health findings, and logistics reporting — though operational capacity and funding gaps
constrain consistency and scale. The Lebanon Flash Appeal targets USD 308.3 million to assist 1 million people but was only
approximately 38–42% funded as of mid-May, limiting partners' ability to sustain monitoring and response operations
[779][801][757][766].
Active, time-bound needs assessments are underway across sectors. UNHCR completed field data collection for a nationwide GBV
assessment (37 focus group discussions and 21 key informant interviews), with data collection closed on 8 May and publication
expected by end-May [779]. WHO reported that approximately 85% of the Rapid Health Assessment had been completed as of 14 May
2026, with preliminary findings to inform Ministry of Public Health decision-making [791]. International Medical Corps reported routine
monitoring of medical activity and mobile outreach, including 13,741 consultations and 35 mobile units reaching 125 shelters as of 11
May [781]. IOM/DTM produce regular mobility snapshots and rapid assessments — consulting 6,057 key informants across 1,229
locations as of 11 May — to inform operational planning [812][770].
The Logistics Cluster publishes live convoy schedules weekly, provides common storage and cargo consolidation services, and uses
the Log.ie platform to share real-time logistics constraints and telecommunications connectivity information with partners [769][763].

affected locations [763][757]. The Logistics Cluster's regional convoy service — including the first Amman–Lebanon convoy on 7 May —
further supports timely supply delivery, with real-time logistics and telecommunications data shared via the Log.ie platform
[769][763].

Digital platforms support information exchange and capacity-building: WHO delivered online training and a radio-nuclear hazards

Face-to-face channels remain central, with mobile medical teams, field visits, shelter-based workshops, and site management

Barriers to Information
Physical access constraints are a primary barrier to information delivery. Damage to infrastructure, localized insecurity, unexploded
ordnance contamination, and movement restrictions in the South and Nabatieh governorates are constraining humanitarian
movements and hindering the delivery of life-saving information alongside supplies [765][757][759]. Reduced humanitarian presence
at border points and limited capacity at arrival locations have further curtailed immediate assistance and information services for
returnees and newly arrived people [771][812].
A broader energy crisis is impairing telecommunications and other critical infrastructure, increasing the likelihood of communication
blackouts that would limit information flow to affected populations [800]. Telecommunications connectivity assessments were
finalized and indicate risks to operational continuity from power outages and connectivity disruptions, with initial funding planned to
cover only three months while additional resources are being mobilized [763].
Specific population groups face compounding barriers. Migrants and non-Arabic speakers encounter language barriers and concerns
over migration status that restrict their access to shelter, assistance, and reliable information, limiting their ability to request help or
evacuation assistance [775]. Education disruptions and limited access to remote learning exacerbate information gaps for children
and caregivers, particularly in active conflict zones and displacement settings [838]. Protection outreach has relied heavily on
one-way mass messaging and hotlines, while reach outside collective shelters remains limited at around 39%, indicating uneven
access to two-way information and complaints mechanisms [769][771].
Underfunding of the 2026 Lebanon Flash Appeal — approximately 42% funded as of mid-May — and critical funding shortfalls in the
health sector are reducing partners' capacity to sustain outreach, risk communication, and community engagement activities
[757][784][766].
Misinformation and Rumors
Misinformation and polarized narratives increased public anxiety following the ceasefire announcement, contributing to short-lived
returns and reversed displacement movements as of 11 May [769]. Social media and the rapid sharing of maps, videos, and alerts
have circulated both authentic and out-of-context content, prompting warnings that unverified posts can mislead residents about
local risk zones [780]. Misinformation has compounded displacement pressures and shelter demand amid renewed displacement
orders and fluid movement patterns observed across governorates [770][769]. National agency short dispatches are being used as
baseline information, but authorities and responders note these require field confirmation to avoid amplifying miscontextualized
reports [780].
In response, humanitarian actors have deployed mass communication and structured feedback channels. Protection partners
disseminated one-way protection messages reaching approximately 90,000 people and processed about 21,000 protection feedback
and complaints via hotlines as of 11 May [769]. WHO is developing an all-hazard Risk Communication and Community Engagement
(RCCE) and infodemic management strategy for Lebanon, and deployed a regional consultant in April to establish a multisectoral
MHPSS operational framework, with missions underway to strengthen safeguarding and GBV/PRSEAH integration as of 14 May
[761][791]. The Emergency Preparedness Working Group convened a thematic session on communication products, referral systems,
and hotlines, though gaps persist in multilingual harmonization — notably in Farsi — and alignment of referral mechanisms [771].
Accountability Needs and Transparency
Community-based accountability mechanisms are operational but unevenly accessible. Nineteen of 21 Community Development
Centres remained active, providing outreach, service information, and feedback and complaint mechanisms, reaching 9,724
displaced people (63% women and girls) since March [779]. Protection, child protection, and GBV partners reported reaching 192,600
internally displaced persons, with community outreach and information sessions reaching 89,651 people on protection, safety, and
access to services [763]. Emergency Rapid Needs Assessments and other data collection processes are being used to inform
response prioritization and planning [769].
The Health Sector and WHO are scaling up RCCE efforts, including a Lebanon case study on integrated NCD and MHPSS community
engagement to improve awareness and participation [766][761]. However, systemic gaps persist: a formative Global Health Cluster
evaluation found that accountability to affected populations remains weak, with few mechanisms enabling affected people to
meaningfully influence decision-making [754]. Protection monitoring similarly reports that complaints and feedback mechanisms
remain unevenly accessible, particularly for marginalized groups outside collective shelters [755].

Ongoing insecurity, movement restrictions, and infrastructure damage — compounded by significant funding shortfalls, with the 2026

Legal and Policy
Legal and Policy Framework
Despite the ceasefire that started on 17 April, Israeli airstrikes, demolitions and renewed displacement orders continued across
southern Lebanon, undermining humanitarian access and prospects for safe returns [873][852]. UN agencies and the Humanitarian
Coordinator reiterated that parties are bound by international humanitarian law to protect civilians, medical personnel and
humanitarian infrastructure and to facilitate rapid, unimpeded humanitarian passage [873][857]. Lebanon has sought to consolidate
the ceasefire into a more sustainable agreement and requested US pressure on Israel to stop attacks and demolitions during
Washington-hosted talks [876]. A draft declaration of intent circulated for Lebanon–Israel negotiations foresees Israeli withdrawal
from Lebanese territory, handover of occupied areas to the Lebanese Armed Forces (LAF), LAF deployment with US/international
training and support, and references UNIFIL's mandate ending on 31 December 2026 [895][891]. The Lebanese National Council for
Scientific Research published a report covering October 2023–December 2024 estimating about US$25 billion in damage, and the
Lebanese Minister of the Environment formally accused Israel of ecocide [904][906]. Commentators and experts have invoked
international accountability mechanisms, noting ICC arrest warrants issued in November 2024 and ICJ interim measures from January
2024 in discussions on alleged violations and accountability [930]. Humanitarian coordination mechanisms — including OCHA-led
inter-sector coordination, the Lebanon Response Plan/Flash Appeal and the Humanitarian Notification System — continue to prioritise
response planning and to facilitate humanitarian movements to hard-to-reach areas despite access constraints and funding
shortfalls [864][852][857].
Humanitarian Impact of Legislation
The Ministry of Social Affairs (MoSA) leads the Lebanon Response Plan, while UNHCR co-leads multiple sectors — including protection,
shelter/core relief items and cash — and retains overall responsibility for the refugee response, embedding humanitarian coordination
within government-led frameworks [851]. Lebanon's main social protection instrument, the National Poverty Targeting Programme
administered by MoSA, was expanded after 2019 to deliver direct cash support and remains the primary formal channel shaping
access to assistance, while local political affiliation and fragmented systems continue to influence entitlements and distribution
[859][865]. Movement restrictions — checkpoints, curfews and raids — together with lack of legal residency are reported to constrain
mobility for displaced refugees and Lebanese in the North, Bekaa, Baalbek and parts of the South, increasing eviction and access risks
[851][867]. Arrivals and returnees frequently face missing, damaged or lost personal IDs, residency papers and family records, which
hinder access to services and require administrative and legal support to replace [883][867]. Border control measures further affect
cross-border assistance and population movements: Masnaa and Al Qaa remain operational, Aarida is partially open to pedestrians
only, Abboudiyeh's reopening was postponed until 1 July, and Jisr Qmar/Bouqaia was only partially and unofficially reopened for
limited pedestrian movement [866]. Humanitarian access is increasingly constrained by declared military zones, infrastructure
damage, unexploded ordnance contamination, GPS spoofing and rapidly changing route conditions; the Humanitarian Notification
System has facilitated over 100 coordinated movements to deliver aid since March, but operational windows remain narrow and
unpredictable [857][852]. Attacks on healthcare and medical personnel have been repeatedly recorded since 2 March, undermining
protection of medical services and prompting UN calls for respect for international humanitarian law and unimpeded humanitarian
passage [864][852][873].
International Agreements Impacting the Crisis
US-mediated direct Lebanon–Israel negotiations convened in Washington in mid-May, with the Lebanese delegation led by Simon
Karam (joined by Ambassador Nada Moawad and military attaché Brig. Gen. Oliver Hakmeh) and an Israeli delegation headed by
Ambassador Yechiel Leiter and military representatives [882][856][907]. Lebanon framed its negotiating priorities as an effective
ceasefire, full Israeli withdrawal, return of displaced persons, release of prisoners, deployment of the Lebanese Armed Forces and
reconstruction, while Israel's agenda prioritized neutralizing Hezbollah and securing border protections [870][850][876][907]. The
ceasefire that began on 17 April remained fragile, with Lebanese authorities reporting cumulative fatalities in the mid- to high-2,800s
and recurrent violations during the Washington talks [873][858][849]. UNIFIL and the prospect of expanded Lebanese Armed Forces
deployment south of the Litani were central to proposed implementation arrangements, with Lebanon presenting legal texts and UN
resolutions — including Resolution 1701 — to underpin any stabilization package [856][907][876]. UN Humanitarian Coordinator Imran
Riza and UN agencies repeatedly called for protection of civilians, respect for international humanitarian law and rapid, unimpeded
humanitarian access during the negotiations [873][853]. On the humanitarian side, an EU/Luxembourg airlift delivered more than
4,500 emergency relief items on 11 May, and partners reported large-scale food and shelter assistance including over 9.2 million meals
distributed, even as funding and access constraints limited further scale-up [878][857]. The UN's $308 million Lebanon emergency
appeal (March–May 2026) was reported approximately 41–42% funded as of mid-May, constraining longer-term government-led
response and recovery planning [903][857][868].
Limitations of Current Policies
Humanitarian access and civilian protection in Lebanon are being undermined by sustained attacks on health services, widespread
infrastructure damage and route insecurity, concentrated access constraints in southern Lebanon (South and Nabatieh), fragmented
referral systems, and severe funding shortfalls that together reduce service continuity and operational adaptability
[864][852][857][878][868].

Food Security and Livelihoods
Access to Food and Market Functionality
Acute food insecurity in Lebanon is projected to affect approximately 1.24 million people between April and August 2026, up from about
874,000 in the November 2025–March 2026 period, driven by escalating hostilities, mass displacement, income losses and rising costs
[945][959]. Markets remain operational in several cities but high prices, transport disruptions and local breakdowns are preventing
many households from buying essential food, while currency depreciation and fuel price increases have eroded purchasing power
and pushed food prices higher [945]. WHO and health partners note that food affordability is the principal constraint and that nutrition
risks are rising—particularly for infants, young children, pregnant and lactating women—amid limited dietary diversity and reduced
meal frequency in displaced and host households [959][951].
Growing access constraints, infrastructure damage and insecurity—particularly in South and Nabatieh governorates—are disrupting
supply chains and humanitarian operations, prompting partners to preposition food and adapt delivery modalities to sustain
assistance in hard-to-reach areas [958][948][970]. The food security sector is engaging with authorities on measures such as the
Government-led Shock Responsive Social Safety Net (SRSN), but sustained funding shortfalls and operational constraints are limiting
the scale and sustainability of responses [970].

Coping Mechanisms
Households across Lebanon are reducing meal quality and frequency, skipping meals, delaying care, selling goods and exhausting
savings, and increasingly depending on humanitarian assistance [945][948]. Displaced and host households report growing reliance
on borrowing and accumulating debt as a coping strategy, reinforcing cyclical financial vulnerability [972][971]. Evaluations of cash
and cash-plus programming in Lebanon indicate that cash often provides short-term relief but does not remove underlying debt
cycles or secure durable livelihoods without complementary market access and sustained support [971].
Protection actors report rising use of negative and harmful coping mechanisms, including increased risks of child labour, and note that
roughly 390,000 children remain displaced, heightening child protection concerns [970][948]. Some southern districts—Bent Jbeil,
Marjayoun, Nabatiyah and Tyre—show 55–65% of the population at high food-insecurity levels, with about 10% in the emergency phase
[945][967]. Humanitarian partners have delivered large-scale food assistance and protection cash grants, but coverage gaps and
funding shortfalls persist, limiting the reach and sustainability of support [948][970].
Livelihood Disruption
Escalation of hostilities and economic shock have driven widespread job losses and business closures in Lebanon, with Economy
Minister Amer Bisat estimating an economic loss of around 7% of GDP as companies close and people lose work [978]. Hospitality and
service businesses report catastrophic revenue declines (one restaurateur cited a ~90% drop), while traders have closed and many
employees and daily workers cannot reach workplaces, eliminating regular income for large numbers of households [978][945]. High
fuel and power costs, plus generator and transport expense increases, are raising operating costs and reducing hours and
productivity for businesses and informal workers [978][970].
Displacement of approximately 1.2 million people has reduced household income streams as families shelter in schools or rent out
properties without replacement earnings, with savings exhausted and growing reliance on borrowing and negative coping strategies
[978][945][972]. According to IOM's Displacement Tracking Matrix (DTM, 11 May 2026), returnees and arrivals face very limited and
inadequate income: 57% of assessed arrival locations reported returnees not engaged in any employment; where work exists it is
largely informal, with daily wage labour (75%), salaried employment (39%) and agriculture/livestock (38%) the most cited sources;
most key informants judged income insufficient (51% not sufficient at all; 47% only partially sufficient), and remittances (19%) and
humanitarian assistance (5%) covered only a minority of locations [955][973].
The 2026 tourism season has effectively collapsed, removing a major source of seasonal and informal employment—including drivers,
cleaners, suppliers, guides, artisans and other short-term workers—and threatening permanent closure of small hotels, guesthouses
and related service providers [974].
Impact on Agricultural Activities and Production
Assessments estimate direct damage to Lebanon's agricultural sector at USD 118 million, with losses of USD 586 million (total damage
and losses exceeding USD 700 million) and reconstruction and recovery needs of USD 263 million, of which USD 95 million are
prioritised for 2025–2026 (Ministry of Agriculture / UN assessments) [982]. The Ministry of Agriculture has estimated some 54,000
hectares of agricultural land directly affected (about 22.5% of the country's 250,000 ha of agricultural land), while UNDP identified more
than 12,000 hectares of abandoned agricultural land in Nabatiyah and southern Lebanon, and roughly 76% of farmers have been
displaced [982][988].
18
Irrigation and water-collection infrastructure have been damaged, including networks linked to the Litani Authority and the destruction
of about 20 local water collection basins and their associated networks, constraining irrigation-dependent production and recovery
[982]. Assessments also report the destruction of agricultural support infrastructure—about 23 cooperatives, 16 olive mills and over 200
nurseries—alongside crop and orchard losses including 134 ha of olive groves, 48 ha of citrus, 44 ha of banana and 15 ha of other fruit
trees [982]. Beekeeping and livestock were directly affected: an estimated 1,500 hives destroyed, about 10% of animals lost and a
further 30% sold at reduced prices in southern governorates, eroding productive capital and slowing recovery prospects for
households [982].
Field reports indicate greenhouses have been damaged, agricultural roads are dangerous, and many farmers have abandoned their
land, limiting access for planting, tending and harvesting and threatening upcoming seasons [945][982]. Restoration and return to
production are further constrained by damage to small- and medium-scale irrigation installations and the need for parts, fuel,
technicians and secure access to repair and operate pumps and networked systems, undermining prospects for timely recovery of
livelihoods and seasonal crops [982].
Food and Livelihood Needs and Current Response
Since the escalation on 2 March 2026, humanitarian partners have delivered large-scale in-kind food assistance, reporting over 9.2
million hot and cold meals and 120,936 ready-to-eat (RTE) rations as of 14 May 2026 [970][948]. Assistance modalities in use include
hot/cold meal distributions, RTE kits and food parcels, multipurpose cash assistance, targeted protection cash grants (1,796 grants
reported), pre-positioning of supplies, and a planned self-cooking pilot in 10 shelters in the first week of June 2026 [970][948].
Coverage outside collective shelters remains limited: protection actors note only 39% of people reached to date were supported
outside formal sites, indicating underserved populations in host-community and informal settings [972].
Nutrition actors report substantive coverage gaps, with 38% of collective shelters lacking nutrition support and major gaps
concentrated in Beirut and Mount Lebanon; ACF screening and outreach reached 404 children and pregnant/lactating women and
732 people through awareness sessions, while isolated sites (Rmeish, Ain Ebel) have at least 200 children aged 0–3 identified as
needing nutrition support [949][951]. Livelihoods and cash-plus programming provide short-term stabilisation but face limits:
evaluations from Lebanon show cash eased immediate consumption needs yet rarely translated into sustained income without
integrated market-linked support and post-training income assistance, while labour-market constraints meant many training
placements did not yield continued employment [971].
Operational constraints and funding shortfalls are limiting scale and sustainability, with insecurity, movement restrictions and
infrastructure damage impeding reach and the 2026 Lebanon Flash Appeal approximately 42% funded (around US$129 million of
US$308 million) as of mid-May 2026 [970][944]. Coordination mechanisms are active: sectors are engaging with the Ministry of Social
Affairs on a possible government-led Shock Responsive Social Safety Net, using the Humanitarian Notification System to facilitate
humanitarian movements, and consulting the Food Safety Task Force to operationalise shelter self-cooking and approvals [970].
Education
Learning Environment and Facility Conditions
As of 11 May 2026, 448 educational institutions (332 public, 67 private, 49 TVET) remained unavailable for educational use because they
were sheltering internally displaced persons [1013]. Hundreds of schools in southern Lebanon have been destroyed or repurposed as
collective shelters, with UNESCO estimating 339 schools in warzones and hundreds converted to shelters, intensifying disruptions to
learning since March 2026 [1010]. More than 800 public schools had reopened for over 220,000 students, about 100 operated in hybrid
mode serving approximately 27,000 students, and over 580 public schools were delivering remote learning to more than 116,000
students, including 320+ in conflict-affected areas [1013]. A significant participation gap remained for afternoon-shift students, with
only 23% of enrolled students in remote afternoon learning actively using the platform, partially because national education
broadcasts run 07:30–14:00 and exclude the afternoon shift [1013][1011]. Teacher capacity is strained: salaries have been devalued by
about 80% and roughly 30% of the sector left the country or changed professions since 2019, with many teachers further displaced by
recent hostilities [1010]. Continuity of online learning remains constrained by poor connectivity and limited device access, and
education-in-emergencies measures include distribution of education kits, approval for partners to run learning activities inside
shelters, and MHPSS and staff well-being training for frontline education responders [1011][1013].
Access to Education and Barriers
Since March, Israeli attacks have displaced more than 1.2 million people in Lebanon, including an estimated 500,000 school-aged
children, with many families remaining in collective shelters (approximately 125,000 people, including roughly 44,800 children)
[1010][1031][1014]. According to UNESCO, about 100 schools are in high-risk areas that could soon become inaccessible, on top of the
hundreds already converted to shelters or located in active warzones [1010]. Key barriers to enrollment and regular attendance include
poor connectivity, intermittent electricity and lack of devices for remote learning, widespread use of school buildings as shelters,
ongoing insecurity and movement restrictions (checkpoints, curfews), and acute affordability pressures that force families to choose
between basic needs and education — contributing to rising dropouts, child labour, and early marriage as harmful coping
mechanisms [1010][1014][1031]. Psychosocial distress and learning loss are widespread, with UNICEF estimating some 770,000 children
experiencing increased psychological distress and reporting that, since the ceasefire, dozens of children have been killed or injured —
factors that further hinder return to school and sustained attendance [1016]. MEHE has prioritized in-person learning, approved
partners to deliver learning activities within shelters, maintained registration for national examinations (open until 15 May), and
confirmed that Syrian and other non-national students may register for intermediate and secondary certificate examinations upon
submission of certified educational documents [1011][1013].

The tutoring hotline (Call&Learn) recorded a sharp increase in use — approximately 13,018 children accessed it since 1 May, a 400%

Education Needs and Current Response
By 14 May 2026, approximately 164,059 internally displaced children had accessed formal education online, 94,067 attended in-person
classes, and 4,222 were enrolled in non-formal education programmes [1011]. The Ministry of Education and Higher Education (MEHE)
activated online accounts for 390,000 students and 40,000 teachers to support continuity of learning [1013]. Education partners
distributed 1,694 education kits reaching roughly 84,700 children, provided 764 individual student kits and learning materials, and
supported 49 children with disabilities with assistive devices and/or rehabilitation and therapy services [1011]. Partners are also
delivering targeted trainings for frontline staff on well-being, inclusive approaches, psychosocial support, and referral pathways, while
UNESCO, UNICEF, and UN/sector partners have promoted measures such as opening multiple shifts, establishing temporary learning
centres, integrating psychosocial and mental health services, and scaling safe spaces and community-based MHPSS to mitigate
learning loss and trauma [1011][1010][1016][1014]. Key gaps include the large number of schools still used as shelters (limiting access to
safe, functional classrooms), pronounced participation shortfalls for afternoon shifts, limited connectivity and devices, insufficient
learning hubs and teacher training, and inadequate coverage in hard-to-reach areas; continued donor support is needed to scale up
learning hubs, trainings, and distribution of learning materials [1011][1013].
Health
Availability and Access to Healthcare Services
Health service availability remains severely constrained, with three hospitals and 41–42 primary health care centres (PHCCs) closed,
six PHCCs providing only restricted (chronic/emergency) services, and six hospitals yet to resume delivery services — limiting maternal
care in Tyre, Bint Jbeil, Hasbaya and Nabatieh for almost two months [1066][1076][1069][1071]. Damaged infrastructure, limited health
staffing, gaps in essential medicines and reduced maternal and emergency services are overburdening remaining functional facilities
[1067][1069]. Humanitarian access and referral pathways are increasingly constrained by UXO contamination, damaged roads and
bridges, conflict debris and localised insecurity, complicating patient transport and secondary referrals; HNS-facilitated movements
(121 movements to 11 May) and IOM transport support (1,766 people transported to PHCs) have been used to reach hard-to-reach
areas [1072][1084][1074].
Outreach and mobile modalities are sustaining access where possible. Partners report 211 outreach units linked to 185 PHCCs, and 35
mobile medical teams reached 125 shelters and community sites delivering 13,741 consultations and other services as of mid-May
[1076][1073][1080]. Maternal and child services remain disrupted: outreach recorded 4,756 antenatal consultations, but deliveries have
decreased at some hospitals (e.g., Baalbek Governmental Hospital), and displaced pregnant women in shelters face suboptimal meal
frequency; an estimated 12,200 pregnant women are displaced nationwide based on March–May assessments
[1076][1066][1085][1070].
A Rapid Health Sector Partners Capacity Assessment conducted in late April found 11% of partners at high risk and 53% at moderate risk
of service interruptions within 4–8 weeks absent new funding, underscoring the fragility of service continuity [1066]. The Health Sector
and MoPH have called for urgent replenishment of essential medicines, trauma kits and EmONC items and requested an additional
USD 37 million to avert further deterioration, while WHO's regional emergency appeal remains critically underfunded at approximately
13% [1066][1070].
Disease Outbreaks
Displacement, overcrowding in collective shelters, damaged sanitation infrastructure and disrupted WASH services are increasing the
risk of communicable disease transmission across Lebanon [1072][1085][1070]. Field assessments report high prevalence of acute
diarrhoea, respiratory infections, eye infections and skin conditions among infants and young children in shelters, driven by damp,
unhygienic and overcrowded living conditions [1085]. No major disease outbreaks were reported in shelters as of mid-May, though
partners note intermittent food-poisoning incidents and persistent outbreak risk in overcrowded sites with compromised WASH and
limited services [1070]. Regional outbreaks — notably measles and hepatitis A in neighbouring Syria — linked to immunity gaps,
population movement and overcrowding represent additional cross-border risk that WHO notes could affect Lebanon's vulnerable
displaced populations [1067].
Health authorities and the Health Sector have called for expanded facility- and community-based surveillance, strengthened risk
communication and rapid response capacity. WHO and partners are supporting surveillance, rapid response teams and risk
communication activities, while Rapid Health Assessment data are being finalised (approximately 85% complete by mid-May) to
guide response planning [1066][1069]. Vaccination and outreach efforts are ongoing: MoPH-linked outreach through 211 primary
outreach units reported 9,840 IDP children vaccinated and 296,215 primary care consultations, with 110,069 IDPs receiving medications
and 48,851 supported for chronic medications as of 14 May 2026 [1076]. IOM and partners also report 10,651 TB screenings and 14,614
health-awareness contacts in displacement settings to support early detection of communicable threats [1084]. Mobile medical
teams continue routine vaccination and service delivery at shelters, with International Medical Corps reporting 262 routine
vaccinations across 35 mobile units reaching 125 sites by mid-May [1073][1080].
WASH partners have scaled shelter support — including distribution of hygiene and menstrual kits and millions of litres of potable
water — but critical sanitation gaps and funding shortfalls limit the ability to fully mitigate public-health risks in collective sites
[1072][1077]. The health system's capacity to prevent and contain outbreaks is under severe strain, with multiple hospitals and roughly
41–42 PHCs closed or partially functional and shortages of essential medicines and diagnostics constraining outbreak response
[1066][1069][1076].

Health System Challenges
Health service delivery remained severely constrained in mid-May 2026, with damaged health infrastructure, limited staffing and
ongoing insecurity restricting access to care and increasing pressure on functional facilities [1067]. Multiple hospitals sustained
physical damage (16 establishments reported), and dozens of PHCCs, pharmacies and pharmaceutical warehouses were damaged
or closed, further degrading service capacity and supply storage [1069][1087].
Attacks on healthcare have continued despite the ceasefire: since the onset of hostilities on 2 March, the Health Sector recorded
155–161 attacks on health care resulting in 104–110 deaths and 244–252 injuries among health-care workers, with continued
post-ceasefire incidents including the killing of paramedics on 12 May [1066][1076][1103][1072]. Health partners reported critical
shortages of trauma and surgical supplies, blood bank capacity, trained personnel, essential medicines including chronic disease
treatments, diagnostics and emergency equipment [1067][1069][1070]. Humanitarian access and referral pathways were further
constrained by UXO contamination, damaged roads and bridges, and localised insecurity, delaying secondary and specialised
referrals to functioning facilities [1074][1072].
Funding shortfalls are acute: the 2026 Lebanon Flash Appeal was approximately 42% funded (roughly USD 129 million of USD 308 million
required) as of mid-May, and WHO's USD 30.3 million emergency appeal remained critically underfunded at approximately 13%,
limiting partners' capacity to maintain and scale essential health and WASH services [1077][1070]. WHO and partners are supporting 14
emergency medical teams (36 deployed personnel) across 21 frontline hospitals, prepositioning trauma and emergency surgery kits
and ICU ventilators, and providing NCD medication substitution guidance and trainings to sustain continuity of care [1067][1069].
Mental Health and Psychosocial Support (MHPSS)
Protection and health partners have scaled MHPSS delivery significantly in mid-May 2026. Sector figures indicate 64,976 people
participated in MHPSS interventions as of 11 May, rising to 68,528 as of 14 May; UNHCR provided MHPSS to 4,971 individuals during 4–10
May, and IOM reached 4,159 people with MHPSS since 2 March [1072][1076][1079][1084]. WHO has supported inpatient care for severe
mental health conditions for 38 patients since 2 March (34 Lebanese, 2 Syrian refugees, 2 migrant workers) and co-chairs the MHPSS
Coordination Group with the Ministry of Public Health/National Mental Health Programme [1069].
Community-based MHPSS is provided through Community Development Centres (CDCs), collective shelters and outreach by trained
social workers, counsellors and psychologists — 19 of 21 CDCs remained operational and CDCs have reached 9,724 displaced people
since 2 March [1079]. The National Mental Health Programme and partners are delivering self-care and burnout-prevention trainings
for frontline workers across governorates in May (eight sessions), and WHO conducted a mental health professional training on 13 May
for 110 participants with another session scheduled for 16 May [1066][1067].
Children and other vulnerable groups face acute mental health needs: UNICEF estimates approximately 770,000 children are
experiencing increased psychological distress; partners reported 978 persons with disabilities reached with specialised support; and
shelter monitoring records include 689 IDPs reported with mental disabilities in collective sites [1065][1068][1076][1069]. Hotline and
remote support mechanisms remain active, including the Ministry of Public Health mental health support hotline (1564), a national
suicide prevention hotline, and NGO-operated free emotional-support hotlines such as the IRC's "National Lifeline" [1069][1066][1092].
Despite these efforts, agencies warn that needs far outstrip available resources and that staffing shortages and funding gaps risk
service interruptions, limiting the ability to reach all people in need [1068][1070][1076][1077].
Health Needs and Current Response
The Health Sector urgently calls for protection of medical personnel and rapid, flexible funding. An additional USD 37 million is required
under the Lebanon Flash Appeal (March–May 2026) to sustain emergency health operations and prevent further deterioration of
public health outcomes [1066]. The Flash Appeal was approximately 42% funded as of mid-May, and WHO's USD 30.3 million
emergency appeal remained critically underfunded at approximately 13%, constraining the ability to replenish supplies, scale trauma
and maternal services, and prevent pipeline breaks [1070][1077].
Primary care outreach is being maintained at scale: 211 outreach units linked to 185 PHCCs reported 296,215 consultations, 110,069 IDPs
receiving medications, support to 48,851 chronic patients, 9,840 child vaccinations and 4,756 antenatal consultations as of 11–14 May
[1076]. International Medical Corps mobile teams (35 units) reached 125 shelters and community sites, providing 13,741 consultations,
7,213 acute treatments, 1,596 chronic disease services, 262 routine vaccinations, nutrition screening and MHPSS support [1073][1080].
WHO and partners are supporting 14 emergency medical teams (36 deployed personnel) across 21 hospitals, supplying trauma and
surgical kits, ICU ventilators, and technical guidance including an NCD medication substitution guide and trainings to sustain trauma,
NCD, maternal and critical care services [1067][1069].
Critical gaps persist in essential medicines and supplies (trauma kits, EmONC items), blood bank capacity, diagnostics, trained
surgical and emergency personnel, and weakened referral pathways. Eleven percent of partners are at high risk and 53% at moderate
risk of service interruption within 4–8 weeks absent new funding [1066][1067][1070]. Coordination is led by the Ministry of Public Health
with Health Sector and Public Health Emergency Operations Centre support, with active coordination between government, UN
agencies and NGOs to sustain mobile outreach and facility reactivation [1072][1067][1073].

Nutrition Needs and Current Response
Acute food insecurity is rising sharply: an IPC projection estimates approximately 1.24 million people in Lebanon will face acute food
insecurity between April and August 2026, with Crisis (IPC Phase 3) outcomes expected to persist across multiple governorates amid
disrupted markets and livelihoods [1121][1138]. Nutrition needs are especially severe among infants, young children, and pregnant and
lactating women, with IYCF practices deeply compromised as documented by ACF assessments conducted between March and May
2026 [1116][1117].
The Nutrition Sector and partners have mounted a multi-pronged response since the onset of escalations. Emergency nutrition rations
and micronutrient supplementation reached 19,869 children under five, adolescent girls, and pregnant and lactating women
[1118][1120]. Targeted infant feeding support scaled up notably between 11 and 14 May: unbranded infant formula distributed rose from
948 tins provided to 113 of 567 assessed infants to 1,277 tins provided to 154 of 610 assessed infants, and BMS safety kits increased from
70 to 84 over the same period [1118][1120]. IYCF hotline activity also expanded during this period, with calls rising from 481 to 522 and
referrals from 357 to 394, while caregivers reached through counselling and awareness activities grew from 10,508 to 11,799 [1118][1120].
Key gaps continue to undermine the response. Coverage outside collective shelters remains limited, and 38% of collective shelters still
lack nutrition services [1116][1118][1120]. Closures and reduced functionality of health facilities constrain maternal and child nutrition
services [1121][1131]. Most critically, major funding shortfalls persist: approximately US$129 million of the US$308 million Flash Appeal had
been received as of mid-May, threatening the continuity and scale-up of nutrition interventions [1118][1120][1121][1131].
WASH
Availability of Water Sources
WASH partners have sustained public water supply by providing 803,000 litres of fuel to Water Establishments—supporting an
estimated 656,973 people—and by supporting 256 water stations with fuel, repairs and spare parts to maintain network functionality
under increased demand [1161]. Partners also delivered 3,765,725 litres of potable water and 76,090 m³ through water trucking to sites
with limited or disrupted supply [1161]. WASH coverage across open collective shelters was reported at 96.7%, and since 2 March
partners have delivered WASH assistance in 611–615 collective shelters [1166][1161].
Environmental assessments documented damage to irrigation networks and the destruction of approximately 20 local water
collection basins, and warned that debris and pollutants from strikes risk contaminating wells and groundwater, complicating safe
water access in affected areas [1192]. Overcrowded shelters with water leakage, blocked drainage and sewage contamination have
been identified as further threats to safe water access, driving repeated infections and raising malnutrition risks among infants and
young children [1173].
Funding shortfalls—with the Flash Appeal approximately 42% funded by mid-May—and access constraints including insecurity,
damaged roads, UXO and infrastructure damage were flagged as major risks to sustaining public water systems and water provision

Waste Management and Vector-Borne Disease Risks
Assessments and media reporting note large volumes of rubble containing asbestos, paints, solvents, heavy metals and other
hazardous materials, with dust generation and contaminant migration posing risks to soil, wells and people involved in clean-up and

during clearance and reconstruction operations [1212][1192][1197].
No major disease outbreaks have been reported in shelters to date, though health partners warn that overcrowding, limited water
access and inadequate sanitation sustain significant risk for waterborne and vector-borne disease transmission [1167][1166].
Main WASH Needs and Humanitarian Response
Humanitarian actors identify urgent needs to restore public water supply, rehabilitate and expand sanitation infrastructure in
collective shelters and return areas, and scale up hygiene promotion and water quality interventions to reduce disease risk in
overcrowded sites [1166][1161][1175]. Immediate operational priorities cited by partners and authorities include installation of water
filtration systems, urgent fuel support for water systems, and rehabilitation and expansion of sanitation facilities, with WHO and others
supporting water safety monitoring, filter repairs and installation, and hygiene promotion in shelters [1166][1196].
These combined interventions—water trucking, fuel provision, station repairs, latrine and shower installation, emergency desludging, kit
distributions and hygiene education—have maintained high WASH coverage in collective shelters (96.7%) and reduced reliance on
costlier emergency modalities by sustaining network functionality for hundreds of thousands of people [1166][1161][1196].
Scaling the response faces major constraints: critical gaps in sanitation infrastructure identified by baseline assessments, continuous
population movements increasing pressure on services, access and logistics impeded by insecurity, damaged roads and UXO
(especially in southern Lebanon and Nabatieh), and funding shortfalls with the Flash Appeal only approximately 42% funded by
mid-May, putting WASH services at risk of interruption [1166][1165][1160][1162].

Damage to Housing and Shelter Needs
Since the truce, 10,632 housing units have been registered as affected—about 5,386 completely destroyed and 5,246 partially
damaged—and earlier CNRSL data identified 37,836 housing units damaged or destroyed between 2 March and 7 April 2026 [1232].
Many damaged dwellings have lost roofs, windows and basic services (water, electricity) or have structural cracks, rendering
otherwise standing houses uninhabitable [1232]. More than one million people remain displaced overall, with roughly 129,700 people
sheltering in some 632 collective sites as of midMay, while the vast majority of displaced households are hosted outside formal
shelters in rented accommodation, with host families or informal arrangements [1222][1224]. Collective sites host high numbers of
vulnerable people, including approximately 44,800 children, 6,700 older persons and 2,699 people with disabilities, with further
disaggregation indicating about 1,200 people with physical disabilities, 689 with mental disabilities, 422 with hearing impairments and
388 with visual impairments [1230]. Health partners report inadequate meal frequency among displaced pregnant women in
collective shelters, and assessments estimate some 12,200 pregnant women are currently displaced nationwide [1230][1234].
Reconstruction and safe return are constrained by access restrictions (including the socalled "Yellow Line"), unexploded ordnance
contamination, damaged roads and bridges, loss of ownership documentation complicating claims, and limited financing and
materials for repairs [1232][1226][1222]. Schools remain used as collective shelters and multiple health facilities remain
nonoperational—including three hospitals and 42 primary healthcare centres reported closed—reducing options for durable shelter
and essential services in return areas [1252][1222]. Repeated, fluid movements and secondary displacements are increasing
overcrowding, protection risks and the complexity of targeting shelter interventions and tracking needs across governorates
[1230][1226].

Living conditions in collective sites remain overcrowded and substandard, with reports of damp rooms, poor ventilation, sewage

since 2 March, with partners providing assistance in some 611–615 collective shelters, distributing 88,043 hygiene kits, 50,333 menstrual
hygiene kits and 1,387 cleaning kits, delivering approximately 3,765,725 litres of potable water, and installing 85 external latrines and 314
external showers, with shelter WASH coverage reported at roughly 96.7% where services are maintained [1226][1244][1222].

Factors Driving protection risks
Ongoing airstrikes and hostilities across southern Lebanon, the Bekaa Valley and Beirut's southern suburbs continue to be the primary
driver of civilian casualties, displacement and protection risks [1283]. Legal vulnerability and lack of civil documentation are limiting
mobility, access to services and legal protection for displaced populations and returnees, with missing or damaged documentation
reported as a frequent barrier at arrival and return locations [1311][1273][1268].
Severe economic deprivation, disrupted livelihoods and depleted savings are forcing negative coping mechanisms—including rising
child labour, increased street-based work, and early marriage—while also increasing GBV and child protection concerns and
exacerbating malnutrition risks among infants, young children and pregnant and lactating women [1268][1269][1293]. The breakdown
of social structures and overcrowded, inadequate accommodation conditions further compound risks for the most vulnerable groups,
including persons with disabilities, older persons, women and girls, separated children, and people outside formal shelters [1279][1268].
Limited humanitarian reach, operational constraints in parts of the South and Nabatieh, and critical funding shortfalls—with the
Lebanon Flash Appeal approximately 38–42% funded in midMay—are reducing protection coverage outside collective shelters and
hindering scaleup of durable solutions and specialized services [1273][1274][1291].
Psychological and mental health impacts
According to UNICEF, an estimated 770,000 children in Lebanon are experiencing heightened psychological distress from repeated
exposure to violence, loss and displacement. UNICEF's 2025 Child-focused Rapid Assessment found 72% of caregivers reported their
children were anxious or nervous and 62% reported children were depressed or sad [1276][1287]. Displacement, overcrowding in
collective shelters and disrupted basic services are further aggravating psychosocial needs across affected communities [1278][1279].
Children repeatedly displaced or exposed to attacks, as well as displaced persons with pre-existing vulnerabilities including people
with mental disabilities, face elevated risk of chronic mental health conditions [1276][1287][1279][1310].
The International Rescue Committee reported widespread household reports of overwhelming anxiety, grief and depression and
warned of declining funding for lifesaving mental health services, underscoring a critical gap between needs and available resources
[1310][1306]. Health and humanitarian actors report ongoing MHPSS delivery but major gaps and constrained coverage due to facility
closures, attacks on health care and limited funding [1289][1268][1306]. The National Mental Health Programme and WHO are running
specialized self-care and burnout-prevention trainings for frontline workers—eight sessions across Beirut, Mount Lebanon, the Bekaa,
the South and the North in May—to address provider stress and capacity [1289][1306].

Child Protection concerns

Violence against women and girls
Protection actors report acute GBV risks for women and girls amid widespread displacement, overcrowded collective sites and limited

girls—through outreach, MHPSS and protection information [1269]. WHO deployed missions and guidance to strengthen PSEAH and

harassment [1279][1278].

UNHCR and partners are providing legal assistance and civil-documentation support to returnees and refugees, with UNHCR reporting
it had reached over 24,300 people with protection support and issued more than 2,000 Emergency Protection Cash Assistance grants
reaching about 9,700 people, including over 700 persons with disabilities, by early May [1362]. A nationwide GBV assessment covering
all governorates included displaced and host community women and girls, persons with disabilities and LGBTIQ individuals; data
collection closed on 8 May and publication was expected by end-May, reflecting efforts to identify and prioritise marginalised groups
in protection planning [1349].
Lebanon's fragmented social protection system and clientelist access patterns — shaped by political affiliation and local networks —
constrain equitable access for minorities and limit longer-term integration, even as initiatives such as the Tripoli Peace Initiative and
cash-plus programmes aim to reach marginalised communities [1376][1387][1405]. Humanitarian leadership has called for more
inclusive, decentralised responses and stronger engagement with municipalities to support safe, dignified returns and better include
affected communities in recovery planning [1352]. Persistent access constraints, infrastructure damage and significant funding
shortfalls in mid-May are limiting partners' ability to sustain services and reach marginalised groups in hard-to-reach and return
areas [1352][1344].
