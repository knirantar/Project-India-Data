MdM network welcomes the European commission communication on humanitarian action as an important political sign that humanitarian aid remains a strategic priority for the European Union but warns against shortcomings and calls for concrete implementation and predictable financing.


The European Commission adopted today its 
Communication on EU Humanitarian Action
, a significant policy framework document intended to guide the EU’s approach to humanitarian engagement over the coming years.


Médecins du Monde – Doctors of the World welcomes the European Commission’s Humanitarian Communication as an important policial signal that humanitarian aid remains a strategic priority for the European Union
 at a time of erosion of respect for international humanitarian law, shrinking funds and humanitarian space, and rising conflicts and fragility.


One of the most substantive contributions of the Communication is its 
clear definitional framing of humanitarian diplomacy
 and its unambiguous emphasis on International Humanitarian Law (IHL) and the protection of humanitarian workers. In a period marked by alarming rates of attacks on aid workers and deliberate obstruction of humanitarian access, this political reaffirmation is most welcomed.


The Communication includes a notably 
strong commitment to Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights (SRHR)
 in humanitarian settings — an area that has been subject to significant political pressure globally, including from the current US administration. The introduction of the 
SHIELD initiative
 (Sexual and Reproductive Health in Emergencies and Life in Dignity) is a concrete and welcome development.


We also welcome the reaffirmation of 
EU engagement in fragile and conflict-affected settings
. Additionally, references to simplified reporting requirements are welcomed. Bureaucratic reporting burdens have been a persistent barrier for NGOs and any steps toward streamlining these processes improve humanitarian effectiveness.


The emphasis on 
pooled funds
 and the conditionality attached to compliance with humanitarian principles is positive though implementation will be key. Country-Based Pooled Funds should be treated as one modality among others, alongside bilateral and multi-year funding, to ensure flexibility and sustainability. As the EU deepens its engagement with pooled mechanisms, it must ensure that governance, partner selection and design processes remain transparent, principled and free from political conditionalities that could compromise humanitarian independence.


However, the question of 
disinformation
 and harmful narratives, which constitutes a growing threat to the humanitarian space, remains underdeveloped. The coverage is fragmented and lacks a coherent framework for how the EU intends to respond to coordinated disinformation campaigns targeting humanitarian actors. Section 3.2 also references disinformation in the domestic EU context, including engagement with EU citizens through communication campaigns. While welcome, the domestic framing should not come at the expense of a more robust treatment of disinformation in field contexts, given the growing weaponisation of disinformation in conflict settings.


Several provisions merit careful scrutiny. The emphasis on 
private sector investment, including artificial intelligence
, as a 
“funding and efficiency driver”
 (p.13) reflects a broader trend in EU policy toward competitiveness, including in the humanitarian space. While certain private sector partnerships can bring added value in specific contexts (e.g., logistics, supply chains), the framing risks normalising a transactional approach to humanitarian action that prioritises cost-efficiency over principled response in sector that are not intended to generate profit by nature. The use of AI in humanitarian operations (e.g., data analysis, needs assessments), raises serious questions regarding 
accountability, data protection, and the concentration of technological power
 in a small number of private actors, therefore the translation of this into programming should be under great scrutiny.


In addition, any EU data-sharing framework among humanitarian partners should require robust safeguards and be built on a strong governance and accountability system, 
meaningful consent, data protection principles, and robust protection against misuse
. In contexts where humanitarian data can be accessed by state security actors or used for population surveillance, the risks of data-sharing are substantial. Finally, the proposed EU-wide certification framework for humanitarian organisations risks creating bureaucratic gatekeeping that disadvantage smaller and local actors.


Political ambition must now be matched with concrete implementation and predictable financing. The Communication’s value depends entirely on whether it is backed by robust and sustained funding.


As an immediate step, the Communication should be backed by 
Council Conclusion
s to ensure Member State ownership and political accountability.


On financing, the negotiations on the next Multiannual Financial Framework, particularly the Global Europe Instrument, will be the true test of the EU’s humanitarian ambition. Médecins du Monde calls for:


A humanitarian envelope of at least 25 billion euros under the next MFF


At least 20% of the GEI dedicated to non-programmable actions, preserving flexibility to respond to sudden-onset crises


The Emergencies cushion to be ring-fenced and protected from reallocation to other policy priorities, including migration management


Multi-year, predictable funding commitments that allow humanitarian organisations to plan and deliver effectively
